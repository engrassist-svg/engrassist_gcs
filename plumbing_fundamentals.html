Overview of Plumbing Systems

Building plumbing systems serve three primary functions: deliver clean water to fixtures, remove wastewater safely, and protect public health. Understanding the fundamental principles behind these systems is essential for proper design, operation, and maintenance.

Three Main Plumbing Systems

  • Water Supply System: Delivers potable water under pressure to fixtures
  • Drainage System: Removes wastewater by gravity flow
  • Vent System: Allows air flow to maintain drainage function

Hydraulic Principles

Water Pressure and Flow

Pressure Basics

  • Pressure (psi): Force per unit area - pushes water through pipes
  • Static Pressure: Pressure when no water is flowing
  • Dynamic Pressure: Pressure when water is flowing (lower due to friction)
  • Pressure Head: Pressure expressed as equivalent height of water (1 psi = 2.31 ft of head)

Elevation Effects

Water pressure changes with elevation:

  • Pressure Loss: 0.433 psi per foot of rise
  • Pressure Gain: 0.433 psi per foot of descent
  • Example: Fixture 30 feet above water source loses 13 psi (30 × 0.433)

Friction Loss

As water flows through pipes, friction between water and pipe walls causes pressure drop:

  • Factors Affecting Friction: Flow rate, pipe diameter, pipe length, pipe roughness
  • Relationship: Doubling flow rate quadruples friction loss
  • Pipe Size: Larger pipes have significantly lower friction loss
  • Fittings: Elbows, tees, and valves add equivalent length

Flow Rate and Velocity

Flow Rate (GPM): Volume of water per minute

Velocity (fps): Speed of water flow

Relationship: GPM = (Velocity × Area) × 449 (conversion factor)

Velocity Limits:

  • Too fast (>8-10 fps): Erosion, noise, water hammer
  • Too slow (<2 fps): Oversized pipes, water quality issues
  • Optimal: 4-8 fps for cold water, 3-5 fps for hot water

Drainage System Theory

Gravity Flow Principles

Drainage systems rely on gravity - water flows downhill. Key concepts:

  • Slope: Minimum 1/4" per foot for pipes under 3"
  • Capacity: Determined by pipe size, slope, and flow characteristics
  • Self-Cleansing: Velocity must be high enough to prevent solids settling
  • No Pressure: Drainage pipes must not run full (need air space)

Fixture Units and Load Calculation

Roy B. Hunter developed the fixture unit system in 1940:

  • DFU (Drainage Fixture Unit): Represents drainage load of fixture
  • Probability: Accounts for fact that not all fixtures drain simultaneously
  • Sizing: Sum DFUs on a pipe, use code tables to find required pipe size
  • Example: Water closet = 4 DFU, lavatory = 1 DFU

Trap Seal Protection

Every fixture must have a trap - a U-shaped pipe section that holds water:

  • Purpose: Water in trap prevents sewer gases from entering building
  • Seal Depth: Typically 2-4 inches of water
  • Threats: Siphonage, evaporation, back pressure, capillary action
  • Protection: Proper venting maintains trap seals

Vent System Purpose

Vent systems are critical but often misunderstood. They serve several vital functions:

Primary Functions of Vents

  1. Trap Seal Protection: Equalizes pressure to prevent siphoning trap seals
  2. Air Circulation: Allows fresh air into drainage system
  3. Gas Removal: Carries sewer gases safely above roof
  4. Drainage Assist: Allows smooth flow by preventing vacuum formation

How Venting Works

When water flows down a drain pipe:

  • Water creates partial vacuum behind it (tendency to suck trap seal out)
  • Vent pipe allows atmospheric pressure air to enter
  • This equalizes pressure and maintains trap seal
  • Without venting: Gurgling, slow draining, seal loss, odors

Vent System Requirements

  • Every Trap: Must be vented (individual, common, or circuit vent)
  • Termination: Extend through roof, minimum 6" above roof surface
  • Location: Vents must be above flood level rim of fixtures
  • Size: Minimum 1-1/4" (IPC) or 1-1/2" (UPC in some jurisdictions)
  • Connection: Must connect to drainage system properly

Plumbing Fixtures

Fixture Categories

Water Closets (Toilets)

Tank Type: Gravity-fed, 1.28-1.6 gpf

Flush Valve: Pressure-assisted, commercial

Trap: Built into fixture

Lavatories (Sinks)

Types: Wall-hung, counter-mounted, pedestal

Flow: 1.2-2.2 gpm at faucet

Trap: 1-1/4" external P-trap

Showers and Tubs

Shower: 1.8-2.5 gpm showerhead

Tub: Fill rate 4-8 gpm

Trap: 1-1/2" or 2" P-trap

Kitchen Sinks

Flow: 1.8-2.2 gpm at faucet

Disposal: 1/3-1 HP garbage disposal

Trap: 1-1/2" P-trap

Fixture Requirements

  • Overflow: Integral overflow to prevent flooding (lavatories, tubs)
  • Air Gap: Faucet spout above flood level rim
  • Materials: Smooth, non-absorbent, cleanable surfaces
  • Access: Accessible for use, cleaning, and maintenance
  • Water Efficiency: Must meet federal and local flow limits

Pipe Materials and Selection

Water Supply Pipe Materials

Material Advantages Disadvantages Best Applications
Copper Durable, long life, antimicrobial, high temp Higher cost, can corrode in acidic water Universal - residential and commercial
PEX Flexible, freeze-resistant, easy install, lower cost Not UV stable, can't use outdoors Residential, radiant heating, retrofits
CPVC Corrosion-proof, lower cost, easy to work Brittle, lower temp rating than copper Residential, budget installations

DWV Pipe Materials

Material Applications Key Features
PVC (Schedule 40) Most common DWV material Lightweight, easy to install, solvent cement joining
ABS DWV systems (not allowed in all areas) Similar to PVC, easier to work in cold weather
Cast Iron Commercial buildings, multi-family Superior sound dampening, fire resistance

Key Takeaways

  • Water supply systems work under pressure; drainage systems work by gravity
  • Every trap must be vented to maintain seal and allow proper drainage
  • Pressure decreases with elevation and friction; velocity must be controlled
  • Fixture units account for probability of simultaneous use
  • Material selection depends on application, budget, and local codes
