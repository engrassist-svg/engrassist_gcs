HVAC Fundamentals

Master the essential principles of heating, ventilation, and air conditioning. From thermodynamics and psychrometrics to comfort conditions and system components, build a solid foundation in HVAC engineering.

Introduction to HVAC

HVAC stands for Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning. These systems provide thermal comfort and acceptable indoor air quality in residential, commercial, and industrial buildings. Understanding HVAC fundamentals is essential for designing efficient, reliable systems that maintain comfortable and healthy indoor environments.

The Three Pillars of HVAC

Heating

Heating systems add thermal energy to indoor spaces to maintain comfortable temperatures during cold weather. Common heating methods include:

  • Forced Air Systems: Furnaces that heat air and distribute it through ductwork
  • Hydronic Systems: Boilers that heat water circulated through radiators or radiant floors
  • Heat Pumps: Devices that transfer heat from outdoor air or ground to indoor spaces
  • Electric Resistance: Electric heaters that convert electricity directly to heat

Ventilation

Ventilation provides fresh outdoor air to dilute contaminants and maintain indoor air quality. Proper ventilation is critical for occupant health and comfort:

  • Natural Ventilation: Uses windows, doors, and vents driven by wind and temperature differences
  • Mechanical Ventilation: Uses fans to control airflow rates and distribution
  • Infiltration: Uncontrolled air leakage through building envelope (generally undesirable)
  • Energy Recovery: Transfers heat and moisture between exhaust and outdoor air to reduce energy costs

Air Conditioning

Air conditioning removes heat and moisture from indoor air to provide cooling and dehumidification during warm weather:

  • Vapor Compression Cycle: Most common method using refrigerant phase changes
  • Chilled Water Systems: Central chillers produce cold water distributed to air handlers
  • Evaporative Cooling: Uses water evaporation to cool air in dry climates
  • Absorption Cooling: Uses heat source to drive cooling cycle (less common)

Key Point

Modern HVAC systems integrate heating, ventilation, and air conditioning into unified systems that can simultaneously control temperature, humidity, air quality, and air distribution throughout a building.

Thermodynamics Basics

Thermodynamics is the study of energy, heat, and work. Understanding basic thermodynamic principles is essential for HVAC design.

Laws of Thermodynamics

First Law: Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another. In HVAC applications:

  • Heat added to a system equals the increase in internal energy plus work done by the system
  • Energy input to equipment must equal energy output plus losses
  • Heat removed from a space must equal heat gains to maintain steady conditions
Q - W = ΔU
Where: Q = heat added, W = work done, ΔU = change in internal energy

Second Law: Entropy and Direction of Heat Flow

Heat naturally flows from hot to cold. To move heat from cold to hot (as in air conditioning), work must be added:

  • No heat engine can be 100% efficient
  • Refrigeration requires energy input to move heat against its natural direction
  • Real processes always generate entropy (energy degradation)

Key Thermodynamic Properties

  • Temperature: Measure of thermal energy level (°F or °C)
  • Pressure: Force per unit area (psi, Pa, in. Hg)
  • Enthalpy (h): Total heat content of a substance (Btu/lb or kJ/kg)
  • Entropy (s): Measure of energy unavailability or disorder
  • Specific Heat: Energy required to raise temperature of unit mass by one degree
Sensible Heat: Q = m × cp × ΔT
Where: m = mass, cp = specific heat, ΔT = temperature change

Example: Heating Air

To heat 1000 CFM of air from 60°F to 100°F:

  • Mass flow rate = 1000 CFM × 0.075 lb/ft³ × 60 min/hr = 4,500 lb/hr
  • Heat required = 4,500 lb/hr × 0.24 Btu/lb·°F × 40°F = 43,200 Btu/hr

Heat Transfer Mechanisms

Heat transfers through three fundamental mechanisms, all of which occur in HVAC systems.

1. Conduction

Heat transfer through solid materials by molecular contact. Important in building envelope heat gain/loss:

Q = k × A × ΔT / d
Where: k = thermal conductivity, A = area, ΔT = temperature difference, d = thickness
  • U-factor (U): Overall heat transfer coefficient (Btu/hr·ft²·°F)
  • R-value (R): Thermal resistance (hr·ft²·°F/Btu), R = 1/U
  • Higher R-value = better insulation

2. Convection

Heat transfer by fluid movement (air or water). Critical for heating/cooling coils and room air distribution:

Q = h × A × ΔT
Where: h = convection coefficient, A = surface area, ΔT = temperature difference
  • Natural Convection: Driven by buoyancy (hot air rises)
  • Forced Convection: Driven by fans or pumps (more effective)
  • Air velocity significantly affects heat transfer rates

3. Radiation

Heat transfer by electromagnetic waves, requiring no medium. Major source of solar heat gain:

Q = ε × σ × A × (T₁⁴ - T₂⁴)
Where: ε = emissivity, σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant, A = area, T = absolute temperature
  • Solar Radiation: Primary heat gain through windows
  • Thermal Radiation: Exchange between surfaces at different temperatures
  • Radiant Heating/Cooling: Uses radiation as primary heat transfer mode

Application in HVAC

All three mechanisms occur simultaneously in buildings. For example, a window experiences:

  • Conduction through the glass
  • Convection at indoor and outdoor surfaces
  • Radiation from solar gain and thermal exchange

Psychrometrics

Psychrometrics is the study of thermodynamic properties of moist air. It's essential for analyzing HVAC processes and calculating loads.

Properties of Moist Air

  • Dry-Bulb Temperature (DB): Actual air temperature measured with standard thermometer (°F)
  • Wet-Bulb Temperature (WB): Temperature measured with moistened thermometer (accounts for evaporative cooling)
  • Dew Point Temperature (DP): Temperature at which moisture begins to condense
  • Relative Humidity (RH): Ratio of actual moisture to maximum possible at given temperature (%)
  • Humidity Ratio (W): Mass of water vapor per mass of dry air (lb water/lb dry air)
  • Enthalpy (h): Total heat content of moist air (Btu/lb dry air)
  • Specific Volume (v): Volume per unit mass of dry air (ft³/lb)

The Psychrometric Chart

A graphical tool that shows relationships between air properties. The chart allows you to:

  • Determine all air properties when two properties are known
  • Visualize HVAC processes (heating, cooling, humidification, dehumidification)
  • Calculate required capacity for heating and cooling equipment

Common HVAC Processes on Psychrometric Chart

Sensible Heating

Adding heat without changing moisture content:

  • Dry-bulb temperature increases (move right)
  • Humidity ratio remains constant (horizontal line)
  • Relative humidity decreases
  • Example: Furnace heating air in winter

Sensible Cooling

Removing heat without changing moisture content:

  • Dry-bulb temperature decreases (move left)
  • Humidity ratio remains constant (horizontal line)
  • Relative humidity increases
  • Example: Sensible cooling with chilled water coil above dew point

Cooling and Dehumidification

Removing both heat and moisture (most common AC process):

  • Air is cooled below its dew point
  • Moisture condenses on cold coil surfaces
  • Both temperature and humidity ratio decrease (move down and left)
  • Example: Standard air conditioning in summer

Humidification

Adding moisture to air:

  • Humidity ratio increases
  • With steam: temperature increases (move up and right)
  • With evaporative: temperature decreases (move up and left along constant enthalpy)
  • Example: Winter humidification for comfort
Sensible Cooling/Heating: Qs = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT
Latent Cooling/Heating: Ql = 0.68 × CFM × ΔW
Total Cooling/Heating: Qt = 4.5 × CFM × Δh

Where: CFM = airflow, ΔT = temperature change, ΔW = humidity ratio change, Δh = enthalpy change

Human Comfort Conditions

The primary goal of HVAC systems is to provide thermal comfort for occupants. Comfort is subjective but can be quantified based on several factors.

Factors Affecting Thermal Comfort

Environmental Factors (Controllable by HVAC)

  • Air Temperature: Most obvious factor, typically 68-76°F for comfort
  • Radiant Temperature: Temperature of surrounding surfaces affects comfort
  • Air Velocity: Air movement affects convective heat transfer and evaporation
  • Relative Humidity: Affects evaporative cooling from skin

Personal Factors (Not Controllable by HVAC)

  • Clothing Insulation: Amount and type of clothing worn
  • Metabolic Rate: Activity level and heat generation
  • Individual Differences: Age, health, acclimatization

ASHRAE Comfort Standards

ASHRAE Standard 55 defines acceptable thermal environmental conditions for human occupancy.

Season Temperature Range Humidity Range Air Velocity
Winter (Heating) 68-76°F 30-60% RH < 30 FPM
Summer (Cooling) 73-79°F 30-60% RH < 50 FPM

Design Implications

  • Design indoor conditions typically: 70-75°F, 30-50% RH
  • Cannot satisfy all occupants simultaneously (design for 80% acceptability)
  • Provide individual control where possible (thermostats, diffusers)
  • Consider both temperature and humidity for true comfort

Indoor Air Quality (IAQ)

Beyond thermal comfort, HVAC systems must provide acceptable air quality:

  • Ventilation: ASHRAE 62.1 specifies minimum outdoor air rates (typically 15-20 CFM/person)
  • Filtration: Remove particulates (MERV ratings 8-13 common)
  • Contaminant Control: Dilute or remove pollutants, odors, and VOCs
  • Humidity Control: Prevent mold growth (keep RH below 60%) and static electricity (keep above 30%)

Heating and Cooling Loads

HVAC systems must be sized to handle the heating and cooling loads imposed on a building. Understanding load types is crucial for proper system design.

Cooling Load Components

External Loads (From Outside)

  • Solar Gain Through Windows: Direct and diffuse solar radiation through glass
    • Varies by window orientation (south, west highest)
    • Affected by shading devices and glass properties (SHGC)
    • Time-dependent (peak afternoon for west windows)
  • Conduction Through Envelope: Heat flow through walls, roof, windows, doors
    • Q = U × A × ΔT
    • Depends on insulation (U-value) and temperature difference
  • Infiltration: Unconditioned outdoor air leaking into building
    • Brings in both sensible and latent load
    • Depends on building tightness and wind pressure
  • Ventilation: Required outdoor air for occupants
    • Per ASHRAE 62.1 requirements
    • Can be significant portion of total load in commercial buildings

Internal Loads (Generated Inside)

  • Occupants: People generate both sensible and latent heat
    • Sensible: ~250 Btu/hr per person (radiated/convected heat)
    • Latent: ~200 Btu/hr per person (moisture from breathing and perspiration)
    • Varies with activity level
  • Lighting: Heat from light fixtures
    • 3.41 Btu/hr per watt of lighting power
    • LED lighting significantly reduces this load compared to older technologies
  • Equipment: Computers, appliances, machinery
    • Varies widely by building type
    • Data centers have very high equipment loads
  • Process Loads: Cooking, manufacturing, special equipment

Heating Load Components

Heating loads are generally simpler than cooling loads:

  • Conduction Losses: Heat loss through building envelope (Q = U × A × ΔT)
  • Infiltration: Cold outdoor air entering building
  • Ventilation: Heating outdoor air to room temperature

Important Differences

  • Heating loads do NOT include internal gains (occupants, lights, equipment help with heating)
  • Cooling loads DO include internal gains (they add to cooling requirements)
  • Solar gain is a major cooling load but helps reduce heating loads
Simplified Load Equations:

Envelope Conduction: Q = U × A × ΔT
Infiltration/Ventilation Sensible: Qs = 1.08 × CFM × ΔT
Infiltration/Ventilation Latent: Ql = 0.68 × CFM × ΔW
People Sensible: Q = Number × 250 Btu/hr
People Latent: Q = Number × 200 Btu/hr
Lighting: Q = Watts × 3.41 Btu/hr/W

Refrigeration Cycle

Air conditioning and heat pumps work on the vapor-compression refrigeration cycle. Understanding this cycle is fundamental to HVAC engineering.

The Four Basic Components

1. Compressor

  • Compresses low-pressure refrigerant vapor to high pressure and temperature
  • Adds energy to the system (requires electrical power)
  • Types: Reciprocating, scroll, screw, centrifugal

2. Condenser

  • Rejects heat to outdoor air or cooling water
  • Hot, high-pressure vapor condenses to liquid
  • Types: Air-cooled (with fans), water-cooled, evaporative

3. Expansion Device

  • Reduces pressure of liquid refrigerant
  • Causes refrigerant to partially vaporize (flash gas)
  • Types: Thermostatic expansion valve (TXV), electronic expansion valve (EEV), capillary tube

4. Evaporator

  • Absorbs heat from indoor air or chilled water
  • Low-pressure liquid refrigerant evaporates to vapor
  • Types: Direct expansion (DX) coils, flooded evaporators

How the Cycle Works

  1. Evaporation: Low-pressure liquid refrigerant in evaporator absorbs heat from indoor air and evaporates to vapor (this cools the air)
  2. Compression: Compressor draws in low-pressure vapor and compresses it to high pressure and high temperature
  3. Condensation: Hot, high-pressure vapor flows to condenser where heat is rejected to outdoors, causing vapor to condense to liquid
  4. Expansion: High-pressure liquid passes through expansion device, reducing pressure and temperature, returning to evaporator to repeat cycle

Key Concepts

  • Phase Change: Refrigerant alternates between liquid and vapor states, absorbing heat during evaporation and rejecting heat during condensation
  • Energy Input: Compressor adds energy to "pump" heat from cool to warm
  • COP (Coefficient of Performance): Cooling output / Energy input (typically 2.5-4.0 for air conditioning)
  • Heat Pumps: Same cycle operated in reverse for heating (indoor and outdoor coils switch roles)
COP = Cooling Capacity (Btu/hr) / Compressor Power (Watts × 3.41)

EER (Energy Efficiency Ratio) = Cooling Capacity (Btu/hr) / Power Input (Watts)

SEER (Seasonal EER) = Seasonal average efficiency

Common Refrigerants

  • R-410A: Current standard for residential and light commercial AC
  • R-134a: Used in automotive and some chillers
  • R-32: Newer, lower GWP alternative
  • R-454B, R-32: Next-generation low-GWP refrigerants

HVAC System Components

Modern HVAC systems consist of many components working together to provide comfort.

Air-Side Components

Air Handling Units (AHU)

Central units that condition and circulate air:

  • Supply fan: Moves conditioned air through ductwork
  • Heating coil: Hot water, steam, or electric resistance
  • Cooling coil: Chilled water or direct expansion refrigerant
  • Filters: Remove particulates from air
  • Outdoor air dampers: Control ventilation air intake
  • Mixing section: Blends return and outdoor air

Terminal Units

  • VAV Boxes: Variable air volume boxes that modulate airflow to zones
  • Fan Coil Units: Local units with coil and fan serving individual zones
  • Unit Ventilators: Similar to fan coils with outdoor air connection
  • Diffusers and Grilles: Distribute air within spaces

Water-Side Components

Boilers

Generate hot water or steam for heating:

  • Fire-tube or water-tube designs
  • Fuel: Natural gas, oil, electric
  • Efficiency: 80-99% depending on type (condensing boilers highest)
Boiler Sizing Calculator

Chillers

Produce chilled water for cooling:

  • Air-Cooled: Reject heat to outdoor air (simpler, less efficient)
  • Water-Cooled: Reject heat to cooling tower (more efficient, more complex)
  • Compressor types: Scroll, screw, centrifugal
  • Typical efficiency: 0.4-0.8 kW/ton
Chiller Sizing Calculator

Pumps

Circulate water through hydronic systems:

  • Centrifugal pumps most common in HVAC
  • Must overcome friction losses in pipes, coils, and fittings
  • Variable speed pumps save energy at part load
Pump Selection Tool

Cooling Towers

Reject heat from water-cooled chillers:

  • Use evaporative cooling for high efficiency
  • Types: Open-circuit, closed-circuit, hybrid
  • Require water treatment and maintenance

Packaged Equipment

  • Rooftop Units (RTU): All-in-one heating and cooling units, common in commercial buildings
  • Split Systems: Outdoor condensing unit paired with indoor air handler or furnace
  • VRF Systems: Variable refrigerant flow with multiple indoor units served by one outdoor unit
  • Heat Pumps: Provide both heating and cooling by reversing refrigeration cycle

Air Distribution Fundamentals

Effective air distribution ensures uniform conditions throughout occupied spaces.

Ductwork Design

Duct Sizing Methods

  • Equal Friction: Maintain constant pressure drop per foot of duct
  • Static Regain: Convert velocity pressure to static pressure at fittings
  • Velocity Method: Limit maximum velocities for noise control
Duct Sizing Calculator

Design Considerations

  • Velocity Limits:
    • Residences: 600-900 FPM branches, 700-1200 FPM mains
    • Commercial: 1000-2000 FPM depending on noise tolerance
    • Main ducts near fan: Up to 2500 FPM acceptable
  • Pressure Drop: Typically 0.1 in. w.g. per 100 ft for low-pressure systems
  • Aspect Ratio: Keep rectangular ducts within 4:1 ratio for efficiency

Air Distribution Patterns

Mixing Ventilation

Most common approach - mix supply air with room air:

  • Supply air from ceiling diffusers
  • Creates well-mixed conditions throughout room
  • Supply air temperature typically 15-25°F below room temperature
  • Airflow patterns determined by diffuser type and throw

Displacement Ventilation

Supply cool air at floor level, warm air rises and exhausts at ceiling:

  • Better air quality at occupied zone
  • More energy efficient (higher supply air temperatures)
  • Requires higher ceiling heights
  • Not suitable for all applications

Diffuser Selection

  • Throw: Distance air travels before velocity drops to 50 FPM (should reach 75% across room)
  • Spread: Width of air pattern
  • NC (Noise Criteria): Select diffusers with acceptable noise levels (NC 25-35 for offices)
  • Types: Ceiling diffusers (square, round), linear diffusers, slot diffusers, displacement diffusers

Control System Fundamentals

Modern HVAC systems use sophisticated controls to maintain comfort while minimizing energy use.

Control Modes

On-Off Control

Simplest control mode:

  • Equipment fully on or fully off
  • Results in temperature swings around setpoint
  • Used for residential systems and small equipment
  • Deadband prevents short cycling

Modulating Control

Varies equipment output to match load:

  • Proportional (P): Output proportional to error
  • Proportional-Integral (PI): Adds integral action to eliminate offset
  • PID: Adds derivative for faster response (less common in HVAC)
  • Provides tighter temperature control and better efficiency

Common Control Strategies

Zone Temperature Control

  • Thermostat measures zone temperature
  • Modulates VAV box damper or coil valve to maintain setpoint
  • Deadband between heating and cooling prevents simultaneous operation

Supply Air Temperature Reset

  • Vary supply air temperature based on load
  • Warmest supply air temperature that satisfies most demanding zone
  • Reduces reheat energy and increases system efficiency

Static Pressure Reset

  • Reduce duct static pressure when zones don't need full airflow
  • Saves significant fan energy at part load
  • Based on most-open VAV box position

Economizer Control

  • Use cool outdoor air for "free cooling" when available
  • Compare outdoor and return air conditions
  • Can provide up to 100% outdoor air when beneficial
  • Significant energy savings in many climates

Demand Controlled Ventilation

  • Vary ventilation air based on actual occupancy
  • Uses CO₂ sensors to estimate occupancy
  • Reduces energy waste when spaces are unoccupied or partially occupied

Energy Management Sequences

  • Optimal Start/Stop: Start equipment at optimal time to reach setpoint when occupants arrive
  • Night Setback/Setup: Allow temperatures to drift during unoccupied periods
  • Demand Limiting: Shed loads during peak demand periods to reduce utility costs
  • Load Sequencing: Stage equipment on/off to match load and maximize efficiency

Control System Benefits

  • Maintain comfort conditions throughout occupied spaces
  • Reduce energy consumption by 20-40% compared to basic controls
  • Provide remote monitoring and diagnostics
  • Enable data collection for commissioning and optimization

Conclusion

Understanding these HVAC fundamentals provides the foundation for designing, analyzing, and troubleshooting building mechanical systems. From the basic principles of thermodynamics and heat transfer to the operation of refrigeration cycles and control strategies, these concepts are essential for mechanical engineering professionals.

Key Takeaways

  • HVAC systems provide thermal comfort (temperature + humidity) and acceptable indoor air quality
  • Heat transfer occurs through conduction, convection, and radiation - all three are important in buildings
  • Psychrometrics describes properties of moist air and allows analysis of HVAC processes
  • Comfort depends on air temperature, radiant temperature, humidity, and air velocity
  • Loads include envelope losses/gains, ventilation, infiltration, occupants, lights, and equipment
  • Refrigeration cycle uses phase change to move heat from cool to warm spaces
  • Modern systems use modulating controls and energy management strategies for efficiency
