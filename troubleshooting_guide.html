HVAC Troubleshooting Guide

Systematic diagnostic procedures and proven solutions for identifying and resolving common mechanical system problems in commercial HVAC applications.

Introduction to HVAC Troubleshooting

Effective troubleshooting requires a systematic approach that combines technical knowledge, diagnostic tools, and logical problem-solving. This guide provides structured procedures for diagnosing and resolving common issues in commercial HVAC systems.

Always follow these fundamental principles:

  • Safety first - lockout/tagout procedures, PPE, and proper training
  • Gather information - review system documentation, talk to operators, check history
  • Verify the problem - confirm symptoms before making changes
  • Use a systematic approach - eliminate possibilities methodically
  • Document findings - record all observations and actions taken

General Troubleshooting Process

Step 1: Define the Problem

  • What is the specific symptom or complaint?
  • When did the problem start?
  • Has anything changed recently (setpoints, equipment, weather)?
  • Is the problem continuous or intermittent?
  • Which zones or systems are affected?

Step 2: Gather Data

  • Review BMS/BAS trending data and alarms
  • Check equipment status indicators and gauges
  • Measure temperatures, pressures, flows, and electrical parameters
  • Review maintenance records and equipment logs
  • Obtain design documents and sequence of operations

Step 3: Analyze and Diagnose

  • Compare actual values to design values
  • Identify which systems/components are functioning correctly
  • Isolate the problem to a specific subsystem or component
  • Develop hypotheses for root cause
  • Test each hypothesis systematically

Step 4: Implement Solution

  • Develop corrective action plan
  • Obtain necessary parts and tools
  • Make repairs or adjustments
  • Verify the fix resolves the original problem
  • Monitor system performance after repair

Step 5: Document and Follow Up

  • Record problem description, diagnosis, and solution
  • Update maintenance records
  • Notify affected parties of resolution
  • Monitor for recurrence
  • Consider preventive measures to avoid future issues

Chiller Troubleshooting

Common Chiller Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
Low Leaving Water Temperature Fouled tubes, low flow, low load, refrigerant undercharge Check approach temperature, measure flow rate, inspect tubes, verify refrigerant charge Clean tubes, repair flow issues, adjust load, recharge refrigerant
High Discharge Pressure Fouled condenser, high condenser water temp, non-condensables, refrigerant overcharge Check condenser approach, inspect tubes, check cooling tower, purge air Clean condenser, repair cooling tower, purge system, recover excess refrigerant
Low Suction Pressure Low load, refrigerant undercharge, expansion valve issue, evaporator fouling Check building load, verify charge, test TXV/EXV, inspect evaporator Recharge system, replace valve, clean evaporator
High Amp Draw High head pressure, mechanical issues, electrical problems Check all pressures, listen for abnormal sounds, verify voltage/phase balance Address pressure issues, repair mechanical problems, fix electrical supply
Frequent Starts/Stops Oversized unit, low load, control issues, flow switch issues Review load calculations, check minimum run timers, verify flow rates Adjust controls, add thermal storage, repair flow issues
Oil Level Issues Oil migration, oil foaming, refrigerant in oil, oil return problems Check sight glass, measure oil temperature, verify oil separator operation Add oil, repair oil return system, reduce refrigerant migration

Critical Chiller Measurements

  • Evaporator Approach: Leaving water temp - refrigerant evaporating temp (typically 2-4°F)
  • Condenser Approach: Refrigerant condensing temp - entering water temp (typically 2-6°F)
  • Superheat: Suction temp - evaporating temp (typically 8-12°F)
  • Subcooling: Condensing temp - liquid line temp (typically 5-10°F)
  • Delta T: Entering - leaving water temp (typically 10-12°F design)

Common Boiler Problems

Combustion Analysis Parameters

Target Values for Natural Gas Boilers:

  • Oxygen (O₂): 4-6% for optimal efficiency
  • Carbon Dioxide (CO₂): 9-10% (inversely related to O₂)
  • Carbon Monoxide (CO): <100 ppm (400 ppm max)
  • Stack Temperature: 300-450°F for non-condensing, 100-140°F for condensing
  • Excess Air: 20-40% for atmospheric burners, 10-20% for power burners
  • Combustion Efficiency: 78-82% for non-condensing, 88-95% for condensing

Air Handler Troubleshooting

Common Air Handler Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
Low Airflow Dirty filter, closed dampers, belt slipping, fan issue, duct obstruction Check filter ΔP, inspect dampers, verify fan speed, measure static pressures Replace filter, open dampers, adjust belt, repair fan, clear obstruction
High Static Pressure Dirty coil, dirty filter, closed dampers, undersized ductwork Measure pressure drops across components, inspect coils and filters Clean coils, replace filter, open dampers, modify ductwork
Inadequate Heating/Cooling Low airflow, water flow issues, valve problems, coil fouling, control issues Measure airflow, check water temps and flow, verify valve operation Fix airflow issues, repair water flow, replace valve, clean coils
Water Dripping from Unit Clogged drain, improper slope, drain trap issues, excessive condensate Check drain line, verify trap depth, inspect drain pan, check coil entering temp Clear drain, adjust slope, repair trap, reduce coil load
Excessive Vibration/Noise Unbalanced fan, loose components, bad bearings, resonance, loose belt Inspect fan wheel, check all fasteners, listen to bearings, check belt tension Balance fan, tighten components, replace bearings, adjust speed, tension belt
Frozen Coil Low airflow, low load, low water temp, faulty valve, control issue Measure airflow, check entering water temp, verify valve operation, check control sequence Fix airflow, adjust water temp, repair valve, reprogram controls

Air Handler Diagnostic Measurements

  • Total Static Pressure: Supply + return static (fan discharge - fan suction)
  • Filter ΔP: Clean: 0.1-0.2" w.g., Replace at: 0.5-1.0" w.g.
  • Coil ΔP: Typical: 0.5-1.5" w.g. air-side, 5-15 psi water-side
  • Fan Speed: Measure with tachometer, compare to design RPM
  • Motor Amps: Should be ≤ nameplate FLA, typically 80-90% at design
  • Supply Air Temperature: Cooling: 55-58°F, Heating: 95-120°F

Cavitation Insufficient NPSH, clogged strainer, high suction lift, closed suction valve Calculate NPSH available vs. required, check suction pressure, inspect strainer Raise water level, clean strainer, open valves, relocate pump lower

Pump Performance Verification

To verify pump is operating on its curve:

  1. Measure discharge pressure (psig)
  2. Measure suction pressure (psig)
  3. Calculate head: H (ft) = (P_discharge - P_suction) × 2.31 / SG
  4. Measure flow rate (GPM) using flow meter or balance valve
  5. Plot point on pump curve - should fall on or near the curve
  6. If operating off curve: check impeller diameter, verify speed, look for recirculation

Cooling Tower Troubleshooting

Common Cooling Tower Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
High Leaving Water Temperature Fouled fill, low airflow, high load, inadequate water flow, poor distribution Check approach to wet-bulb, inspect fill, verify fan operation, check distribution Clean fill, repair fan, increase water flow, fix distribution nozzles
Excessive Water Loss Drift eliminators damaged, excessive blowdown, high winds, splashing Inspect drift eliminators, check blowdown rate, measure cycles of concentration Repair/replace eliminators, adjust blowdown, add wind screens
Scale/Fouling Poor water treatment, high TDS, inadequate blowdown, biological growth Test water chemistry, check cycles of concentration, inspect fill Improve water treatment, increase blowdown, clean fill, add biocide
Fan Motor Failure Moisture intrusion, overload, bearing failure, electrical issues Check amp draw, inspect for water intrusion, test motor windings Replace motor, improve weather protection, repair electrical supply
Legionella Concerns Inadequate biocide, low circulation, dead legs, temperature issues Test for Legionella, review water treatment program, inspect system Hyperchlorinate, improve treatment program, eliminate dead legs

Cooling Tower Performance Metrics

  • Approach: Leaving water temp - wet-bulb temp (typically 7-10°F)
  • Range: Entering water temp - leaving water temp (typically 10-15°F)
  • Effectiveness: Range / (Entering temp - wet-bulb) × 100%
  • Cycles of Concentration: Makeup conductivity / blowdown conductivity (target 3-6)
  • Blowdown Rate: Evaporation rate / (Cycles - 1)

Control System Troubleshooting

Temperature Control Issues

Symptom Possible Causes Solution
Space too hot/cold Sensor calibration, wrong setpoint, valve/damper stuck, control logic error Calibrate sensor, verify setpoint, check actuator, review sequence
Temperature swings PID tuning, slow response, sensor location, simultaneous heating/cooling Tune PID, adjust deadband, relocate sensor, add lockouts
Control hunting Improper PID tuning, loose linkage, too small deadband Retune PID, tighten linkage, increase deadband

Sensor Troubleshooting

  • Verify Reading: Compare to calibrated reference instrument
  • Check Wiring: Verify connections, look for shorts/opens, test resistance
  • Inspect Location: Ensure proper placement, avoid drafts/radiation/vibration
  • Test Signal: Measure voltage/current output, verify input to controller
  • Calibrate: Follow manufacturer procedure for offset and span adjustment

Actuator Troubleshooting

  • Manual Override: Test if valve/damper can be moved manually
  • Power Check: Verify correct voltage at actuator
  • Signal Verification: Measure control signal (0-10V, 4-20mA, etc.)
  • Stroke Test: Command full open and full closed, verify travel
  • Spring Return: On loss of power, verify fail-safe position
  • Linkage: Check for binding, proper attachment, correct rotation

PID Control Tuning Basics

Start with these typical values and adjust:

  • Proportional Band: Start at 10-20% for temperature, 20-40% for pressure
  • Integral Time: Start at 5-10 minutes, increase if hunting, decrease if slow response
  • Derivative Time: Often not needed in HVAC, use sparingly (0.5-2 min if needed)
  • Tuning Method: Make small adjustments, allow system to stabilize between changes
  • Signs of Poor Tuning: Oscillation (reduce P or I), slow response (increase P or reduce I), overshoot (reduce P, increase D)

Essential Diagnostic Tools

Electrical Measurements

  • Multimeter (voltage, current, resistance)
  • Clamp meter (current without breaking circuit)
  • Phase rotation meter
  • Megohmmeter (insulation testing)
  • Power quality analyzer

Pressure/Temperature

  • Pressure gauges (various ranges)
  • Manometer (low pressure, draft)
  • Thermometers (contact and IR)
  • Psychrometer (humidity)
  • Data logger (trending)

Flow Measurements

  • Pitot tube and manometer (air)
  • Hot wire anemometer (air velocity)
  • Ultrasonic flow meter (water)
  • Balance valve with gauge ports
  • Venturi/orifice calculator

Specialized Tools

  • Combustion analyzer
  • Refrigerant gauges and scales
  • Vibration analyzer
  • Ultrasonic leak detector
  • Thermal imaging camera

Safety Considerations

DANGER - Safety Requirements

Always follow these safety procedures when troubleshooting HVAC equipment:

Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)

  • Identify all energy sources (electrical, mechanical, thermal, pressure)
  • Notify affected personnel of lockout
  • Shut down equipment using normal procedures
  • Isolate all energy sources
  • Apply locks and tags to all isolation points
  • Release stored energy (capacitors, springs, pressure, elevated components)
  • Verify zero energy state before beginning work

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

  • Always Required: Safety glasses, work boots
  • Electrical Work: Arc flash rated clothing, insulated gloves, face shield
  • Refrigerant Work: Safety goggles, gloves, ventilation
  • Confined Spaces: Harness, respirator, gas monitor, retrieval system
  • Chemical Handling: Chemical resistant gloves, goggles, apron

Specific Hazards

  • Electrical: Assume all circuits are live until verified, maintain approach distances, use proper tools
  • High Pressure: Relieve pressure before opening systems, use pressure relief valves
  • Hot Surfaces: Allow equipment to cool, use heat-resistant gloves, post warnings
  • Rotating Equipment: Ensure lockout, keep loose clothing away, use guards
  • Refrigerants: Work in ventilated areas, avoid skin contact, proper recovery procedures
  • Chemicals: Read SDS sheets, use proper PPE, ensure ventilation, have eyewash/shower available
  • Heights: Use proper ladders/scaffolds, wear fall protection above 6 feet

Preventive Maintenance Best Practices

Most troubleshooting can be avoided through proper preventive maintenance:

Monthly Tasks

  • Inspect and change filters as needed
  • Check belt condition and tension
  • Verify control operation and setpoints
  • Check for unusual noise, vibration, or leaks
  • Review BAS alarms and trending
  • Test drain pans and condensate drains

Quarterly Tasks

  • Lubricate motors and bearings per manufacturer schedule
  • Test safety controls and interlocks
  • Inspect coils for cleanliness
  • Check refrigerant charge and pressures
  • Test water treatment and adjust chemicals
  • Verify economizer operation (if seasonal)

Annual Tasks

  • Clean coils (evaporator, condenser, heating)
  • Perform combustion analysis on boilers
  • Megger test motors
  • Vibration analysis on rotating equipment
  • Calibrate sensors and control devices
  • Inspect and clean cooling tower
  • Test and exercise all valves
  • Perform infrared scan of electrical connections
  • Full system commissioning check

Documentation is Critical

Maintain detailed records of all maintenance activities:

  • Date, technician, and equipment identification
  • All measurements and observations
  • Parts replaced and adjustments made
  • Any issues found and corrective actions taken
  • Recommendations for future work
  • Photo documentation of conditions

These records are invaluable for identifying trends, planning budgets, and troubleshooting recurring issues.
