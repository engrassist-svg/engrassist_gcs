Design Process Overview

Plumbing system design is a systematic process that requires consideration of building codes, hydraulic principles, energy efficiency, and constructability. This guide outlines the step-by-step approach used by professional engineers.

Design Phases

  1. Schematic Design: Identify requirements, preliminary layouts, system concepts
  2. Design Development: System sizing, equipment selection, routing development
  3. Construction Documents: Detailed drawings, specifications, calculations
  4. Bidding/Negotiation: Support contractor selection
  5. Construction Administration: Review submittals, site observations, respond to RFIs

Preliminary Design Phase

Step 1: Gather Project Information

  • Building Type & Occupancy: Residential, commercial, industrial, institutional
  • Building Size: Square footage, number of floors, occupant load
  • Architectural Plans: Floor plans, sections, fixture locations
  • Local Codes: Adopted plumbing code, amendments, utility requirements
  • Site Conditions: Water service availability, sewer connection, gas service
  • Special Requirements: Healthcare, food service, laboratories

Step 2: Develop Fixture Schedule

Create detailed fixture schedule listing all plumbing fixtures:

  • Fixture type and quantity
  • Location (floor, room)
  • Water supply fixture units (WSFU) - hot and cold
  • Drainage fixture units (DFU)
  • Trap size and vent requirements
  • Special features (ADA, low-flow, etc.)

Step 3: Determine Code Requirements

  • Minimum Fixtures: Verify minimum fixture count for occupancy
  • Accessibility: ADA/accessibility requirements for public fixtures
  • Water Conservation: Maximum flow rates, flush volumes
  • Backflow Prevention: Required devices and locations
  • Materials: Approved pipe materials for application

Water Supply System Design

Design Steps

1. Calculate Water Demand

Fixture Unit Method:

  • Sum fixture units for hot and cold water separately
  • Convert to GPM using Hunter's Curve or code tables
  • Account for diversity and simultaneous use factors
  • Add special loads (cooling towers, process equipment)

2. Size Water Service

  • Determine total building demand (GPM)
  • Calculate meter size based on flow and pressure loss
  • Size building service pipe from meter to building
  • Account for elevation changes and developed length
  • Coordinate with water utility for service size

3. Design Distribution System

System Layout Options:

  • Trunk and Branch: Main trunk lines with branches to fixtures (traditional)
  • Home Run (Manifold): Central manifold with individual lines to each fixture
  • Loop System: Continuous loop with branches (commercial buildings)

4. Size Piping

Starting at most remote fixture, work back to source:

  • Sum fixture units served by each pipe segment
  • Convert to GPM for the segment
  • Select pipe size to maintain velocity limits (5-8 fps)
  • Verify adequate pressure at all fixtures (min 15-20 psi)
  • Calculate pressure loss through pipe, fittings, and devices

5. Water Heater Sizing

  • Determine peak hot water demand (GPM or GPH)
  • Calculate storage tank capacity or recovery rate
  • Size for first-hour rating exceeding peak demand
  • Consider energy source (gas, electric, heat pump)
  • Specify temperature settings and mixing valves

6. Auxiliary Equipment

  • Pressure-Reducing Valves: If supply pressure >80 psi
  • Expansion Tanks: Thermal expansion protection
  • Backflow Preventers: At service entrance and cross-connections
  • Water Hammer Arrestors: At quick-closing valves
  • Recirculation Pumps: For long hot water runs
  • Booster Pumps: If inadequate supply pressure

Drainage System Design

Design Steps

1. Establish Drainage Layout

  • Group fixtures on common vertical stacks where possible
  • Identify building drain and sewer routing
  • Determine invert elevations and slopes
  • Minimize horizontal offsets in stacks
  • Plan for cleanout locations per code

2. Size Drainage Piping

Working from fixtures to building sewer:

  • Size fixture drains based on DFU and trap size
  • Size branch drains: sum DFUs, apply to code tables
  • Size stacks (soil and waste): total DFUs on stack
  • Size building drain: sum all DFUs, account for slope

  • Grease Interceptors: Required for commercial kitchens
  • Oil Separators: Parking garages, vehicle service areas
  • Floor Drains: Mechanical rooms, commercial kitchens, restrooms
  • Indirect Waste: Equipment with potential contamination
  • Sumps and Ejectors: Below-grade fixtures

Vent System Design

Vent Types and Applications

Individual Vent

Separate vent for each trap

Use: Optimal trap protection

Cost: Highest material and labor

Common Vent

Shared vent for 2 fixtures back-to-back

Use: Efficient for paired fixtures

Limits: Specific fixture and piping requirements

Wet Vent

Pipe serves as both drain and vent

Use: Bathrooms, material savings

Rules: Strict code limitations on length and fixtures

Circuit Vent

Vents multiple fixtures on horizontal branch

Use: Commercial restrooms

Req'd: Minimum 8 fixtures on branch

Vent Sizing Guidelines

  • Size based on DFUs drained and developed length
  • Minimum vent size: 1-1/4" (IPC) or 1-1/2" (UPC, some jurisdictions)
  • Vent must be sized equal to or larger than trap size for individual vents
  • Vent stacks sized per code tables based on DFU load
  • Vent termination: Extend through roof, minimum 6" above roof surface
  • Keep vents above flood level rim of fixtures

Storm Drainage Design

Design Process

  1. Determine Rainfall Intensity: Use local 100-year, 1-hour storm data
  2. Calculate Roof Areas: Horizontal projection of roof areas
  3. Size Roof Drains: Based on area served and rainfall rate
  4. Size Storm Piping: Code tables or hydraulic calculations
  5. Secondary (Overflow) Drainage: Required at higher elevation
  6. Discharge: To storm sewer, dry well, or surface drainage

Important: Separate Storm and Sanitary

Storm drainage must be completely separate from sanitary drainage systems. Combined systems are prohibited by modern codes except in legacy systems.

Gas Piping Design

Design Steps

  1. Calculate Gas Load: Sum BTU/hr for all appliances
  2. Determine Gas Type: Natural gas or LP (affects sizing)
  3. Establish Pressure: Low pressure (<2 psi) or elevated
  4. Size Piping: Longest run method or branch length method
  5. Pressure Drop: Limit to 0.5" w.c. for low-pressure systems
  6. Materials: Black steel (most common), CSST, copper (if approved)
  7. Testing: Pressure test all gas piping before use

Documentation and Drawings

Required Drawings

Plumbing Plans

  • Floor Plans: Show fixture locations, pipe routing, equipment
  • Riser Diagrams: Vertical pipe routing and connections
  • Details: Connections, equipment installation, special conditions
  • Schedules: Fixtures, equipment, valves
  • Notes: General notes, code references, standards

Calculations and Documentation

  • Water Supply Calculations: Fixture units, GPM, pipe sizing, pressure loss
  • Drainage Calculations: DFUs, pipe sizing, slope verification
  • Storm Drainage: Rainfall intensity, roof areas, drain and pipe sizing
  • Gas Piping: BTU loads, pipe sizing, pressure drop
  • Equipment: Water heater sizing, pump curves, specialty equipment
