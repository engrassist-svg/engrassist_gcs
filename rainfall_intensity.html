Understanding Rainfall Intensity

What is Rainfall Intensity?

Rainfall intensity is the rate at which rain falls, measured in inches per hour (in/hr) or millimeters per hour. Design rainfall intensity is used to size storm drainage systems to handle expected precipitation without flooding.

Storm Frequency

Also called return period or recurrence interval:

  • 10-year storm: Storm intensity expected to be exceeded once every 10 years on average (10% annual chance)
  • 25-year storm: 4% annual chance of exceedance
  • 50-year storm: 2% annual chance of exceedance
  • 100-year storm: 1% annual chance of exceedance (common design standard)

Note: A 100-year storm can occur in consecutive years - it represents statistical probability, not a prediction.

Storm Duration

The length of time over which rainfall occurs at a given intensity:

  • Short Duration (5-15 min): Very intense rainfall, used for roof drains and small areas
  • Medium Duration (30-60 min): Standard for storm drainage design
  • Long Duration (2-24 hr): Used for detention basin sizing and watershed analysis

IDF Curves

Intensity-Duration-Frequency curves show the relationship between rainfall intensity, storm duration, and return period:

  • Intensity decreases as duration increases (shorter storms are more intense)
  • Intensity increases as return period increases (rarer storms are more intense)
  • IDF curves are location-specific based on historical rainfall data
  • NOAA Atlas 14 provides IDF data for the continental United States

Time of Concentration

The time required for runoff from the most hydraulically distant point to reach the point of interest:

  • Storm duration for design typically equals time of concentration
  • Shorter Tc = higher intensity = larger pipes needed
  • Minimum Tc usually 5-10 minutes for small urban areas
  • Calculated based on overland flow time plus pipe flow time

Climate Change Considerations

Many jurisdictions are updating design standards to account for climate change:

  • Increasing design storm intensities by 10-30%
  • Using higher return periods for critical infrastructure
  • Planning for more frequent extreme events
  • Check with local authorities for adopted climate factors

Regional Variations

  • Pacific Northwest: Lower intensities, long rainy seasons
  • Southeast/Gulf Coast: Very high intensities, tropical storms
  • Southwest: Highly variable, intense monsoon storms
  • Mountain West: Influenced by elevation and orographic effects
  • Midwest/Northeast: Moderate intensities, seasonal variation

Local Jurisdiction Requirements

Always check with your local building department, public works department, or water authority for adopted design standards. Many jurisdictions have specific requirements that supersede NOAA data.

How to Use NOAA Atlas 14

  1. Go to hdsc.nws.noaa.gov/pfds
  2. Select your state and location on the map (or enter coordinates)
  3. Choose precipitation type (partial duration or annual maximum)
  4. Select return period (e.g., 100-year)
  5. Select duration (e.g., 60-min for roof drains)
  6. Review intensity value and 90% confidence limits
  7. Print or save data for project records

Professional Disclaimer

Rainfall intensity data shown in this tool is approximate and intended for educational purposes and preliminary estimates only. For actual design work, obtain site-specific precipitation frequency data from NOAA Atlas 14 or local jurisdiction requirements. Rainfall intensities vary significantly within metropolitan areas due to microclimates and topography. Design storm frequency and duration must comply with local building codes and drainage standards. For any storm drainage design, consult a licensed professional engineer and obtain approval from local authorities.
