Overview

Variable Air Volume (VAV) terminal units, commonly called VAV boxes, are zone-level air distribution devices that regulate the volume of conditioned air delivered to a space based on heating or cooling demand. Unlike constant volume systems that vary air temperature, VAV systems vary airflow while maintaining a relatively constant supply air temperature (typically 55°F).

VAV boxes are the workhorses of modern commercial HVAC systems, found in office buildings, schools, hospitals, hotels, and virtually every type of commercial facility. A typical building may have dozens to hundreds of VAV boxes, each serving an individual zone or room, providing independent temperature control while allowing the central air handling unit to operate more efficiently.

Energy Savings vs. Constant Volume

  • Fan energy reduction: 30–50% (due to reduced airflow and static pressure)
  • Cooling energy reduction: 10–20% (less overcooling of zones)
  • Key strategy: Reset supply air temperature up as loads decrease to minimize reheat energy waste

A VAV terminal unit receives constant-temperature supply air (typically 55°F) from the central AHU and modulates airflow to each zone based on demand:

Basic Operation

  1. Airflow Measurement: A velocity pressure sensor measures the airflow entering the box.
  2. Zone Thermostat: A thermostat in the zone measures space temperature and compares it to setpoint.
  3. Damper Modulation: Based on the temperature signal, the controller modulates a damper to increase or decrease airflow.
  4. Cooling Mode: When the space needs cooling, the damper opens to increase cold airflow. When satisfied, it closes to minimum position.
  5. Heating Mode (if equipped): When heating is required, airflow remains at minimum while reheat (electric, hot water, or steam) is activated.

System Interaction

  • As VAV boxes close, duct static pressure increases
  • Static pressure sensor signals AHU to slow supply fan (via VFD)
  • Fan energy reduces proportionally to cube of speed reduction
  • Result: Significant fan energy savings at part-load conditions
  • Office Buildings: Most common application. Each office, conference room, and open area has its own VAV box for independent control.
  • Schools & Universities: Each classroom gets a VAV box. Energy savings significant due to unoccupied periods.
  • Hospitals: Patient rooms, offices, and common areas. Critical areas (ORs, isolation rooms) may use other systems.
  • Hotels: Meeting rooms and common areas have larger boxes. Guest rooms typically use FCUs or PTACs.
  • Retail: Sales floors divided into zones. Fitting rooms often have dedicated boxes for comfort.
  • Laboratories: Lab spaces have VAV boxes coordinated with fume hood exhaust for proper pressure control.
  • Data Centers: Specialized high-capacity VAV boxes for server room cooling with precise control.

Types of VAV Boxes

The simplest VAV configuration. Provides cooling only by modulating cold air from maximum to minimum flow. No heating capability. Used in spaces that never require heating, such as interior zones of large buildings.

Adds a heating coil downstream of the damper for heating mode. The most common VAV type for perimeter zones. During cooling, operates like a cooling-only box. During heating, airflow goes to minimum while the reheat coil activates.

  • Hot Water Reheat: Most common. Requires hot water piping (180°F enter / 160°F leave). Lower operating cost than electric. Suitable for all sizes.
  • Electric Reheat: Simplest installation, no piping required, but highest operating cost. Typically limited to boxes <1,500 CFM due to electrical load.
  • Steam Reheat: Rapid response, used in hospitals and labs. Requires steam piping and condensate return.

Controls & Selection

Feature Pressure Dependent Pressure Independent
Airflow Control Damper position only; airflow varies with duct pressure Measures actual airflow & adjusts damper to maintain setpoint
Accuracy Poor when duct pressure fluctuates Maintains exact CFM regardless of pressure changes
Balancing Requires careful system balancing Easier — self-correcting
Cost Lower ($100–$200 less per box) Higher (includes flow sensor + controller)
Best For Small systems (<10 boxes), budget projects Standard for modern commercial buildings

Recommendation: Pressure independent controls are the standard for modern commercial buildings and are required for LEED points. Use pressure dependent only on very small systems or budget-constrained projects with stable duct pressures.

Modern VAV boxes communicate with Building Management Systems for centralized monitoring and control:

  • Protocols: BACnet (most common), Modbus, LonWorks
  • Points Monitored: Actual airflow, zone temperature, damper position, reheat output, occupancy status
  • Benefits: Remote monitoring, trend logging, fault detection, energy optimization, automated demand-control ventilation
  • Cost Adder: $150–$300 per box for BACnet/Modbus integration

Control & Monitoring

  • CO2-Based Demand Control Ventilation: Adjust minimum airflow based on occupancy (CO2 levels)
  • Occupancy Sensors: Reduce airflow to unoccupied setback when space is vacant
  • Airflow Monitoring: Display or transmit actual CFM to BAS for verification and trending

Acoustic Options

  • Acoustic Liner: Reduces generated noise by 5–10 NC rating points
  • Discharge Silencer: For critical applications like recording studios or patient rooms
  • Flex Duct: 3–6 feet from box to diffuser helps reduce noise transmission

Physical Options

  • Inlet Configurations: Round, rectangular, top, side, end — to match duct arrangement
  • Damper Types: Single-blade (standard) or opposed-blade (better control at partial loads)
  • Access Panels: For easy maintenance and coil cleaning
  • Insulation: Internal or external to prevent condensation in high-humidity applications

Key Sizing Rules

  • Don't Oversize: Oversized boxes operate at low velocities where control is poor and sensors are inaccurate. Select the closest standard size above calculated maximum.
  • Minimum Airflow: Must meet the greater of: (1) ASHRAE 62.1 ventilation requirements, (2) 30% of max for proper air distribution, or (3) minimum duct velocity to prevent stratification.
  • Inlet Sizing: Size inlet duct for 800–1,200 FPM at maximum airflow for adequate sensor accuracy.
  • Standard Sizes: 100, 150, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 800, 1000, 1250, 1500, 2000, 2500, 3000, 4000, 5000 CFM.

Common Sizing Mistakes

  • Selecting boxes based on room area × CFM/sq ft without considering actual cooling load
  • Oversizing "to be safe" — results in poor control and noise issues
  • Not considering minimum ventilation requirements per ASHRAE 62.1
  • Ignoring acoustic requirements — selecting a box that meets airflow but exceeds NC limits
  • Not coordinating inlet duct size with box inlet size — causes turbulence and noise

Example: Private Office, 150 SF

  • Cooling load: 1,500 BTU/hr
  • Max CFM: 1,500 BTU/hr ÷ (1.08 × 20°F ΔT) = 69 CFM
  • Ventilation requirement: Per ASHRAE 62.1 for office
  • Min CFM: 69 × 0.30 = 21 CFM (use 30% rule)
  • Select: 70 CFM max VAV with HW reheat, 21 CFM minimum
  • Reheat coil: 150 SF × 35 BTU/hr/SF = 5,250 BTU/hr = 1-row HW coil at 180°F

Best Practices

  • Perform room-by-room cooling load calculation — don't rely solely on area-based estimates
  • Calculate ventilation requirements per ASHRAE 62.1 and ensure minimum airflow meets or exceeds this
  • Use manufacturer selection software for final selection — includes acoustic analysis
  • Coordinate VAV locations with ceiling grid and diffuser layout
  • Plan for adequate access to boxes for maintenance (minimum 24" clearance recommended)
  • Size ducts properly — undersized inlet ducts cause noise and control problems
  • Specify pressure independent controls for systems larger than 10–15 boxes
  • Locate boxes in accessible ceiling spaces — NOT above inaccessible areas
  • Avoid locating boxes directly above occupied workstations (noise)
  • Size reheat for actual heating load, not just "throw in a coil"
  • Support boxes independently — don't hang from ductwork
  • Provide 3–5 duct diameters of straight duct upstream of box inlet for proper airflow measurement
  • Install with adequate clearance for damper and coil access
  • Follow manufacturer's wiring diagrams exactly — improper wiring is #1 cause of control problems
  • Insulate hot water piping to coils to prevent heat loss in plenum
  • Install flow switches on hot water coils to prevent coil freeze-up
  • Use flex duct connections to prevent vibration transmission
  • Wire controls before installing ceiling — easier access
  • Label each box clearly with zone number/name
  • Set maximum and minimum airflows using box controller, not damper stops
  • Verify actual airflow with independent measurement — don't rely solely on box readout
  • Test controls through full range of operation (heating, cooling, unoccupied)
  • Document settings for each box (max CFM, min CFM, heating capacity, etc.)
  • Perform functional testing per ASHRAE Guideline 0 or equivalent
  • Calibrate airflow sensors per manufacturer instructions
  • Verify static pressure control with VAV boxes modulating
  • Check sound levels in occupied spaces
  • Train operators on system operation and troubleshooting

Quarterly

  • Check for unusual noise during operation
  • Verify controller is displaying and responding
  • Spot-check airflow readings vs. setpoints
  • Review BAS alarms and trending for this zone
  • Change filters on fan-powered units

Annual

  • Verify actual airflow with independent measurement
  • Exercise damper through full range of motion
  • Check damper blade linkage for wear
  • Inspect hot water coil for leaks
  • Test electric heaters (continuity and amperage)
  • Verify zone temperature sensors
  • Clean airflow sensor ports

Every 3–5 Years

  • Recalibrate airflow sensors
  • Full functional test (heating, cooling, min, max)
  • Retune control loops if hunting observed
  • Update controller firmware if available
  • Consider replacement of 15+ year old controllers
  • Inspect fan bearings and motors (fan-powered units)

Access for Service

  • VAV boxes require periodic service (filter changes on FP boxes, sensor calibration)
  • Provide ceiling access panels within 10 feet of each box
  • Don't install above hard ceilings or in inaccessible soffits
  • Consider catwalks or walkable ceiling grid for large installations

Minimum Flow & Ventilation

  • Energy codes and ASHRAE 62.1 have strict requirements
  • Can't just set minimum to 0% — need adequate ventilation air
  • Some boxes may run at minimum most of the time (interior zones)
  • Consider DOAS (Dedicated Outdoor Air System) for high-ventilation applications to reduce reheat energy

Acoustics

  • Noise increases at high inlet pressures and partially-closed dampers
  • Specify acoustic liner for noise-sensitive areas
  • Use flex duct from box to diffuser to attenuate breakout noise
  • Always use manufacturer selection software to verify NC ratings at design conditions

Excessive Airflow Damper stuck open, calibration error, high inlet pressure Repair damper, recalibrate controller, reduce system pressure

Hunting/Cycling Poor PID tuning, oversized box, pressure fluctuations Retune PID settings, replace with smaller box, stabilize system pressure
Reheat Not Working Valve stuck, no hot water, electric heater failure, wiring error Repair/replace valve, restore HW, replace heater elements, correct wiring
Inaccurate Flow Reading Sensor drift, turbulent inlet, controller calibration, damaged sensor Recalibrate sensor, add straight duct upstream, replace sensor

Step 1: Gather Information

  • What is the specific complaint? (too hot, too cold, noisy, etc.)
  • When did the problem start? Is it constant or intermittent?
  • Has anything changed recently? (setpoints, occupancy, renovations)
  • Are other zones affected?

Step 2: Check the Basics

  • Power supply to VAV box controller
  • Thermostat batteries (if applicable) and settings
  • Damper physically moves when commanded
  • No alarms or faults on controller
  • Upstream air handling unit is operating

Step 3: Verify Airflow

  • Check actual airflow vs. setpoint (should be within ±10%)
  • Verify minimum and maximum airflow settings
  • Ensure box is not at minimum when zone calls for cooling
  • Measure inlet static pressure — low pressure = insufficient flow

Step 4: Check Controls

  • Verify zone temperature sensor is reading correctly
  • Check setpoints and schedules (occupied vs. unoccupied)
  • Test override commands from BAS
  • Verify control sequence is appropriate for mode (heating vs. cooling)

Damper Hunting (Oscillating)

Symptoms: Damper continuously moves, airflow unstable, zone temperature swings

  • Increase proportional band (make less sensitive)
  • Add damper action delay or deadband
  • Reduce integral gain if using PI or PID control
  • Check for and correct oversizing — may need smaller box
  • Investigate system pressure stability — may need VFD tuning on AHU

Won't Reach Minimum Airflow

Symptoms: Box reports less than minimum setpoint, zone overcools

  • Measure inlet pressure — should be at least 0.5 in. w.c.
  • Check damper for proper sealing when closed
  • Verify minimum setpoint is appropriate
  • Recalibrate flow sensor
  • Check for downstream duct leaks

Reheat Discharge Temp Too High

Symptoms: Discharge air exceeds 90–95°F, occupant "baking" complaints

  • Verify minimum airflow is met during heating
  • Check reheat capacity — may be oversized for the airflow
  • Add discharge temperature limit control (90–95°F max)
  • For electric: stage heaters to prevent all elements firing simultaneously

Key Measurement Points

  • Inlet Static Pressure: Measure in duct just upstream of box (should be 0.5–2.0 in. w.c.)
  • Actual Airflow: Measure with pitot tube traverse or capture hood at diffuser
  • Discharge Air Temperature: Should not exceed 90–95°F per ASHRAE guidelines
  • Damper Position: 0–100% or 0–10V signal — verify matches actual position
  • Zone Temperature: Compare thermostat to independent measurement
  • Hot Water Temperatures: Entering and leaving (typical: 180°F enter, 160°F leave)

Safety Considerations

  • Use ladder safety — VAV boxes are often 10–20 feet above floor
  • Watch for sharp edges on sheet metal boxes and ductwork
  • Be aware of electrical hazards — 24V controls but may be 120–480V for reheat
  • Don't bypass safeties, especially high-limit thermostats on electric heaters
  • Hot water coils can reach 180–200°F — use caution
  • Wear appropriate PPE: safety glasses, gloves, hard hat if required

Specifications & Pricing

Electric Reheat

  • Capacity: 1–20 kW
  • Temp Rise: 3,413 BTU/hr per kW
  • Formula: kW = (CFM × ΔT) / 3,413
  • Voltage: 120V, 208V, 240V, 277V, 480V
  • Stages: Usually 2–3 for better control

Hot Water Reheat

  • Entering Water: 180°F typical
  • Leaving Water: 160°F (20°F drop)
  • Entering Air: 55°F (primary air temp)
  • GPM: GPM = MBH / (500 × ΔT water)
  • Coil Selection: 1–8 rows typical

Steam Reheat

  • Steam Pressure: 2–15 PSIG typical
  • Capacity: ~970 BTU/lb at 5 PSIG
  • Control Valve: Modulating or two-position
  • Condensate Return: Required
  • Applications: Hospitals, labs, rapid response

Energy Savings: ECM vs. PSC Motors

For a 600 CFM series fan-powered VAV box running 8,760 hours/year at $0.12/kWh:

  • PSC Motor: 600 × 0.8 W/CFM = 480W = 4,205 kWh/yr = $504/year
  • ECM Motor: 600 × 0.4 W/CFM = 240W = 2,102 kWh/yr = $252/year
  • Savings: $252/year per box, 2–4 year payback

Installation Considerations

  • Ceiling Clearance: Verify adequate height above ceiling — boxes can be 24–36" tall
  • Access: Minimum 24" clearance for damper and coil service
  • Support: Independently supported — not hung from ductwork
  • Straight Duct: 3–5 diameters before inlet for accurate flow measurement
  • Electrical: 24VAC for controls, 120–480VAC for electric reheat
  • Piping: Hot water supply and return if HW reheat specified
