Understanding Backflow Prevention

What is Backflow?

Backflow is the unwanted reverse flow of water or other substances from a plumbing fixture, vessel, or system back into the potable water supply. This cross-connection can contaminate drinking water with chemicals, fertilizers, human waste, or other hazardous materials.

Types of Backflow

Backsiphonage

Occurs when negative pressure (vacuum) in the supply line pulls contaminated water backward into the potable supply. Caused by water main breaks, high withdrawal rates, or supply line repairs.

Backpressure

Occurs when downstream pressure exceeds supply pressure, forcing contaminated water back into the potable supply. Caused by pumps, thermal expansion, elevation differences, or pressure vessel discharge.

Hazard Levels

High Hazard (Health Hazard)

Substances that pose a serious health threat if ingested. Examples: sewage, toxic chemicals, radioactive materials, industrial fluids. Requires highest level of protection.

Moderate Hazard (Non-Health Hazard)

Substances objectionable but not immediately dangerous to health. Examples: process water, softened water, swimming pool water, irrigation water.

Low Hazard

Nuisance conditions with minimal contamination risk. Examples: residential fixtures with air gap, simple garden hoses without chemical injectors.

Types of Backflow Prevention Devices

RPZ (Reduced Pressure Zone Assembly)

  • Protection: Highest level - high hazard, backpressure and backsiphonage
  • Applications: Industrial, fire sprinklers, irrigation with chemicals
  • Installation: Above ground, accessible, 12" minimum above grade
  • Testing: Annual by certified tester
  • Features: Relief valve discharges on failure, testable

DCVA (Double Check Valve Assembly)

  • Protection: Moderate - low to moderate hazard, backpressure and backsiphonage
  • Applications: Commercial buildings, fire sprinklers (non-chemical), boilers
  • Installation: Above or below ground (if approved)
  • Testing: Annual by certified tester
  • Features: Two check valves in series with test cocks

PVB (Pressure Vacuum Breaker)

  • Protection: Backsiphonage only (not backpressure)
  • Applications: Irrigation systems, residential hose bibbs
  • Installation: Must be 12" above highest downstream outlet
  • Testing: Annual by certified tester
  • Features: Cannot have shutoff valve downstream, testable

AVB (Atmospheric Vacuum Breaker)

  • Protection: Backsiphonage only, non-continuous pressure
  • Applications: Toilets, urinals, individual fixtures
  • Installation: Must be 6" above flood level rim
  • Testing: Not testable, replace if suspected failure
  • Features: Simple, inexpensive, cannot be under continuous pressure

Air Gap

  • Protection: Absolute - highest level of protection
  • Applications: Sinks, drinking fountains, cooling towers
  • Installation: Vertical separation between outlet and flood level
  • Testing: Visual inspection only
  • Features: No mechanical parts, foolproof but space-intensive

Backflow Prevention Troubleshooting

Common Backflow Preventer Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
RPZ Relief Valve Discharging Check valve fouling, debris, excessive pressure differential, failed relief valve Test differential pressure, inspect check valves, verify supply pressure Clean or replace check valves, replace relief valve, reduce supply pressure if excessive
Failed Annual Test Worn seals, debris in checks, spring failure, damaged diaphragm Disassemble and inspect all components, check for debris and wear Replace repair kit (seals, springs, diaphragms), flush system, re-test
Low Pressure Downstream Undersized device, check valves stuck partially closed, debris obstruction Verify proper sizing for flow rate, test pressure drop across device, inspect for debris Upsize device if undersized, clean or replace check valves, flush system
Water Hammer Fast-closing valves downstream, no air chambers, excessive pressure Identify valve closing speed, check for water hammer arrestors, measure pressure Install water hammer arrestors, slow valve closing, install pressure reducing valve

Installation Best Practices

  • Install with proper clearances for testing and maintenance access
  • RPZ must be minimum 12" above grade with adequate drainage for relief valve discharge
  • PVB must be 12" above highest downstream outlet, no downstream shutoff valves
  • Provide shutoff valves on both sides for testing and maintenance
  • Ensure proper orientation (most devices must be installed horizontally)
  • Protect outdoor devices from freezing with insulation or heat trace
  • Keep detailed records of testing and maintenance

Professional Disclaimer

Backflow prevention requirements are determined by local plumbing codes, water authority regulations, and specific site conditions. This guide provides general information only. Always consult with local authorities having jurisdiction, follow manufacturer installation instructions, and use certified testers for annual testing. Improper backflow prevention can result in contamination of potable water supplies.
