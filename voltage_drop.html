Voltage Drop Information

What is Voltage Drop?

Voltage drop is the decrease in electrical potential along the path of a current flowing in an electrical circuit. It occurs due to the resistance of the conductors and is a critical factor in electrical system design. Excessive voltage drop can lead to inefficient equipment operation, overheating, and premature equipment failure.

NEC Requirements

The National Electrical Code (NEC) provides guidance on acceptable voltage drop in Article 210.19(A) Informational Note No. 4 and Article 215.2(A)(1) Informational Note No. 2:

  • Branch Circuits: Maximum recommended voltage drop of 3% for reasonable efficiency of operation
  • Feeders: Maximum recommended voltage drop of 2% for reasonable efficiency of operation
  • Combined (Feeder + Branch): Maximum recommended voltage drop of 5% for reasonable efficiency of operation
Important: While the NEC recommendations are not mandatory requirements, they represent industry best practices for ensuring efficient system operation and equipment longevity.

Voltage Drop Formulas

Single-Phase AC Circuits:

VD = 2 × K × I × L × (R × cos(θ) + X × sin(θ))

  • VD = Voltage Drop (volts)
  • K = Constant (1 for calculations in consistent units)
  • I = Current (amperes)
  • L = One-way length of circuit (feet)
  • R = Resistance per 1000 feet (ohms)
  • X = Reactance per 1000 feet (ohms)
  • cos(θ) = Power factor
  • sin(θ) = Reactive factor

Three-Phase AC Circuits:

VD = √3 × K × I × L × (R × cos(θ) + X × sin(θ))

  • Same parameters as single-phase, with √3 factor for three-phase systems

DC Circuits:

VD = 2 × K × I × L × R

  • VD = Voltage Drop (volts)
  • K = Constant (1 for calculations in consistent units)
  • I = Current (amperes)
  • L = One-way length of circuit (feet)
  • R = Resistance per 1000 feet (ohms)

Factors Affecting Voltage Drop

  • Conductor Size: Larger conductors have lower resistance and therefore less voltage drop
  • Circuit Length: Voltage drop increases proportionally with circuit length
  • Load Current: Higher current results in greater voltage drop
  • Conductor Material: Copper has lower resistance than aluminum for the same size
  • Temperature: Conductor resistance increases with temperature
  • Power Factor: Lower power factor in AC circuits increases voltage drop
  • Conduit Type: Metallic conduits can affect AC impedance

Design Considerations

When designing electrical systems to minimize voltage drop:

  • Size conductors appropriately for the load and distance
  • Consider oversizing conductors for long circuit runs
  • Locate electrical equipment as close as possible to the load
  • Balance loads across phases in three-phase systems
  • Consider power factor correction for inductive loads
  • Account for future load growth in initial design

Voltage Drop Troubleshooting

Common Voltage Drop Problems

Problem: Lights Dimming When Equipment Starts

Symptoms:

  • Lights flicker or dim when motors or large loads start
  • More noticeable at end of long circuit runs
  • Equipment may run but at reduced capacity

Causes:

  • Undersized conductors for the load
  • Excessive circuit length
  • High inrush current from motor loads
  • Poor connections creating additional resistance

Solutions:

  • Upsize conductors to reduce resistance
  • Install soft-start devices on motors
  • Provide dedicated circuits for large loads
  • Check and tighten all connections
  • Consider voltage stabilizers or buck-boost transformers

Problem: Equipment Not Operating at Full Capacity

Symptoms:

  • Motors running slower than rated speed
  • Heating elements not reaching full temperature
  • Equipment tripping on overload protection
  • Reduced lighting levels

Causes:

  • Excessive voltage drop in supply circuit
  • Undersized feeders or branch circuits
  • Utility voltage issues
  • Voltage drop exceeding NEC recommendations

Solutions:

  • Calculate actual voltage drop and compare to NEC recommendations
  • Upgrade conductor size in affected circuits
  • Relocate equipment closer to power source if possible
  • Install voltage monitoring equipment
  • Contact utility if supply voltage is consistently low

Problem: Conductors Running Hot

Symptoms:

  • Warm or hot conductors under normal load
  • Melted insulation or discolored conductors
  • Hot electrical panels or junction boxes
  • Burning smell from electrical equipment

Causes:

  • Undersized conductors causing excessive current density
  • Poor connections creating resistance and heat
  • Overloaded circuits
  • High ambient temperature affecting conductor ampacity

Solutions:

  • Verify conductor sizing meets ampacity requirements
  • Check all connections for tightness
  • Verify actual load does not exceed circuit rating
  • Apply temperature correction factors if needed
  • Install additional circuits to reduce load per circuit

Problem: Low Voltage Affecting Sensitive Equipment

Symptoms:

  • Computer equipment resetting or malfunctioning
  • Electronic controls not operating properly
  • LED lights flickering or dimming
  • Variable frequency drives (VFDs) faulting

Causes:

  • Voltage drop exceeding equipment tolerance
  • Long circuit runs to sensitive loads
  • Shared circuits with heavy loads
  • Inadequate voltage regulation

Solutions:

  • Provide dedicated circuits for sensitive equipment
  • Install voltage regulators or UPS systems
  • Upsize conductors to minimize voltage drop
  • Separate sensitive loads from heavy loads
  • Consider installing isolation transformers

Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

  1. Measure Voltage: Use a quality multimeter to measure voltage at the source and at the load under full load conditions
  2. Calculate Expected Drop: Use circuit parameters to calculate theoretical voltage drop
  3. Compare Values: Compare measured drop to calculated and NEC recommendations
  4. Inspect Connections: Check all connections, terminals, and splices for tightness and corrosion
  5. Verify Load: Confirm actual load current matches design expectations
  6. Check Conductor Size: Verify installed conductor size matches design drawings
  7. Implement Solution: Address root cause through upsizing, load reduction, or circuit modification
  8. Re-test: Verify voltage drop is within acceptable limits after modifications

Temperature Correction

Conductor resistance increases with temperature. The resistance at any temperature can be calculated using:

R₂ = R₁ × [1 + α(T₂ - T₁)]

  • R₁ = Resistance at reference temperature T₁
  • R₂ = Resistance at temperature T₂
  • α = Temperature coefficient (0.00323/°F for copper, 0.00353/°F for aluminum)
