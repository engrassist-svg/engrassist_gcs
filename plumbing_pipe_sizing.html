Sizing Guidelines & Reference Values

Fixture Unit Values (WSFU)

Water Supply Fixture Units (WSFU) are standardized values from IPC/UPC codes that represent the water demand of plumbing fixtures. These values account for flow rate and probability of simultaneous use.

Velocity Limits by Pipe Material

  • Copper - Cold Water: 8 ft/s maximum
  • Copper - Hot Water: 5 ft/s maximum
  • PEX - Cold Water: 10 ft/s maximum
  • PEX - Hot Water: 8 ft/s maximum
  • CPVC - Cold Water: 8 ft/s maximum
  • CPVC - Hot Water: 5 ft/s maximum
  • Galvanized Steel: 5 ft/s maximum (both hot and cold)

Pressure Requirements

  • Minimum Supply Pressure: 40-60 psi
  • Maximum Supply Pressure: 80 psi (pressure reducer required above this)
  • Minimum Fixture Pressure: 15-20 psi for most fixtures
  • Flush Valve Toilets: 25-35 psi minimum

Overview

Proper domestic water pipe sizing is essential for delivering adequate water flow and pressure to all plumbing fixtures while minimizing energy costs, water waste, and pipe erosion. Undersized pipes result in poor flow and low pressure, while oversized pipes waste money, increase water waste, and can lead to water quality issues.

Domestic water systems must be designed to deliver water at sufficient pressure (typically 40-60 psi at the supply, minimum 15-20 psi at fixtures) while maintaining acceptable flow velocities to prevent noise, erosion, and water hammer.

How Pipe Sizing Works

Plumbing pipe sizing involves calculating the water demand for a building or system, then selecting pipe sizes that can deliver that flow while maintaining:

Key Design Parameters

  1. Adequate Flow Rate: Sufficient GPM to meet fixture demands
  2. Minimum Pressure: Enough pressure at the most remote fixture
  3. Acceptable Velocity: Fast enough for efficiency, slow enough to prevent erosion
  4. Reasonable Pressure Loss: Minimize friction losses through proper sizing

The process accounts for the fact that not all fixtures will be used simultaneously. This "diversity factor" is built into the fixture unit method developed by Roy B. Hunter in 1940.

Sizing Methods

1. Fixture Unit Method (Hunter's Curve)

Most Common Method

Assigns standardized Water Supply Fixture Unit (WSFU) values to each fixture type. Total fixture units are converted to GPM using probability curves (Hunter's Curve) that account for diversity of use.

  • Advantages: Standardized, code-approved, accounts for probability
  • Disadvantages: Can be conservative (oversized) with modern low-flow fixtures
  • Best for: Residential and commercial buildings, code compliance

2. Direct GPM Method

Calculates actual flow rates based on fixture flow rates and estimated simultaneous use.

  • Advantages: More accurate for specific applications, accounts for low-flow fixtures
  • Disadvantages: Requires more detailed analysis, less standardized
  • Best for: Custom applications, water-efficient designs

3. Velocity-Based Sizing

Selects pipe size to maintain velocities within acceptable ranges (typically 4-8 ft/s).

  • Advantages: Prevents erosion and noise, simple to calculate
  • Disadvantages: Must be combined with pressure loss calculations
  • Best for: Final verification after initial sizing

Pipe Materials

Copper (Type L or M)

Industry Standard - Excellent Track Record

  • Advantages: Durable, long lifespan (50+ years), handles high temperatures and pressures, naturally antibacterial, recyclable
  • Disadvantages: Higher material cost, requires skilled installation, can corrode in acidic water, affected by mineral buildup
  • Typical Applications: All residential and commercial plumbing, especially hot water systems
  • Velocity Limits: 8 ft/s (cold), 5 ft/s (hot)
  • Temperature Rating: Up to 400°F (annealed), 250°F (drawn)

PEX (Cross-Linked Polyethylene)

Modern Choice - Growing Market Share

  • Advantages: Flexible (fewer fittings), freeze-resistant, corrosion-proof, lower cost, easy installation, quiet operation
  • Disadvantages: Cannot be used outdoors (UV sensitive), not suitable for recirculation loops without special consideration, some concerns about chemical leaching
  • Typical Applications: Residential plumbing, radiant floor heating, retrofit applications
  • Velocity Limits: 10 ft/s (cold), 8 ft/s (hot)
  • Temperature Rating: 200°F at 80 psi, 180°F at 100 psi

CPVC (Chlorinated Polyvinyl Chloride)

Plastic Alternative to Copper

  • Advantages: Lower cost than copper, corrosion-resistant, easy to work with, good for hot water
  • Disadvantages: Brittle (can crack), affected by chlorine, UV sensitive, lower temperature rating than copper
  • Typical Applications: Residential hot and cold water, budget installations
  • Velocity Limits: 8 ft/s (cold), 5 ft/s (hot)
  • Temperature Rating: 180-200°F at 100 psi

Galvanized Steel

Legacy Material - Rarely Used in New Construction

  • Advantages: Very strong, handles high pressures, fire-resistant
  • Disadvantages: Prone to corrosion and mineral buildup, heavy, difficult to work with, reduced flow over time
  • Typical Applications: Older buildings (pre-1960s), some commercial applications
  • Velocity Limits: 5 ft/s (both hot and cold)
  • Expected Life: 40-50 years, but flow degrades significantly

Fixture Units Explained

Water Supply Fixture Units (WSFU) were developed by Roy B. Hunter at the National Bureau of Standards in 1940. The system assigns each fixture a unit value based on:

  • Flow Rate: GPM demanded by the fixture
  • Duration: How long the fixture operates per use
  • Frequency: How often the fixture is used

Common Fixture Unit Values (IPC/UPC)

  • Water Closet (Tank): 2.5 WSFU (cold only)
  • Water Closet (Flush Valve): 6.0 WSFU (cold only)
  • Lavatory: 0.5 WSFU (hot and cold each)
  • Shower: 1.0 WSFU (hot and cold each)
  • Bathtub: 1.0 WSFU (hot and cold each)
  • Kitchen Sink: 0.75 WSFU (hot and cold each)
  • Dishwasher: 1.4 WSFU (hot only)
  • Clothes Washer: 1.0 WSFU (hot and cold each)
  • Hose Bibb (3/4"): 2.5 WSFU (cold only)

Converting Fixture Units to GPM

Hunter's Curve converts total fixture units to probable flow rate. The relationship is non-linear because the probability of simultaneous use decreases as more fixtures are added. Example conversions:

  • 5 WSFU: ≈ 4 GPM
  • 10 WSFU: ≈ 8 GPM
  • 20 WSFU: ≈ 14 GPM
  • 30 WSFU: ≈ 20 GPM
  • 50 WSFU: ≈ 30 GPM
  • 100 WSFU: ≈ 50 GPM

Velocity and Pressure

Why Velocity Matters

Too Fast (> 8-10 ft/s)

  • Erosion-corrosion of pipe walls, especially in copper
  • Water hammer and noise
  • Excessive pressure drop
  • Increased wear on valves and fittings

Too Slow (< 2 ft/s)

  • Oversized pipes waste money
  • Water sits in pipes longer (water quality issues)
  • Temperature loss in hot water lines
  • More fixtures = more water waste waiting for hot water

Pressure Loss Considerations

Pressure loss through piping is caused by friction between water and pipe walls. Loss increases with:

  • Flow Rate: Higher flow = more loss (exponential relationship)
  • Pipe Length: Longer runs = more loss (linear)
  • Smaller Diameter: Smaller pipes = much higher loss
  • Roughness: Older/rougher pipes have higher friction
  • Fittings: Each elbow, tee, or valve adds loss

Rule of Thumb: Budget 5-10 psi loss per 100 feet of pipe, plus 2-5 psi for each fixture supply.

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Start with a detailed fixture schedule including fixture types and quantities
  • Calculate hot and cold water demands separately
  • Use Hunter's Curve for code compliance, verify with velocity calculations
  • Account for pressure loss: friction in pipes, elevation changes, meter loss, fixtures
  • Consider future expansion - easier to install larger pipe now
  • Design for recirculation on hot water runs over 50-75 feet
  • Specify pressure-reducing valves if supply exceeds 80 psi

Installation

  • Maintain proper support spacing per code (copper: 6 ft horizontal, 10 ft vertical)
  • Slope horizontal pipes 1/4" per foot for drainage
  • Install isolation valves for future maintenance
  • Use proper insulation on hot water pipes (minimum R-3)
  • Install water hammer arrestors where needed (washing machines, dishwashers)
  • Test entire system at 150% working pressure for leaks
  • Flush thoroughly before final connections

Residential Best Practices

  • Service Entrance: Minimum 3/4", often 1" for larger homes
  • Main Distribution: 3/4" trunk, 1/2" branches to individual fixtures
  • Fixture Supplies: 3/8" or 1/2" supply tubes
  • Manifold Systems: 3/4" or 1" manifold with 1/2" or 3/8" PEX home runs
  • Hot Water Recirculation: 3/4" supply, 1/2" return typical

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Undersizing Errors

  • Mistake: Using 1/2" pipe for main trunk lines serving multiple fixtures
  • Result: Low pressure at fixtures, especially when multiple are in use
  • Solution: Use 3/4" for trunk lines, 1/2" for individual branches

Oversizing Errors

  • Mistake: Installing 1" or larger pipe everywhere "to be safe"
  • Result: Wasted money, water quality issues, long wait for hot water
  • Solution: Size based on actual demand, not "bigger is better"

Material Selection Errors

  • Mistake: Using CPVC for recirculation loops
  • Result: Premature failure due to constant hot water exposure
  • Solution: Use copper or specialized PEX for recirculation

Velocity Errors

  • Mistake: Ignoring velocity limits, especially on hot water
  • Result: Erosion-corrosion, premature pinhole leaks in copper
  • Solution: Calculate velocity, keep hot water ≤ 5 ft/s in copper

Code Requirements

Plumbing codes in the United States are primarily based on two model codes:

International Plumbing Code (IPC)

Adopted by most states, particularly in the eastern US. Published by the International Code Council (ICC).

  • Appendix E: Sizing of Water Piping System
  • Table E103.3(2): Water Supply Fixture Units
  • Table E103.3(3): Conversion to GPM

Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC)

Adopted primarily in western states. Published by the International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials (IAPMO).

  • Chapter 6: Water Supply and Distribution
  • Table 610.3: Fixture Unit Values
  • Table 610.4: Water Distribution Pipe Sizing

Always Verify Local Requirements

While IPC and UPC are model codes, many jurisdictions adopt them with amendments. Always check:

  • Local building department for adopted code version and amendments
  • State-specific modifications to model codes
  • Water purveyor requirements (water company may have additional rules)
  • Backflow prevention requirements
  • Water conservation ordinances (may require low-flow fixtures)

Plumbing System Troubleshooting

Common Domestic Water System Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
Low Pressure at All Fixtures Main valve partially closed, PRV set too low, undersized service, clogged meter or filter, municipal supply issue Check main valve, test pressure at meter and furthest fixture, check PRV setting, call water company Open main valve fully, adjust PRV, clean/replace filters, upgrade service size if needed
Low Pressure at One Fixture Clogged aerator/showerhead, kinked supply line, partially closed stop valve, debris in supply line Remove and clean aerator, check supply valve position, disconnect supply and test flow Clean or replace aerator, replace supply line, open valve, flush supply line
Low Pressure When Multiple Fixtures Run Undersized piping, insufficient supply pressure, pressure-reducing valve set too low Measure pressure with multiple fixtures running, check pipe sizes against demand, test PRV Upsize trunk lines, increase PRV setting (if possible), reduce simultaneous demand
Water Hammer / Banging Pipes Missing/failed water hammer arrestors, loose pipes, high pressure, fast-closing valves Identify when noise occurs (which fixture), check pressure, inspect pipe supports Install water hammer arrestors, secure pipes properly, reduce pressure if >80 psi, replace quick-closing valves
No Hot Water or Slow Hot Water Water heater issue, long pipe runs, no recirculation, undersized hot water piping, mixing valve problem Check water heater operation, measure pipe runs, test hot water flow rate, check mixing valves Repair/replace water heater, install recirculation system, insulate pipes, upsize piping, adjust/replace mixing valves
Discolored Water (Brown/Rusty) Corroded pipes (galvanized), municipal main break, sediment in water heater Determine if hot or cold (or both), check if municipal issue, flush water heater, inspect exposed piping Replace corroded piping, flush system if municipal issue, drain and flush water heater, install sediment filter
Fluctuating Pressure Faulty PRV, thermal expansion, undersized piping, municipal pressure variation Monitor pressure over time, check PRV operation, verify expansion tank, check with neighbors Replace PRV, install/replace expansion tank, upsize piping if needed, install pressure tank
Excessive Pressure (>80 psi) No PRV installed, failed PRV, thermal expansion without expansion tank Measure pressure at multiple locations, check for PRV, test PRV adjustment Install PRV, replace failed PRV, install thermal expansion tank, adjust PRV setting
Whistling/Squealing Pipes High velocity, partially closed valve, worn valve washer, resonance in piping Identify source of noise, check valve positions, measure pressure and flow Reduce pressure or upsize pipe, open valves fully, replace worn washers, install flow restrictors
Leaks at Joints Failed solder joint, loose fitting, corrosion, thermal expansion/contraction, water hammer Identify leak location and type, inspect joint condition, check for movement/vibration Re-solder copper joints, tighten compression fittings, replace corroded sections, secure pipes, add expansion loops

Diagnostic Tools and Techniques

Pressure Testing

  • Pressure Gauge: Install at hose bibb or test valve
  • Normal Range: 40-60 psi
  • Test Points: Meter, first fixture, last fixture
  • Dynamic Test: Measure while fixtures are running

Flow Testing

  • Bucket Test: Time how long to fill 5-gallon bucket
  • Expected Flow: 2-3 GPM (lavatory), 2.5 GPM (shower)
  • Low Flow: Clean aerators, check for restrictions
  • Multi-Fixture: Test simultaneous use

Visual Inspection

  • Pipe Condition: Check for corrosion, green stains (copper)
  • Support: Verify proper spacing and security
  • Leaks: Look for stains, minerals, mold
  • Valve Operation: Test all shutoff valves

Water Quality Testing

  • pH Level: Should be 6.5-8.5 (acidic water corrodes copper)
  • Hardness: High hardness causes scale buildup
  • Iron/Manganese: Causes staining
  • Chlorine: Can degrade plastic pipes over time

Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

Step 1: Define the Problem

  • What is the specific symptom?
  • When did it start? Sudden or gradual?
  • Which fixtures are affected? All or specific?
  • Hot water only, cold water only, or both?
  • Is it constant or intermittent?

Step 2: Gather Data

  • Measure water pressure at multiple locations
  • Test flow rates at affected fixtures
  • Check water heater settings and condition
  • Inspect visible piping for damage or corrosion
  • Review any recent plumbing work or changes

Step 3: Isolate the Cause

  • Test upstream vs. downstream of suspected problem
  • Isolate sections using shutoff valves
  • Compare hot vs. cold water systems
  • Check if problem is municipal or building-specific
  • Eliminate possibilities systematically

Safety Considerations

Always follow these safety procedures when troubleshooting plumbing:

  • Shut off water supply before working on pressurized systems
  • Relieve pressure before opening joints or valves
  • Be aware of scalding hazards with hot water systems
  • Use appropriate PPE: safety glasses, gloves
  • Know the location of main shutoff valve
  • Never drink or test water quality from hot water system
  • Call a licensed plumber for gas water heaters or complex issues

Preventive Maintenance

Annual Tasks

  • Test water pressure at multiple locations
  • Drain and flush water heater
  • Test pressure-reducing valve
  • Check expansion tank pre-charge
  • Inspect accessible piping for leaks/corrosion
  • Test all shutoff valves (exercise them)

Every 2-3 Years

  • Replace water heater anode rod
  • Clean aerators and showerheads
  • Test water quality (pH, hardness)
  • Inspect/replace supply line hoses (washing machine, dishwasher)
  • Check for hidden leaks (monitor water meter)

Every 5-10 Years

  • Replace PRV (if installed)
  • Replace expansion tank
  • Replace water heater (typical 10-12 year life)
  • Consider whole-house leak detection system
  • Evaluate pipe condition if galvanized or old copper

Typical Residential Pipe Sizing

Small Home (1-2 Bath)

  • Service Entrance: 3/4"
  • Main Trunk: 3/4"
  • Hot Water Heater: 3/4"
  • Branch to Bath: 1/2"
  • Fixture Supply: 3/8" or 1/2"

Medium Home (2-3 Bath)

  • Service Entrance: 1"
  • Main Trunk: 3/4"
  • Hot Water Heater: 3/4"
  • Branch to Bath: 1/2" or 3/4"
  • Fixture Supply: 3/8" or 1/2"

Large Home (3+ Bath)

  • Service Entrance: 1" or 1-1/4"
  • Main Trunk: 1"
  • Hot Water Heater: 3/4" or 1"
  • Branch to Bath: 3/4"
  • Fixture Supply: 1/2"
