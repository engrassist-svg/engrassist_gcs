Overview

Water quality standards ensure that drinking water delivered to consumers is safe and free from harmful contaminants. The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) sets national standards under the Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA), while plumbing systems must be designed to maintain that quality.

Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA)

Enacted in 1974 and amended in 1986 and 1996, the SDWA is the principal federal law protecting public drinking water supplies throughout the United States.

  • Coverage: All public water systems serving 25+ people or 15+ connections
  • Standards: Sets Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) for 90+ contaminants
  • Enforcement: EPA oversight with state primacy options
  • Treatment: Requires appropriate treatment techniques

EPA Drinking Water Standards

Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs)

MCLs are the highest level of a contaminant allowed in drinking water. They are enforceable standards set as close to health goals as feasible.

Primary Contaminants of Concern

Lead in Drinking Water

Lead Health Risks

Lead is a toxic metal that can cause serious health problems, especially in children and pregnant women:

  • Children: Reduced IQ, learning disabilities, behavioral problems, slowed growth
  • Adults: Cardiovascular effects, decreased kidney function, reproductive problems
  • No Safe Level: There is no safe level of lead exposure

Lead Sources in Plumbing

  • Lead Service Lines: Pipes connecting water main to building (pre-1986)
  • Lead Solder: Used in copper pipe joints (banned 1986)
  • Brass Fittings: Older fittings may contain up to 8% lead
  • Bronze Components: Valves, meters, pumps with leaded bronze
  • Galvanized Pipe: Can accumulate lead from upstream sources

Corrosion Control

Water utilities must implement corrosion control to minimize lead/copper leaching:

  • pH Adjustment: Maintain pH 7.0-8.5 to reduce corrosivity
  • Alkalinity Treatment: Add chemicals to raise alkalinity
  • Phosphate Addition: Creates protective coating on pipes
  • Temperature Control: Hot water increases corrosion rates

Water Testing Requirements

Public Water Systems

Required Testing Frequency (varies by system size and contaminant):

  • Total Coliform: Monthly to daily, depending on population served
  • Lead & Copper: Every 6 months to 3 years, based on results
  • Nitrate: Quarterly to annually
  • Disinfectants: Continuous or daily
  • Disinfection Byproducts: Quarterly
  • Inorganic/Organic Chemicals: Every 3-9 years

Private Well Water

Recommended Testing Schedule:

  • Annual: Total coliform bacteria, nitrate, pH, total dissolved solids
  • Every 2-3 Years: VOCs, inorganic chemicals (if risk factors present)
  • When Noticed: Taste, odor, or color changes
  • Special Situations: After flooding, nearby land use changes, new baby in home

Building Plumbing Systems

Testing for New or Modified Plumbing:

  • Flushing: Flush all new piping thoroughly before use
  • Disinfection: Chlorinate new water mains and piping
  • Bacteriological Testing: Sample after disinfection, before use
  • Lead Testing: Recommended in schools, daycare, healthcare facilities
  • Temperature Testing: Verify mixing valves deliver safe temperatures

Lead and Copper Rule

The Lead and Copper Rule (LCR) protects public health by minimizing lead and copper levels in drinking water. The rule was revised in 2021 with stricter requirements.

Key Provisions

  • Action Levels: Lead: 15 ppb, Copper: 1.3 ppm
  • Testing: First-draw samples from high-risk locations
  • Lead Service Line Inventory: Required by October 2024
  • Lead Service Line Replacement: Mandatory if action level exceeded
  • Public Education: Required when action levels exceeded
  • Schools and Daycare: Additional testing and notification requirements

NSF/ANSI 61 Compliance

NSF/ANSI Standard 61 establishes requirements for drinking water system components to ensure they do not leach harmful levels of contaminants into drinking water.

NSF 61 Covered Products

Pipes and Fittings

  • Copper pipe and fittings
  • PEX tubing and fittings
  • CPVC pipe and fittings
  • Brass components

Plumbing Devices

  • Valves and valve components
  • Meters
  • Backflow preventers
  • Water treatment devices

Joining Materials

  • Solder and flux
  • Solvent cements
  • Gaskets and O-rings
  • Thread sealants

Water Treatment

  • Filters and filter media
  • Softener resins
  • Treatment chemicals
  • UV disinfection units

Cross-Connection Control

Cross-connection control prevents contamination of potable water through backflow from non-potable sources.

Types of Backflow

Backsiphonage

Occurs when negative pressure in supply line draws contaminated water backward into potable supply.

Causes: Water main breaks, heavy water use (firefighting), undersized piping

Backpressure

Occurs when downstream pressure exceeds supply pressure, forcing water backward.

Causes: Booster pumps, thermal expansion, elevated tanks

Backflow Prevention Devices

Device Protection Level Applications
Air Gap (AG) Maximum (High Hazard) Waste water systems, chemical injection, all high hazards
Reduced Pressure Zone (RPZ) High (Health Hazard) Fire sprinklers, irrigation, chemical systems
Double Check Valve Assembly (DCVA) Medium (Non-Health Hazard) Commercial buildings, fire protection, irrigation
Atmospheric Vacuum Breaker (AVB) Low (Backsiphonage Only) Hose bibbs, lawn irrigation
Pressure Vacuum Breaker (PVB) Low (Backsiphonage Only) Irrigation systems, lawn sprinklers

Testing and Maintenance

Backflow prevention devices must be tested annually by certified testers. Failures must be repaired immediately and devices re-tested.
