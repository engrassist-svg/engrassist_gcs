Important Notes

This calculator provides preliminary estimates for stormwater basin sizing using simplified methods. Actual detention/retention design requires detailed hydrologic and hydraulic analysis including routing calculations, stage-storage-discharge relationships, and compliance with local stormwater regulations. Basin design must account for water quality requirements, emergency overflow, maintenance access, and safety features. Always consult a licensed professional engineer for stormwater management facility design.

Understanding Stormwater Retention and Detention

Retention vs. Detention

Retention Basin

Permanent Pool: Maintains water level between storms. Water leaves through infiltration and evaporation only (no surface discharge).

  • Also called retention pond or wet pond
  • Provides water quality treatment
  • Creates wildlife habitat
  • Requires deeper excavation for permanent pool
  • May require liner if infiltration is not desired

Detention Basin

Temporary Storage: Captures stormwater and releases it slowly through controlled outlet. Basin empties between storms.

  • Also called detention pond or dry pond
  • Reduces peak discharge rates
  • Dry between storms (can be used for recreation)
  • Less water quality treatment than retention
  • Lower maintenance than wet ponds

Why Detention/Retention is Required

  • Peak Flow Control: Development increases runoff - detention reduces peak discharge to pre-development rates
  • Downstream Protection: Prevents flooding and erosion in downstream drainage systems
  • Water Quality: Settles suspended solids and pollutants before discharge
  • Regulatory Compliance: Required by MS4 permits, local ordinances, and state regulations

Volume Calculation Methods

Simplified Method (Volumetric)

V = (C × I × A × T) - (Q × T)

Where:

  • V = Required storage volume (cubic feet)
  • C = Runoff coefficient
  • I = Rainfall intensity (in/hr)
  • A = Drainage area (acres)
  • T = Storm duration (hours)
  • Q = Release rate (CFS)

Rational Method

Estimates peak runoff for small watersheds. Used for preliminary detention sizing.

Hydrograph Routing (Detailed Design)

Computer modeling that routes full storm hydrograph through basin using stage-storage-discharge relationships. Required for final design.

Design Requirements

  • Outlet Structure: Orifice, weir, or restricted pipe to control release rate
  • Emergency Overflow: Spillway to safely pass flows exceeding design storm
  • Side Slopes: Typically 4:1 or flatter for safety and stability
  • Access: Maintenance access road to outlet and basin bottom
  • Fencing: May be required for safety if depth exceeds threshold
  • Vegetation: Grass, wetland plants, or erosion control

Water Quality Considerations

  • Water Quality Volume: First flush of runoff with highest pollutant concentration
  • Retention Time: Minimum 24-48 hours for settling
  • Depth Zones: Permanent pool 4-6 feet deep optimal for treatment
  • Forebay: Separate inlet area for sediment deposition
  • Vegetation: Wetland plants enhance treatment

Alternative BMPs

  • Bioretention: Landscaped depressions with engineered soil
  • Permeable Pavement: Allows infiltration through surface
  • Green Roofs: Vegetation on building roofs retains rainfall
  • Rain Gardens: Small-scale bioretention for residential
  • Underground Storage: Vaults or large pipes for space-constrained sites

Maintenance Requirements

Regular Maintenance (Quarterly)

  • Inspect inlet and outlet structures for debris
  • Remove trash and sediment from forebay
  • Mow grass areas and maintain vegetation
  • Check for erosion and repair promptly
  • Verify outlet is functioning properly

Annual Maintenance

  • Survey sediment accumulation
  • Inspect structural integrity of outlet and emergency spillway
  • Check side slope stability
  • Evaluate vegetation health and diversity
  • Update as-built drawings if modifications made

Long-Term Maintenance (Every 5-10 Years)

  • Dredge accumulated sediment (when depth reduced 25%)
  • Major vegetation restoration if needed
  • Repair or replace outlet structures
  • Reevaluate capacity vs. current watershed conditions

Professional Disclaimer

Stormwater detention and retention basin design is governed by local, state, and federal regulations. Requirements vary significantly by jurisdiction. This calculator provides very preliminary estimates only. Actual design requires detailed hydrologic modeling, hydraulic routing calculations, geotechnical investigation, water quality analysis, and compliance demonstration. Detention/retention basins are regulated structures that may be classified as dams requiring state engineer approval. Design must address public safety, long-term maintenance, and environmental protection. Always engage a licensed professional engineer for stormwater management facility design.
