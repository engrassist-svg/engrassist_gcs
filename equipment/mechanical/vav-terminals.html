Overview

Variable Air Volume (VAV) boxes, also called VAV terminal units or VAV dampers, are zone-level air distribution devices that regulate airflow to maintain comfortable conditions in individual spaces. They are the workhorses of modern commercial HVAC systems, providing independent temperature control for each zone while allowing the central air handling unit to operate more efficiently by reducing airflow during part-load conditions.

A typical VAV system might have dozens or even hundreds of VAV boxes, each serving a single zone (conference room, private office, open office area, etc.). Together, they create a flexible, energy-efficient HVAC system that adapts to changing loads throughout the building.

Common Applications

  • Office Buildings: Individual offices, conference rooms, open areas - ideal application
  • Schools: Classrooms, libraries, administrative areas
  • Hospitals: Patient rooms, offices, administrative (not critical care areas)
  • Retail: Individual shops in mall, different departments in department store
  • Hotels: Meeting rooms, back-of-house areas
  • Laboratories: Office areas, non-critical lab spaces

How It Works

VAV boxes receive constant-temperature supply air (typically 55¬∞F) from the central AHU and modulate the airflow to each zone based on cooling demand:

Basic VAV Operation

  1. Cooling Mode: Zone thermostat calls for cooling ‚Üí VAV box damper opens ‚Üí More cold air flows into space ‚Üí Space cools down
  2. Satisfied: Zone reaches setpoint ‚Üí Damper modulates to minimum position ‚Üí Minimum airflow maintains ventilation
  3. Heating Mode (with Reheat): Zone calls for heat ‚Üí Damper goes to minimum ‚Üí Reheat coil activates ‚Üí Warm air delivered to space

System Interaction

VAV boxes work in concert with the AHU:

  • As VAV boxes close, duct static pressure increases
  • Static pressure sensor signals AHU to slow supply fan (via VFD)
  • Fan energy reduces proportionally to cube of speed reduction
  • Result: Significant energy savings compared to constant volume systems (30-50% fan energy reduction typical)

Energy Impact

VAV systems save significant energy compared to constant volume:

  • Fan energy reduction: 30-50% (due to reduced airflow and static pressure)
  • Cooling energy reduction: 10-20% (less overcooling of zones)
  • However: Simultaneous heating and cooling (reheat) wastes energy
  • Solution: Reset supply air temperature up as loads decrease to minimize reheat

Types of VAV Terminal Units

Cooling-Only (Single-Duct)

Most basic type - provides cooling only, no heating capability.

Typical Min/Max: 30% / 100%

Best For: Interior zones with consistent cooling loads, mild climates

Operation: Modulates from minimum (ventilation) to maximum (cooling) airflow

Pros:
  • Lowest first cost
  • Simple operation
  • Minimal maintenance
Cons:
  • No heating capability
  • Limited to cooling-only zones

VAV Reheat (Hot Water)

Includes hot water heating coil downstream of damper for heating mode.

Typical Coil: 1-2 rows, 180¬∞F HW

Best For: Perimeter zones, spaces requiring year-round temperature control

Operation: Damper to minimum, then reheat coil activates for heating

Pros:
  • Efficient heating
  • Precise temperature control
  • Lower operating cost than electric
Cons:
  • Requires hot water piping to each box
  • More complex installation
  • Potential for coil freeze-up

VAV Reheat (Electric)

Uses electric resistance heating elements instead of hot water coil.

Typical Capacity: 1 - 20 kW

Best For: When hot water isn't available, small heating loads

Operation: Damper to minimum, electric elements activate in stages

Pros:
  • No piping required
  • Simple installation
  • Precise control
  • No freeze-up risk
Cons:
  • Expensive to operate
  • High electrical demand
  • Not suitable for large heating loads

Series Fan-Powered VAV

Includes small fan that runs continuously, mixes return plenum air with primary air.

Fan Operation: Continuous

Best For: Perimeter zones, long duct runs, better air distribution

Operation: Fan runs continuously; as primary damper closes, fan draws more return air

Pros:
  • Better air circulation
  • Reduced primary air requirements
  • Warmer air in heating mode
Cons:
  • Continuous fan energy
  • More noise
  • More maintenance (filters, fan)

Parallel Fan-Powered VAV

Fan operates only during heating mode or when primary airflow is insufficient.

Fan Operation: Intermittent

Best For: When continuous fan operation isn't needed

Operation: Primary air during cooling; fan + reheat during heating

Pros:
  • Lower fan energy than series
  • Maintains air circulation during heating
  • Quieter during cooling mode
Cons:
  • More complex controls
  • Higher first cost

Dual-Duct VAV

Two dampers mix hot and cold air streams to achieve setpoint.

Air Streams: Hot & Cold

Best For: Existing dual-duct systems, spaces requiring immediate mode changes

Operation: Modulates hot and cold dampers to mix air to desired temperature

Pros:
  • Rapid temperature response
  • No reheat coil needed
Cons:
  • Energy-intensive
  • Requires dual ductwork
  • Rare in new construction

Control Types

Control Type Description Advantages Disadvantages Best Use
Pressure Dependent (Mechanical) Damper position directly controlled by thermostat - airflow varies with duct pressure Simple, low cost, reliable Airflow not precisely controlled, affected by system pressure changes Small systems, low-budget projects
Pressure Independent (DDC) Measures actual airflow and modulates damper to maintain setpoint regardless of pressure Precise airflow control, better comfort, easier balancing Higher cost, requires airflow sensor and controller Most modern commercial buildings - now standard

Sizing Guidelines

  • Maximum CFM: Size for calculated cooling load, don't just use area √ó 1 CFM/SF
  • Minimum CFM: Greater of:
    • Ventilation requirement per ASHRAE 62.1
    • 30% of maximum for air circulation
    • Minimum needed to maintain duct velocity (prevent stratification)
  • Turndown Ratio: Typical 3:1 to 5:1 (300-500 CFM max = 100 CFM min)
  • Sizing Tip: Don't oversize! Oversized boxes have poor control at low loads

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Size VAV boxes based on actual load calculations, not rules of thumb
  • Specify pressure-independent controls for all but the simplest systems
  • Locate boxes in accessible ceiling spaces - NOT above inaccessible areas
  • Avoid locating boxes directly above occupied workstations (noise)
  • Specify acoustic liner for boxes serving noise-sensitive spaces (conference rooms, private offices)
  • Don't forget to size reheat for actual heating load, not just "throw in a coil"
  • Plan for ceiling access panels at each VAV box for service

Installation

  • Install box level and support from structure (not ductwork)
  • Follow manufacturer's straight duct requirements (typically 3-5 diameters upstream)
  • Use flex duct connections to prevent vibration transmission
  • Wire controls before installing ceiling - easier access
  • Label each box clearly with zone number/name
  • Commission each box individually - verify min/max flows

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Oversizing: Leads to poor control at low loads and short-cycling
  • Insufficient minimum airflow: Causes poor ventilation and air circulation
  • Poor accessibility: Makes maintenance difficult and expensive
  • Ignoring acoustics: Results in noise complaints from occupants
  • Inadequate commissioning: Leads to comfort complaints and energy waste

Often Overlooked Considerations

  • Access for Service: VAV boxes require periodic service (filter changes on FP boxes, sensor calibration). Provide ceiling access panels within 10 feet of each box.
  • Minimum Flow and Ventilation: Energy codes and ASHRAE 62.1 have strict requirements. Can't just set minimum to 0% - need adequate ventilation air.
  • Acoustics: VAV boxes generate noise from damper and fan (FP boxes). Noise increases at high inlet pressures and partially-closed dampers. Specify acoustic liner for noise-sensitive areas.
  • BMS Integration: Ensure VAV controllers are compatible with building automation system (BACnet, Modbus, LonWorks)

Codes & Standards

Relevant Standards

  • ASHRAE 62.1: Ventilation for Acceptable Indoor Air Quality - Minimum ventilation requirements
  • ASHRAE 90.1: Energy Standard for Buildings - Energy efficiency requirements, reheat limitations
  • ASHRAE 111: Practices for Measurement, Testing, Adjusting, and Balancing of Building HVAC Systems
  • NFPA 90A: Standard for the Installation of Air-Conditioning and Ventilating Systems
  • IMC (International Mechanical Code): Installation and safety requirements

Key Code Requirements

  • Minimum Ventilation: Must meet ASHRAE 62.1 requirements for outdoor air per person and per area
  • Reheat Limitations: ASHRAE 90.1 limits use of reheat - exceptions for:
    • Zones with special humidity requirements
    • Systems with heat recovery
    • Zones with minimum airflow >30% of peak
  • Accessibility: Must be accessible for service and maintenance per IMC
  • Labeling: Each unit must be clearly labeled with zone identification
  • Fire/Smoke Dampers: May be required at penetrations through fire-rated assemblies

Industry Best Practices

  • Follow manufacturer's installation instructions for proper operation
  • Provide straight duct runs per manufacturer requirements (typically 3-5 diameters)
  • Commission all boxes individually to verify airflow accuracy
  • Implement supply air temperature reset to minimize reheat energy
  • Integrate with building automation system for monitoring and optimization

System Integration Considerations

  • Static Pressure Control: AHU must maintain proper duct static pressure (typically 1.0-1.5" w.c.) for VAV boxes to operate correctly
  • Supply Air Temperature: Coordinate with AHU design - typically 55¬∞F, but may vary based on system design
  • Reheat Source: If using hot water reheat, coordinate with boiler/heat pump sizing
  • Controls Integration: Ensure VAV controllers are compatible with BAS protocol (BACnet, Modbus, etc.)
  • Sequence of Operations: Coordinate VAV box control with AHU supply air temp reset and static pressure reset

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Zone Too Cold

Possible Causes:

  • Minimum airflow set too high
  • Damper stuck open
  • Supply air too cold
  • Reheat not working

Solutions:

  • Check actual airflow vs. setpoint
  • Verify damper position
  • Check reheat valve/element operation
  • Adjust minimum flow setpoint

Zone Too Warm

Possible Causes:

  • Damper stuck closed
  • Maximum flow too low
  • Airflow sensor miscalibrated
  • Supply air not cold enough

Solutions:

  • Verify damper operation
  • Check actual vs. displayed airflow
  • Recalibrate airflow sensor
  • Increase max flow if undersized

Poor Air Circulation

Possible Causes:

  • Minimum flow too low
  • Diffusers poorly located
  • Return air short-circuiting

Solutions:

  • Increase minimum airflow (within code limits)
  • Check air pattern in space
  • Relocate diffusers or return grilles

Noisy Operation

Possible Causes:

  • Excessive inlet pressure
  • Damper flutter
  • Fan noise (fan-powered boxes)

Solutions:

  • Check inlet static pressure
  • Add acoustic liner
  • Increase minimum position to prevent flutter
  • Balance system static pressure
