Overview

Water-cooled chillers are refrigeration machines that remove heat from a building or process using water as the cooling medium in the condenser. Unlike air-cooled chillers, which reject heat directly to the ambient air, water-cooled chillers use a separate condenser water loop connected to a cooling tower or other heat rejection device. This configuration typically offers higher efficiency, quieter operation, and better performance in hot climates compared to air-cooled alternatives.

Water-cooled chillers are commonly found in large commercial buildings, industrial facilities, hospitals, data centers, and district cooling plants where cooling loads exceed 100-150 tons and where the added complexity of a cooling tower system can be justified by the energy savings and performance benefits.

How It Works

Water-cooled chillers operate on the vapor-compression refrigeration cycle, consisting of four main components:

The Refrigeration Cycle

  1. Evaporator: Chilled water from the building returns at approximately 54-58¬∞F and is cooled to 42-45¬∞F as refrigerant evaporates, absorbing heat from the water.
  2. Compressor: The compressor (centrifugal, screw, or scroll) increases the pressure and temperature of the refrigerant vapor.
  3. Condenser: Hot refrigerant vapor releases heat to condenser water (typically 85¬∞F entering, 95¬∞F leaving), condensing back to a liquid.
  4. Expansion Device: The high-pressure liquid refrigerant passes through an expansion valve, reducing pressure and temperature before returning to the evaporator.

The condenser water is then pumped to a cooling tower where the heat is rejected to the atmosphere through evaporation. This closed-loop system allows for very efficient heat rejection, especially in moderate climates where wet-bulb temperatures are low.

Types of Water-Cooled Chillers

1. Centrifugal Chillers

Capacity Range: 100 - 10,000+ tons

Centrifugal chillers use a centrifugal compressor with an impeller to compress refrigerant. They are the most efficient option for large cooling loads and are commonly used in central plants. Modern magnetic bearing centrifugal chillers offer exceptional efficiency with COP values of 6.0-8.0 or higher at full load and excellent part-load performance.

  • Advantages: Highest efficiency, quietest operation, low maintenance, excellent part-load performance
  • Disadvantages: Higher initial cost, minimum load limitations (typically 10-15%), complex controls
  • Common Refrigerants: R-134a, R-513A, R-1233zd, R-1234ze

2. Screw Chillers

Capacity Range: 50 - 1,500 tons

Screw chillers use twin-screw rotary compressors. They offer good efficiency and are well-suited for variable load applications. Screw chillers can handle lower loads better than centrifugal chillers and are often the choice for mid-sized applications.

  • Advantages: Good part-load efficiency, fewer moving parts than reciprocating, can handle low evaporator temperatures
  • Disadvantages: Slightly lower efficiency than centrifugal at large capacities, higher noise levels
  • Common Refrigerants: R-134a, R-513A

3. Scroll Chillers

Capacity Range: 10 - 200 tons

Scroll chillers use multiple scroll compressors and are ideal for smaller applications. They offer good efficiency and reliability with modular capacity control through compressor staging.

  • Advantages: Lower initial cost, simple maintenance, good reliability, modular capacity control
  • Disadvantages: Lower efficiency at larger capacities, more compressors means more potential failure points
  • Common Refrigerants: R-410A, R-134a

Applications and Uses

Water-cooled chillers are ideal for the following applications:

  • Large Commercial Buildings: Office towers, shopping malls, convention centers (>150 tons)
  • Healthcare Facilities: Hospitals and medical centers requiring high reliability and precise temperature control
  • Data Centers: High-density cooling loads with year-round operation
  • Industrial Process Cooling: Manufacturing facilities, pharmaceutical production, food processing
  • District Cooling: Campus settings serving multiple buildings
  • Hotels and Resorts: Large hospitality facilities with consistent cooling demands
  • Universities and Institutional Buildings: Central plants serving multiple academic buildings

Typical Configurations

Single Chiller Configuration

Smaller facilities (100-300 tons) may use a single chiller with standby capabilities or rental provisions. This is the most economical initial installation but provides no redundancy during maintenance or failure.

Multiple Chiller Arrays

Large facilities typically use 2-4 chillers in parallel to provide:

  • Redundancy - Operation continues if one chiller fails
  • Improved Part-Load Efficiency - Operating fewer chillers at higher loads is more efficient than one at low load
  • Maintenance Flexibility - Chillers can be serviced without shutting down the entire system
  • Future Expansion - Additional capacity can be added as needed

Common Chiller Staging Strategies

Equal Sizing: Multiple chillers of equal capacity (e.g., four 500-ton chillers for 2,000-ton load)

Lead-Lag: One smaller "lead" chiller handles base load with larger chillers for peak (e.g., 200-ton + 800-ton for 1,000-ton peak)

Diverse Sizing: Varied sizes for optimal part-load efficiency across different load conditions

Primary-Secondary Pumping vs. Variable Primary Flow

Primary-Secondary: Traditional design with dedicated constant-flow primary pumps on each chiller and variable-flow secondary distribution. Requires common pipe for hydraulic separation.

Variable Primary Flow: Modern design with variable-speed pumps directly on the chiller loop, eliminating the need for separate loops. Requires careful control to maintain minimum flow through operating chillers.

Sizing Guidelines

  • Safety Factor: Typically 10-15% above calculated peak load to account for uncertainties
  • Diversity Factor: For multiple tenants or zones, actual peak may be 70-85% of sum of individual peaks
  • Future Expansion: Consider sizing infrastructure (pipe, pumps, cooling tower) for future chiller additions
  • Minimum Load: Ensure chiller can operate efficiently at minimum expected load (typically 30-50% of peak)

Common Sizing Mistakes

  • Oversizing based on extreme conditions that rarely occur
  • Not accounting for improved building envelope or equipment efficiency
  • Ignoring part-load operation (where the chiller operates 95% of the time)
  • Forgetting to account for heat gains from pumps, fans, and lighting improvements

Available Options

Efficiency Enhancements

  • Variable Speed Drives: On compressor for improved part-load efficiency (standard on most modern centrifugal chillers)
  • Magnetic Bearings: Eliminate oil system, reduce friction, improve efficiency and reliability
  • Heat Recovery: Capture condenser heat for domestic hot water or space heating
  • Free Cooling (Waterside Economizer): Bypass chiller during cold weather and use cooling tower directly

Control Options

  • Building Management System (BMS) Integration: BACnet, Modbus, or proprietary protocols
  • Plant Optimization Software: Coordinates chillers, pumps, and cooling towers for optimal plant efficiency
  • Demand Limiting: Automatically reduces chiller load to stay within utility demand targets
  • Remote Monitoring: Cloud-based monitoring and diagnostics

Physical Options

  • Sound Attenuation: Additional insulation and vibration isolation for noise-sensitive locations
  • Marine-Grade Coatings: For coastal installations or corrosive environments
  • Seismic Certification: Required in high-seismic zones
  • Dual Refrigerant Circuits: Provides 50% redundancy if one circuit fails

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Perform detailed load calculations; avoid using rules of thumb like "400 SF/ton"
  • Consider load profile and part-load operation when selecting chiller type
  • Coordinate chiller, pump, and cooling tower selection for optimal system efficiency
  • Plan for adequate clearance for tube pulling (typically length of tube bundle + 3-4 feet)
  • Ensure adequate structural support - chillers with water can weigh 15-25 lb/ton
  • Specify VFDs on condenser water pumps for improved system efficiency
  • Include water treatment system from day one

Installation

  • Verify rigging path and reinforcement before equipment arrives
  • Install vibration isolation under chiller and on all piping connections
  • Ensure proper oil return to compressor (pitch horizontal suction lines)
  • Install flow switches on both chilled and condenser water circuits
  • Provide adequate freeze protection if chiller may operate below 35¬∞F ambient
  • Install pressure gauges and thermometers for troubleshooting

Operation & Maintenance

  • Maintain water treatment program to prevent scaling, corrosion, and biological growth
  • Log operating parameters daily (temps, pressures, kW) to identify trends
  • Clean condenser tubes annually (or more frequently in harsh water conditions)
  • Check oil level and condition quarterly (screw/scroll chillers)
  • Verify refrigerant charge annually; even small leaks impact efficiency
  • Reset chilled water temperature based on load to improve efficiency
  • Stage chillers to optimize plant efficiency, not just "first on, first off"

Often Overlooked Considerations

Physical Dimensions and Weight

Water-cooled chillers are large and heavy. A 500-ton centrifugal chiller might be 20' long √ó 8' wide √ó 10' tall and weigh 25,000-35,000 lbs when shipped (more with water). Consider:

  • Can it fit through doors, down ramps, around corners?
  • Will it fit in the elevator, or does the crane need to lift it to the roof?
  • Is structural reinforcement required?
  • Is there sufficient tube-pull clearance for maintenance?

Service and Maintenance Access

  • Tube Bundle Removal: Requires clear space equal to bundle length plus 3-4 feet
  • Control Panel Access: Typically 3-4 feet clearance needed
  • Compressor Service: Some chillers require overhead clearance for motor or bearing removal
  • Refrigerant Recovery: Space for recovery equipment and cylinders during service

Costs vary significantly based on efficiency, options, and market conditions:

  • Scroll Chillers (50-100 tons): $500-700/ton
  • Screw Chillers (100-500 tons): $400-600/ton
  • Centrifugal Chillers (500+ tons): $350-550/ton

Energy Costs

Operating costs often exceed equipment costs over the life of the chiller. A 500-ton chiller operating 2,000 hours/year at $0.12/kWh:

  • Standard Efficiency (0.60 kW/ton): $72,000/year
  • High Efficiency (0.45 kW/ton): $54,000/year
  • Savings: $18,000/year, $360,000 over 20-year life

Water Quality and Treatment

Often underestimated, poor water treatment is the #1 cause of premature chiller failure:

  • Budget 5-10% of chiller cost for water treatment system
  • Plan for regular water testing (monthly minimum)
  • Chemical treatment costs typically $2,000-5,000/year for a 500-ton system
  • One tube bundle replacement can cost $50,000-150,000

Sound Levels

While quieter than air-cooled chillers, water-cooled chillers still generate noise:

  • Centrifugal: 75-85 dBA at 10 feet
  • Screw: 80-90 dBA at 10 feet
  • Scroll: 75-85 dBA at 10 feet
  • Add sound attenuation package if near occupied spaces or property lines

Chiller Troubleshooting

Common Chiller Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
Low Leaving Water Temperature Fouled tubes, low flow, low load, refrigerant undercharge Check approach temperature, measure flow rate, inspect tubes, verify refrigerant charge Clean tubes, repair flow issues, adjust load, recharge refrigerant
High Discharge Pressure Fouled condenser, high condenser water temp, non-condensables, refrigerant overcharge Check condenser approach, inspect tubes, check cooling tower, purge air Clean condenser, repair cooling tower, purge system, recover excess refrigerant
Low Suction Pressure Low load, refrigerant undercharge, expansion valve issue, evaporator fouling Check building load, verify charge, test TXV/EXV, inspect evaporator Recharge system, replace valve, clean evaporator
High Amp Draw High head pressure, mechanical issues, electrical problems Check all pressures, listen for abnormal sounds, verify voltage/phase balance Address pressure issues, repair mechanical problems, fix electrical supply
Frequent Starts/Stops Oversized unit, low load, control issues, flow switch issues Review load calculations, check minimum run timers, verify flow rates Adjust controls, add thermal storage, repair flow issues
Oil Level Issues Oil migration, oil foaming, refrigerant in oil, oil return problems Check sight glass, measure oil temperature, verify oil separator operation Add oil, repair oil return system, reduce refrigerant migration

Critical Chiller Measurements

  • Evaporator Approach: Leaving water temp - refrigerant evaporating temp (typically 2-4¬∞F)
  • Condenser Approach: Refrigerant condensing temp - entering water temp (typically 2-6¬∞F)
  • Superheat: Suction temp - evaporating temp (typically 8-12¬∞F)
  • Subcooling: Condensing temp - liquid line temp (typically 5-10¬∞F)
  • Delta T: Entering - leaving water temp (typically 10-12¬∞F design)

Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

Step 1: Define the Problem

  • What is the specific symptom or complaint?
  • When did the problem start?
  • Has anything changed recently (setpoints, equipment, weather)?
  • Is the problem continuous or intermittent?
  • Which zones or systems are affected?

Step 2: Gather Data

  • Review BMS/BAS trending data and alarms
  • Check equipment status indicators and gauges
  • Measure temperatures, pressures, flows, and electrical parameters
  • Review maintenance records and equipment logs
  • Obtain design documents and sequence of operations

Step 3: Analyze and Diagnose

  • Compare actual values to design values
  • Identify which systems/components are functioning correctly
  • Isolate the problem to a specific subsystem or component
  • Develop hypotheses for root cause
  • Test each hypothesis systematically

Safety Considerations

Always follow these safety procedures when troubleshooting chiller equipment:

  • Follow lockout/tagout (LOTO) procedures for all energy sources
  • Wear appropriate PPE: safety glasses, work boots, gloves
  • Never open refrigerant systems under pressure
  • Ensure proper ventilation when working with refrigerants
  • Use proper refrigerant handling equipment
  • Be aware of electrical hazards - high voltage present
  • Never bypass safety controls

Preventive Maintenance for Chillers

Monthly Tasks

  • Check for unusual noise, vibration, or leaks
  • Verify control operation and setpoints
  • Review BAS alarms and trending
  • Check refrigerant charge indicators
  • Inspect water treatment levels

Quarterly Tasks

  • Test water treatment and adjust chemicals
  • Check oil levels (screw/scroll chillers)
  • Inspect for corrosion or scale buildup
  • Test flow switches and sensors
  • Clean or replace air filters if applicable

Annual Tasks

  • Clean condenser and evaporator tubes
  • Test refrigerant charge and leak check
  • Inspect electrical connections
  • Test all safety controls and interlocks
  • Verify compressor oil condition
  • Full system commissioning check

Water-Cooled vs Air-Cooled

Water-cooled chillers require cooling towers and condenser water pumps, but offer significantly higher efficiency. Air-cooled chillers are easier to install but consume 25-40% more energy. Consider local climate, water availability, and long-term operating costs when selecting chiller type.
