About Fixture Units

Water Supply Fixture Units (WSFU) are standardized values that represent the probable water demand of plumbing fixtures. The conversion to GPM uses Hunter's Curve, which accounts for the probability that not all fixtures will be used simultaneously.

Understanding Fixture Units

What are Water Supply Fixture Units (WSFU)?

Water Supply Fixture Units (WSFU) are dimensionless values assigned to plumbing fixtures to represent their probable water demand. Developed by Roy B. Hunter in 1940, the WSFU system allows engineers to size water distribution systems based on probable simultaneous use rather than assuming all fixtures operate at once.

WSFU Values Account For:

  • Flow Rate: GPM demanded by each fixture type
  • Duration: How long fixtures typically run
  • Frequency: How often fixtures are used
  • Diversity: Probability of simultaneous use

Standard WSFU Values (IPC/UPC)

  • Water Closet (Tank): 2.5 WSFU (cold only)
  • Water Closet (Flush Valve): 6.0 WSFU (cold only)
  • Lavatory: 0.5 WSFU (hot and cold separately)
  • Shower: 1.0 WSFU (hot and cold separately)
  • Bathtub: 1.0 WSFU (hot and cold separately)
  • Kitchen Sink: 0.75 WSFU (hot and cold separately)
  • Dishwasher: 1.4 WSFU (hot only)
  • Clothes Washer: 1.0 WSFU (hot and cold separately)
  • Hose Bibb: 2.5 WSFU (cold only)

Hunter's Curve: Converting WSFU to GPM

Hunter's Curve is an empirical relationship that converts total fixture units to probable flow rate. The relationship is non-linear because the probability of simultaneous use decreases as more fixtures are added to a system.

Approximate WSFU to GPM Conversion:

  • 1-5 WSFU ≈ 3-7 GPM
  • 10 WSFU ≈ 8-9 GPM
  • 20 WSFU ≈ 14-15 GPM
  • 30 WSFU ≈ 20-21 GPM
  • 50 WSFU ≈ 30 GPM
  • 100 WSFU ≈ 50 GPM
  • 200 WSFU ≈ 77 GPM

Hot vs. Cold Water Fixture Units

Fixtures are assigned separate WSFU values for hot and cold water. Some fixtures use only cold water (toilets, hose bibbs), while others use both hot and cold (sinks, showers).

Important Design Considerations

  • Size hot and cold water systems separately based on their respective WSFU totals
  • Hot water systems typically have lower total WSFU than cold water
  • Recirculation systems should be considered for long hot water runs
  • Modern low-flow fixtures may result in conservative sizing with traditional WSFU values

Fixture Unit Troubleshooting

Common Issues When Using Fixture Units

  • Undersizing: Not accounting for all fixtures or future expansion
  • Oversizing: Using conservative WSFU values with modern low-flow fixtures
  • Missing Fixtures: Forgetting utility sinks, janitor closets, outdoor faucets
  • Wrong Values: Using drainage fixture units (DFU) instead of WSFU

Best Practices

  • Create detailed fixture schedule before calculating WSFU
  • Account for future expansion in commercial buildings
  • Verify WSFU values match adopted plumbing code
  • Calculate hot and cold water WSFU separately
  • Consider actual fixture flow rates for final verification
