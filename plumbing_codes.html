Overview of Plumbing Codes

Plumbing codes establish minimum requirements for the design, installation, and inspection of plumbing systems to protect public health and safety. In the United States, model plumbing codes are developed by standards organizations and adopted (often with amendments) by state and local jurisdictions.

Purpose of Plumbing Codes

  • Public Health Protection: Ensure safe drinking water and proper waste disposal
  • Safety: Prevent hazards from gas leaks, scalding, and contamination
  • Standardization: Provide consistent requirements across jurisdictions
  • Quality Assurance: Establish minimum performance standards for materials and installation

International Plumbing Code (IPC)

The International Plumbing Code is developed and published by the International Code Council (ICC), a membership association dedicated to building safety and fire prevention. The IPC is updated on a three-year cycle and is the most widely adopted plumbing code in the United States.

IPC Key Chapters

Chapter 1: Administration

Scope, application, and administrative procedures for code enforcement

Chapter 2: Definitions

Technical terms used throughout the code

Chapter 3: General Regulations

Materials, alternates, repairs, and general requirements

Chapter 4: Fixtures & Faucets

Minimum number, access, and installation of plumbing fixtures

Chapter 5: Water Heaters

General provisions, connections, safety devices, and temperature controls

Chapter 6: Water Supply

Water service, distribution, protection from contamination

Chapter 7: Sanitary Drainage

Materials, joints, connections, sizing, and installation of drainage systems

Chapter 9: Vents

Vent system design, sizing, termination, and relief

Chapter 10: Traps & Interceptors

Trap requirements, seal protection, and specialty interceptors

Chapter 11: Storm Drainage

Roof drainage, area drains, sizing of storm systems

Chapter 12: Special Systems

Fuel gas, medical gas, specialty piping, and solar systems

Appendix E: Sizing Methods

Water supply sizing using fixture units (Hunter's Curve)

IPC Adoption

States that primarily use IPC: Most states east of the Mississippi River, plus Alaska, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, North and South Dakota, Kansas, Oklahoma, and Texas

Current Version: 2024 IPC (published 2023)

Publisher: International Code Council (ICC) - www.iccsafe.org

Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC)

The Uniform Plumbing Code is published by the International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials (IAPMO). The UPC is updated on a three-year cycle and is widely adopted in western states.

UPC Key Chapters

Chapter 1: Administration

Code adoption, permits, inspections, and violations

Chapter 3: General Regulations

Workmanship, conflicts, alternates, materials, and tests

Chapter 4: Plumbing Fixtures

Minimum facilities, access, materials, and installation

Chapter 5: Water Heaters

Installation, safety devices, relief valves, and seismic restraint

Chapter 6: Water Supply

Protection, sizing, materials, hangers, and expansion

Chapter 7: Sanitary Drainage

Drainage system design, materials, and installation

Chapter 9: Vent Systems

Types of vents, sizing, materials, and installation

Chapter 10: Traps & Interceptors

Trap requirements, grease interceptors, oil separators

Chapter 11: Storm Drainage

Roof drains, sizing, materials, and secondary drainage

Chapter 12: Fuel Gas Piping

Materials, sizing, installation of natural and LP gas systems

Chapter 13: Health Care

Medical gas, clinical sinks, and specialty systems

Appendix A: Fixture Units

Sizing water supply and drainage using fixture unit method

UPC Adoption

States that primarily use UPC: California, Washington, Oregon, Nevada, Arizona, New Mexico, Minnesota, and others

Current Version: 2024 UPC (published 2023)

Publisher: IAPMO - www.iapmo.org

National Standard Plumbing Code (NSPC)

The National Standard Plumbing Code, developed by the Plumbing-Heating-Cooling Contractors Association (PHCC), is less widely adopted but still used in some jurisdictions, particularly in the mid-Atlantic region.

NSPC Details

Primary Use: New Jersey, parts of Pennsylvania, and select jurisdictions

Publisher: PHCC - www.phccweb.org

Note: Many NSPC jurisdictions are transitioning to IPC or UPC

Local Code Amendments

Most jurisdictions adopt a model code (IPC, UPC, or NSPC) but make local amendments to address specific regional concerns such as climate, water availability, seismic activity, or local practices.

Always Check Local Requirements

Common Local Amendments Include:

  • Water Conservation: Stricter fixture flow rate limits, mandatory dual-flush toilets
  • Seismic Requirements: Bracing and restraint of equipment and piping (California, Pacific Northwest)
  • Freeze Protection: Minimum depth of burial, insulation requirements (cold climates)
  • Backflow Prevention: Additional requirements for cross-connection control
  • Materials: Prohibitions or allowances for specific pipe materials
  • Permit Requirements: Varying thresholds for when permits are required
  • Inspection Protocols: Number and timing of required inspections

How to Find Local Requirements: Contact your local building department or visit their website. Most jurisdictions publish adopted codes and amendments online.

Key Code Requirements Summary

Water Supply Systems

  • Minimum Pressure: 15-20 psi at fixtures (IPC/UPC)
  • Maximum Pressure: 80 psi (PRV required if exceeded)
  • Backflow Prevention: Required at all cross-connections
  • Hot Water Temperature: Maximum 120Â°F at fixtures accessible to public (anti-scald)
  • Pipe Sizing: Based on fixture unit method or approved calculations
  • Support: Specified intervals by material (e.g., copper: 6 ft horizontal)
  • Testing: Hydrostatic test at 150% working pressure minimum

Drainage Systems

  • Minimum Slope: 1/4" per foot for pipes under 3", 1/8" for 3" and larger
  • Trap Seal: 2-4 inch water seal required on all fixtures
  • Cleanouts: Required at specified intervals and locations
  • Materials: Must be approved for DWV (drain-waste-vent) service
  • Testing: Water or air test to verify leak-free installation
  • Horizontal to Vertical: Connections must use approved fittings (long sweep)

Vent Systems

  • Every Trap Must Be Vented: Individual, common, or circuit vents allowed
  • Vent Termination: Extend through roof, minimum 6" above roof surface
  • Vent Size: Minimum 1-1/4" diameter (some codes allow 1-1/2" minimum)
  • Horizontal Vents: Must be above fixture flood level rim
  • Protection from Freezing: Terminate in heated space or insulate in cold climates

Fixture Requirements

  • Minimum Fixtures: Based on occupancy and number of occupants
  • Accessibility: ADA compliance required in public accommodations
  • Water Closets: Maximum 1.6 gpf (gallons per flush) - many jurisdictions require 1.28 gpf
  • Showerheads: Maximum 2.5 gpm at 80 psi (some jurisdictions require 2.0 gpm or less)
  • Lavatories: Maximum 2.2 gpm (public) or 1.5 gpm (residential)
  • Materials: Smooth, non-absorbent surfaces required
