Sizing Guidelines & Rules of Thumb

Airflow Requirements by Space Type

  • Private Offices: 1.0-1.5 CFM/sq ft (low occupancy)
  • General Offices: 1.2-1.8 CFM/sq ft (moderate occupancy)
  • Conference Rooms: 2.0-3.0 CFM/sq ft (high occupancy, intermittent)
  • Classrooms: 1.5-2.0 CFM/sq ft (moderate to high occupancy)
  • Laboratories: 4.0-8.0 CFM/sq ft (high ventilation requirements)
  • Retail Stores: 1.2-1.8 CFM/sq ft (variable occupancy)
  • Restaurants: 2.5-4.0 CFM/sq ft (high occupancy and cooking loads)
  • Hospital Patient Rooms: 2.0-3.0 CFM/sq ft (infection control)

Minimum Airflow Guidelines

  • Ventilation Requirements: Per ASHRAE 62.1 - typically 5-15 CFM/person
  • Turndown Ratio: 30-50% of maximum for most applications
  • Air Distribution: Below 30% turndown, air distribution may suffer
  • Reheat Applications: Higher minimums (40-50%) to prevent overcooling

Overview

Variable Air Volume (VAV) terminal units, commonly called VAV boxes, are air distribution devices that regulate the volume of conditioned air delivered to a space based on heating or cooling demand. Unlike constant volume systems that vary air temperature, VAV systems vary airflow while maintaining a relatively constant supply air temperature (typically 55¬∞F). This approach provides superior energy efficiency, better zone control, and improved comfort compared to constant volume systems.

VAV boxes are the workhorses of modern commercial HVAC systems, found in office buildings, schools, hospitals, hotels, and virtually every type of commercial facility. A typical building may have dozens to hundreds of VAV boxes, each serving an individual zone or room.

How It Works

A VAV terminal unit operates on a simple but elegant principle:

Basic Operation

  1. Airflow Measurement: An airflow sensor (typically a velocity pressure sensor) measures the airflow entering the box.
  2. Zone Thermostat: A thermostat in the zone measures space temperature and compares it to setpoint.
  3. Damper Modulation: Based on the temperature signal, the controller modulates a damper to increase or decrease airflow to the space.
  4. Cooling Mode: When the space needs cooling, the damper opens to increase cold airflow. When satisfied, it closes to minimum position.
  5. Heating Mode (if equipped): When heating is required, airflow typically remains at minimum while reheat (electric, hot water, or steam) is activated.

Types of VAV Boxes

1. Single Duct - Cooling Only

Applications: Interior zones with year-round cooling loads

The simplest VAV configuration. Provides cooling only by modulating cold air from maximum to minimum flow. No heating capability. Used in spaces that never require heating, such as interior zones of large buildings where heat from lights, people, and equipment provides adequate warmth even in winter.

  • Advantages: Lowest cost, simple installation, minimal maintenance
  • Disadvantages: No heating capability, limited to interior zones
  • Typical Sizes: 100-4,000 CFM

2. Single Duct with Reheat

Applications: Perimeter zones requiring both heating and cooling

Adds a heating coil (electric, hot water, or steam) to provide heating when needed. The most common VAV type for perimeter zones. During cooling, operates like a cooling-only box. During heating, airflow goes to minimum while the reheat coil activates to warm the air.

  • Electric Reheat: Simplest installation, no piping required, but highest operating cost. Typically limited to smaller boxes (<1,500 CFM) due to electrical load.
  • Hot Water Reheat: Most common. Requires hot water piping but lower operating cost than electric. Typical entering water: 180¬∞F, leaving: 160¬∞F.
  • Steam Reheat: Rapid response, used in hospitals and labs. Requires steam piping and condensate return.

3. Fan Powered - Parallel Flow

Applications: Perimeter zones requiring increased air circulation and better heating

Adds a small fan that draws plenum air and mixes it with primary air. The fan runs only during heating or when additional air circulation is needed. Called "parallel" because the fan operates in parallel with the primary airflow path.

  • Advantages: Better air distribution, constant air circulation during heating, can use plenum air as "free" heating
  • Disadvantages: Higher first cost, fan energy consumption, more maintenance (fan motor)
  • Fan Operation: Modern units use ECM motors for energy efficiency, typically drawing 0.3-0.8 watts/CFM

4. Fan Powered - Series Flow

Applications: Zones requiring constant airflow and ventilation

The fan runs continuously, providing constant airflow to the space. Primary air modulates from maximum to minimum, with the fan making up the difference by drawing plenum air. Called "series" because all air passes through the fan.

  • Advantages: Constant air motion and ventilation, excellent for perimeter zones, good mixing
  • Disadvantages: Highest energy consumption (fan runs continuously), highest first cost
  • Energy Use: ECM motors critical for this application to minimize fan energy penalty

5. Dual Duct

Applications: Zones with simultaneous heating and cooling needs, or highly variable loads

Two dampers control cold air and warm air streams that mix in the box before delivery to the space. Provides excellent control but at the cost of energy efficiency (simultaneous heating and cooling).

  • Advantages: Precise temperature control, handles widely varying loads
  • Disadvantages: Expensive installation (two duct systems), energy inefficient, rarely used in new construction

6. Bypass VAV

Applications: Retrofit applications where constant air handler flow is required

Instead of reducing airflow to the space, excess air is bypassed to the ceiling plenum. Maintains constant flow through the air handler, which is sometimes required for older equipment.

  • Advantages: Allows VAV operation with constant volume equipment
  • Disadvantages: No fan energy savings, dumps conditioned air to plenum, inefficient

Control Types

Pressure Dependent vs. Pressure Independent

Pressure Dependent

The damper position is controlled solely by the zone thermostat. Airflow varies with both damper position AND inlet duct pressure. If duct pressure increases (e.g., other boxes close), this box will get more air even at the same damper position.

  • Advantages: Lower cost, simpler
  • Disadvantages: Poor control when duct pressure varies, requires careful system balancing
  • Applications: Small systems with stable pressures, budget-constrained projects

Pressure Independent (PI)

Includes a flow sensor and controller that maintains desired airflow regardless of inlet pressure variations. The controller continuously adjusts the damper to deliver the exact CFM called for by the thermostat.

  • Advantages: Accurate airflow control, stable operation, easier to balance, better for large systems
  • Disadvantages: Higher cost (~$100-200 more per box)
  • Applications: Standard for modern commercial buildings, required for LEED points

Applications and Uses

VAV systems are ideal for the following applications:

  • Office Buildings: Most common application. Each office, conference room, and open area has its own VAV box for independent control.
  • Schools and Universities: Each classroom gets a VAV box. Energy savings significant due to unoccupied periods.
  • Hospitals: Patient rooms, offices, and common areas. Critical areas (ORs, isolation rooms) may use other systems.
  • Hotels: Each guest room typically has a small VAV box (150-400 CFM). Meeting rooms and common areas have larger boxes.
  • Retail: Sales floors divided into zones. Fitting rooms often have dedicated boxes for comfort.
  • Laboratories: Each lab space has VAV boxes coordinated with fume hood exhaust for proper pressure control.
  • Data Centers: Specialized high-capacity VAV boxes for server room cooling with precise control.

Sizing Guidelines

  • Don't Oversize: Oversized boxes operate at low velocities where control is poor and sensors are inaccurate. Select closest standard size above calculated maximum.
  • Minimum Airflow: Must meet the greater of: (1) ASHRAE 62.1 ventilation requirements, or (2) 30% of maximum for proper air distribution.
  • Inlet Sizing: Size inlet duct for 800-1,200 FPM at maximum airflow. This ensures adequate sensor accuracy.
  • Standard Sizes: Most manufacturers offer boxes in increments: 100, 150, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 800, 1000, 1250, 1500, 2000, 2500, 3000, 4000, 5000 CFM.

Common Sizing Mistakes

  • Selecting boxes based on room area √ó CFM/sq ft without considering actual cooling load
  • Oversizing "to be safe" - results in poor control and noise issues
  • Not considering minimum ventilation requirements per ASHRAE 62.1
  • Ignoring acoustic requirements - selecting a box that meets airflow but exceeds NC limits
  • Not coordinating inlet duct size with box inlet size - causes turbulence and noise

Available Options

Control and Monitoring

  • BACnet/Modbus Integration: Connect to building automation system for centralized monitoring and control
  • Airflow Monitoring: Display or transmit actual CFM to BAS for verification and trending
  • CO2-Based Demand Control Ventilation: Adjust minimum airflow based on occupancy (CO2 levels)
  • Occupancy Sensors: Reduce airflow to unoccupied setback when space is vacant

Acoustic Options

  • Sound Attenuators: Internal or external silencers to meet stringent NC requirements (NC-25 or lower)
  • Low-Velocity Inlets: Larger inlet connections to reduce velocity and noise
  • Discharge Silencers: For critical applications like recording studios or patient rooms adjacent to boxes

Physical Options

  • Inlet Configurations: Round, rectangular, top, side, end - to match duct arrangement
  • Discharge Configurations: Round, rectangular, boots for flex duct connection
  • Access Panels: For easy maintenance and coil cleaning
  • Insulation: Internal or external insulation to prevent condensation in high-humidity applications

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Perform room-by-room cooling load calculation - don't rely solely on area-based estimates
  • Calculate ventilation requirements per ASHRAE 62.1 and ensure minimum airflow meets or exceeds this
  • Use manufacturer selection software for final selection - includes acoustic analysis
  • Coordinate VAV locations with ceiling grid and diffuser layout
  • Plan for adequate access to boxes for maintenance (minimum 24" clearance recommended)
  • Size ducts properly - undersized inlet ducts cause noise and control problems
  • Specify pressure independent controls for systems larger than 10-15 boxes

Installation

  • Support boxes independently - don't hang from ductwork
  • Provide 3-5 duct diameters of straight duct upstream of box inlet for proper airflow measurement
  • Install with adequate clearance for damper and coil access
  • Follow manufacturer's wiring diagrams exactly - improper wiring is #1 cause of control problems
  • Insulate hot water piping to coils to prevent heat loss in plenum
  • Install flow switches on hot water coils to prevent coil freeze-up

Commissioning and Balancing

  • Set maximum and minimum airflows using box controller, not damper stops
  • Verify actual airflow with independent measurement - don't rely solely on box readout
  • Test controls through full range of operation (heating, cooling, unoccupied)
  • Document settings for each box (max CFM, min CFM, heating capacity, etc.)
  • Perform functional testing per ASHRAE Guideline 0 or equivalent

Operation & Maintenance

  • Clean or replace filters in fan-powered units every 3-6 months
  • Check damper operation annually - stuck dampers are a common failure
  • Verify airflow calibration every 2-3 years - sensors can drift
  • Inspect hot water coils for leaks, especially in heating season
  • Monitor discharge temperatures - excessively high temps indicate reheat problems
  • Trend airflow and zone temperatures to identify control issues early

  • Inlet Static Pressure: Measure in duct just upstream of box (should be 0.5-2.0 in. w.c.)

  • Zone Temperature: Compare thermostat reading to independent measurement
  • Hot Water Temperatures: Entering and leaving (typical: 180¬∞F enter, 160¬∞F leave)

Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

Step 1: Gather Information

  • What is the specific complaint? (too hot, too cold, noisy, etc.)
  • When did the problem start?
  • Is it constant or intermittent?
  • Has anything changed recently? (setpoints, occupancy, renovations)
  • Are other zones affected?

Step 2: Check the Basics

  • Power supply to VAV box controller
  • Thermostat batteries (if applicable) and settings
  • Damper physically moves when commanded
  • No alarms or faults displayed on controller
  • Upstream air handling unit is operating

Step 3: Verify Airflow

  • Check actual airflow vs. setpoint (should be within ¬±10%)
  • Verify minimum and maximum airflow settings
  • Ensure box is not at minimum when zone calls for cooling
  • Check that damper fully opens when maximum flow commanded
  • Measure inlet static pressure - low pressure = insufficient flow

Step 4: Check Controls

  • Verify zone temperature sensor is reading correctly
  • Check setpoints and schedules (occupied vs. unoccupied)
  • Test override commands from BAS
  • Verify control sequence is appropriate for mode (heating vs. cooling)
  • Check for conflicting control signals

Safety Considerations

Always follow these safety procedures when troubleshooting VAV equipment:

  • Use ladder safety - VAV boxes are often 10-20 feet above floor
  • Watch for sharp edges on sheet metal boxes and ductwork
  • Be aware of electrical hazards - 24V controls but may be 120-480V for reheat
  • Don't bypass safeties, especially high-limit thermostats on electric heaters
  • Hot water coils can reach 180-200¬∞F - use caution
  • Wear appropriate PPE: safety glasses, gloves, hard hat if required
  • Ensure adequate lighting in ceiling spaces

Preventive Maintenance for VAV Boxes

Quarterly Tasks

  • Check for unusual noise during operation
  • Verify controller is displaying and responding
  • Spot-check airflow readings vs. setpoints
  • Review BAS alarms and trending for this zone
  • Change filters on fan-powered units

Annual Tasks

  • Verify actual airflow with independent measurement
  • Exercise damper through full range of motion
  • Check damper blade linkage for wear
  • Inspect hot water coil for leaks
  • Test electric heaters (continuity and amperage)
  • Verify zone temperature sensors
  • Clean airflow sensor ports

Every 3-5 Years

  • Recalibrate airflow sensors
  • Full functional test (heating, cooling, min, max)
  • Retune control loops if hunting observed
  • Update controller firmware if available
  • Consider replacement of 15+ year old controllers
  • Inspect fan bearings and motors (fan-powered units)

Common Control Problems and Solutions

Damper Hunting (Oscillating)

Symptoms: Damper continuously moves in and out, airflow unstable, zone temperature swings

Causes: Overly aggressive PID settings, oversized box, pressure fluctuations, interference between boxes

Solutions:

  • Increase damper loop proportional band (make it less sensitive)
  • Add damper action delay or deadband
  • Reduce integral gain if using PI or PID control
  • Check for and correct oversizing - may need smaller box
  • Investigate system pressure stability - may need VFD tuning on air handler

Won't Reach Minimum Airflow

Symptoms: Box reports less than minimum setpoint, zone overcools

Causes: Insufficient inlet pressure, leaking damper, calibration error, duct leakage

Solutions:

  • Measure inlet pressure - should be at least 0.5 in. w.c.
  • Check damper for proper sealing when closed
  • Verify minimum setpoint is appropriate (not set too low)
  • Recalibrate flow sensor
  • Check for downstream duct leaks robbing airflow

Reheat Discharge Temperature Too High

Symptoms: Discharge air exceeds 90-95¬∞F, occupant complaints of "baking" feeling

Causes: Airflow too low during heating, oversized reheat, failed minimum airflow setting

Solutions:

  • Verify minimum airflow is met during heating (should not drop below minimum)
  • Check reheat capacity - may be oversized for the airflow
  • Add discharge temperature limit control (limit to 90-95¬∞F max)
  • For hot water coils: reduce entering water temperature if possible
  • For electric: stage heaters to prevent all elements from firing simultaneously

  • Typical Capacity: 1-20 kW
  • Temperature Rise: 3,413 BTU/hr per kW
  • Formula: kW = (CFM √ó ŒîT) / 3,413
  • Voltage: 120V, 208V, 240V, 277V, 480V
  • Stages: Usually 2-3 stages for better control

Hot Water Reheat

  • Entering Water: 180¬∞F typical
  • Leaving Water: 160¬∞F typical (20¬∞F drop)
  • Entering Air: 55¬∞F (primary air temperature)

  • Coil Selection: 1-8 rows typical

  • Steam Pressure: 2-15 PSIG typical
