Important Notes

Roof drainage must be designed for the 100-year, 1-hour rainfall intensity for the location (or per local code). Controlled flow roof drains and siphonic systems have different sizing criteria. Secondary (overflow) drains or scuppers are required by most codes. Roof slope affects drainage patterns - ensure proper grading to drains. This calculator uses simplified methods for preliminary sizing. Consult IPC Chapter 11 or local storm drainage codes for detailed requirements.

Understanding Roof Drainage

Why Proper Roof Drainage Matters

Inadequate roof drainage can lead to:

  • Structural Overload: Water accumulation adds significant weight to roof structure
  • Roof Damage: Ponding water accelerates roof membrane deterioration
  • Leaks: Standing water increases likelihood of penetration
  • Ice Dams: Improper drainage contributes to ice formation in cold climates

Design Rainfall Intensity

Roof drains must be sized for the maximum expected rainfall. Most codes require:

  • Primary System: 100-year, 1-hour storm (or per local code)
  • Secondary System: Same storm or larger (100-year, 15-minute typical)
  • Location Specific: Rainfall intensity varies greatly by region
  • Climate Change: Some jurisdictions increasing design intensities

Rainfall Formula

Q = (A × I) / 96.3

Where:

  • Q = Flow rate (GPM)
  • A = Roof area (square feet)
  • I = Rainfall intensity (inches per hour)
  • 96.3 = Conversion constant

Types of Roof Drains

Conventional Roof Drains

Standard gravity-flow drains with dome strainer. Flow is subcritical (partially full pipe). Sized using IPC tables based on rainfall rate and drain size.

Controlled Flow Drains

Restrictor device limits flow rate to allow temporary ponding. Reduces pipe sizes but requires structural approval for additional water weight. Typically 15 GPM per drain.

Siphonic Drains

Engineered systems that create full-pipe siphonic flow. Much higher capacity than gravity drains. Requires specialized design by manufacturer. Can use smaller pipes and eliminate slope requirements.

Roof Drain Capacity

Conventional roof drain capacity depends on:

  • Drain size (diameter)
  • Rainfall intensity
  • Drain location (horizontal roof vs. vertical leader)
  • Water depth above drain (head)

Secondary Drainage (Overflow)

Building codes require secondary drainage to prevent roof collapse if primary drains clog:

  • Overflow Drains: Separate drains set 2" above primary drain level
  • Scuppers: Openings in parapet walls allowing overflow
  • Capacity: Secondary system sized for same or larger storm
  • Separate Discharge: Secondary drainage should discharge to visible location

Roof Slope and Drainage

  • Minimum Slope: 1/4" per foot to drains (typical code requirement)
  • Ponding: Areas where water accumulates more than 48 hours after rain
  • Cricket/Saddle: Ridge structure to divert water around rooftop equipment
  • Tapered Insulation: Can create positive slope on flat roofs

Installation Considerations

  • Install drains at low points of roof
  • Extend drain collar through roof membrane and flashing
  • Securely anchor drain to roof structure
  • Provide strainer/dome to prevent debris entry
  • Maintain minimum 1/4" per foot slope on horizontal storm drains
  • Insulate interior drains in heated buildings to prevent condensation

Roof Drainage Troubleshooting

Common Roof Drainage Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
Ponding Water Inadequate slope, deflected roof structure, clogged drains, too few drains Survey roof with level, check drain flow, calculate drainage capacity vs. area Add drains, improve slope with tapered insulation, clear blockages, repair sagging structure
Drain Overflow Undersized drains, clogged strainers, excessive rainfall, insufficient number of drains Verify rainfall intensity for area, calculate required vs. actual drains, inspect for debris Add additional drains, upsize drains, install larger strainers, regular maintenance
Ice Dams Poor insulation, heat loss through roof, inadequate drainage, gutter blockage Thermal imaging, inspect insulation, check ventilation, examine drainage pattern Improve roof insulation, add ventilation, ensure proper drainage slope, heat trace if needed
Slow Drainage Partially clogged drains, debris in leaders, inadequate slope on horizontal runs Remove strainers and inspect, snake/flush leaders, verify pipe slope Clean drains and leaders, install leaf guards, correct slope, regular maintenance schedule

Preventive Maintenance

  • Inspect and clean roof drains quarterly (more often if trees nearby)
  • Remove debris from strainers and roof surface
  • Flush leaders annually to clear accumulated sediment
  • Inspect overflow drains/scuppers to ensure clear path
  • Check roof slope and ponding after heavy rains
  • Verify drain assemblies are secure and properly sealed
  • Document drain locations and maintenance in building records

Professional Disclaimer

Roof drainage design must comply with IPC Chapter 11, local building codes, and structural requirements. Design rainfall intensities vary by location and are specified by local building departments. This calculator provides preliminary estimates only. Actual roof drainage design should account for roof configuration, parapet heights, overflow provisions, and potential ponding loads. For commercial buildings and complex roof systems, consult a licensed professional engineer. Inadequate roof drainage can lead to structural failure.
