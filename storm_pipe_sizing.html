Important Notes

This calculator uses the Rational Method for preliminary storm drain sizing. Actual design should account for time of concentration, inlet capacity, and local hydrology. Storm drainage systems must be designed for the design storm frequency specified by local codes (typically 10-year to 100-year). This calculator provides estimates only - consult a civil engineer for final storm drainage design. Consider peak flow from upstream areas and provide adequate inlet capacity.

Understanding Storm Drain Sizing

The Rational Method

The most common method for sizing storm drains for small watersheds (typically under 200 acres):

Q = C × I × A

Where:

  • Q = Peak runoff rate (cubic feet per second, CFS)
  • C = Runoff coefficient (dimensionless, 0 to 1)
  • I = Rainfall intensity (inches per hour)
  • A = Drainage area (acres)

Note: To convert CFS to GPM, multiply by 448.8

Runoff Coefficient (C)

Represents the fraction of rainfall that becomes runoff. Depends on surface type:

  • Roofs, Pavement, Concrete: C = 0.90-0.95 (very little absorption)
  • Gravel, Compacted Soil: C = 0.70-0.85
  • Lawns, Turf (flat): C = 0.40-0.50
  • Lawns, Turf (sloped): C = 0.50-0.60
  • Woods, Forest: C = 0.20-0.40
  • Permeable Pavement: C = 0.15-0.30

Composite C Value

For areas with mixed surfaces, calculate weighted average:

C = (C₁×A₁ + C₂×A₂ + ... + Cₙ×Aₙ) / (A₁ + A₂ + ... + Aₙ)

Rainfall Intensity

Design rainfall intensity depends on:

  • Design Storm Frequency: 2-yr, 5-yr, 10-yr, 25-yr, 50-yr, or 100-yr storm
  • Storm Duration: Equals time of concentration for the watershed
  • Location: Regional rainfall data from NOAA Atlas 14 or local requirements

Manning's Equation for Pipe Capacity

Used to determine pipe size needed for calculated flow rate:

Q = (1.486/n) × A × R^(2/3) × S^(1/2)

Where:

  • Q = Flow rate (CFS)
  • n = Manning's roughness coefficient
  • A = Cross-sectional area (sq ft)
  • R = Hydraulic radius (ft)
  • S = Slope (ft/ft)

Manning's n Values

  • PVC, HDPE (smooth): n = 0.010-0.013
  • Corrugated HDPE: n = 0.012-0.015
  • Concrete Pipe: n = 0.011-0.015
  • Corrugated Metal Pipe: n = 0.022-0.026
  • Vitrified Clay: n = 0.011-0.015

Design Storm Frequency

Selection depends on consequences of flooding:

  • 2-year to 10-year: Minor drainage, parking lots, low-risk areas
  • 25-year: Commercial areas, residential streets
  • 50-year: Major commercial, industrial areas
  • 100-year: Critical facilities, areas where flooding causes major damage

Velocity Limits

  • Minimum Velocity: 2-3 ft/s to prevent sediment deposition
  • Maximum Velocity: 10-15 ft/s to prevent erosion (depends on pipe material)
  • Self-Cleaning Velocity: 3 ft/s or greater preferred

Inlet and Junction Considerations

  • Inlet capacity must match or exceed pipe capacity
  • Grate inlets subject to clogging - use 50% efficiency factor
  • Curb inlets more reliable for street drainage
  • Manholes required at junctions, changes in direction, and grade
  • Maximum spacing: 400-600 feet between access points

Storm Drainage Troubleshooting

Common Storm Drain Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
Surface Flooding Undersized pipes, clogged inlets, inadequate slope, insufficient inlets Calculate capacity vs. actual flow, inspect inlets, verify slope, count inlet openings Upsize pipes, clear inlets, add inlets, increase pipe slope if possible
Sediment Buildup Low velocity, insufficient slope, erosion from upstream areas Measure flow velocity, check pipe slope, inspect for erosion sources Increase slope, install sediment traps, stabilize erosion sources, hydro-jet cleaning
Pipe Erosion/Damage Excessive velocity, poor bedding, corrosive soils, settlement Calculate velocity, camera inspection, soil testing, survey for settlement Install erosion protection, repair/replace damaged sections, improve bedding, address settlement
Inlet Clogging Debris accumulation, inadequate grate size, leaf/trash collection Inspect grates and sumps, check upstream for debris sources Regular maintenance, install larger grates or debris screens, eliminate debris sources

Maintenance Best Practices

  • Inspect and clean inlets before and after storm season
  • Video inspect pipes every 3-5 years (annually for critical systems)
  • Remove sediment buildup when depth exceeds 25% of pipe diameter
  • Maintain records of inlet and pipe locations (GPS coordinates)
  • Mark manholes and cleanouts for easy access
  • Stabilize drainage areas to minimize erosion and sediment
  • Install oil/water separators in parking areas to protect water quality

Professional Disclaimer

Storm drainage design is a specialized field requiring knowledge of hydrology, hydraulics, and local regulations. This calculator provides preliminary estimates only using simplified methods. Actual design must account for time of concentration, inlet capacity, backwater effects, downstream conditions, water quality requirements, and local design standards. For any storm drainage project, consult a licensed professional engineer. Improper storm drainage design can result in flooding, property damage, and liability.
