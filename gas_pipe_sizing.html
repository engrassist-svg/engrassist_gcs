Important Notes

This calculator provides preliminary gas pipe sizing based on IPC/IFGC tables. Actual installation must comply with local gas codes (IFGC, NFPA 54, or local amendments). Gas piping must be installed by licensed professionals. Sizing is critical for safety and proper appliance operation. Always verify with gas utility company requirements, use approved materials, test for leaks, and obtain required permits and inspections.

Understanding Gas Pipe Sizing

Why Proper Gas Pipe Sizing Matters

  • Safety: Undersized pipes cause low pressure and incomplete combustion
  • Appliance Performance: Adequate gas supply ensures proper operation
  • Efficiency: Proper sizing prevents energy waste from poor combustion
  • Code Compliance: Required by gas codes for legal installation

Gas Pipe Sizing Factors

  • BTU Load: Total demand of all appliances on the pipe
  • Pipe Length: Longer pipes require larger diameter
  • Inlet Pressure: Higher pressure allows smaller pipes
  • Pressure Drop: Allowable loss between meter and appliance
  • Specific Gravity: Natural gas (0.60) vs. propane (1.50)
  • Pipe Material: Affects flow resistance

Sizing Tables

Gas codes provide capacity tables showing maximum BTU/hr for each pipe size based on length and pressure:

  • Tables organized by inlet pressure and allowable drop
  • Separate tables for natural gas and propane
  • Different tables for steel, CSST, copper, and PE
  • Must use conservative capacity (length and pressure)

Pipe Material Options

Black Steel/Iron Pipe (Most Common)

  • Schedule 40 threaded steel pipe
  • Very durable and long-lasting
  • Approved for indoor and outdoor use
  • Requires threading and pipe dope/tape
  • Can rust if exposed to moisture

CSST (Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing)

  • Flexible yellow or black jacketed tubing
  • Faster installation than rigid pipe
  • Requires special fittings and tools
  • Must be bonded per NEC requirements
  • Higher capacity than steel for same size

Copper (Type K or L)

  • Rigid copper tubing approved for gas
  • Not all jurisdictions allow copper for gas
  • Must be Type K or L (no Type M)
  • Flare or brazed fittings (no soldered)
  • Limited use in some areas

Polyethylene (PE)

  • Plastic pipe for underground use only
  • Service line from meter to building
  • Must be yellow color-coded for gas
  • Requires transition to metal at building entry
  • No mechanical damage allowed

Pressure Systems

Low Pressure (under 0.5 psi)

Typical residential service. Gas delivered at 7 inches water column (0.25 psi). Requires larger pipe sizes but simpler regulations.

2-PSI System

Intermediate pressure for commercial or large residential. Regulator at each appliance or branch. Allows smaller pipe sizes.

5-PSI and Higher

Commercial/industrial systems. Strict code requirements. Professional engineering required. Allows very small pipes for long runs.

Sizing Method

  1. Calculate total BTU/hr load of all appliances
  2. Determine longest run from meter to appliance
  3. Select appropriate sizing table (pressure, gas type, material)

Gas Piping Troubleshooting

Common Gas Piping Problems

Symptom Possible Causes Diagnostic Steps Solution
Low Gas Pressure Undersized pipe, regulator failure, high demand, utility supply issue Measure pressure at meter and appliances, calculate demand vs. capacity, check regulator Upsize piping, replace regulator, reduce simultaneous loads, contact utility
Yellow Flames Insufficient gas pressure, dirty burners, improper air/gas mixture Check gas pressure, clean burners, verify appliance adjustment, inspect venting Correct gas pressure (upsize pipe if needed), clean/adjust burners, service appliance
Appliance Won't Start No gas supply, closed valve, low pressure, appliance fault Check gas valve position, measure pressure, verify pilot/ignition, check appliance controls Open valves, address low pressure, repair/replace appliance controls
Gas Odor Leak in piping or connections, failed joint, corrosion Evacuate area, call gas company, pressure test system when safe, soap test joints Emergency shutoff, professional leak repair, replace damaged sections, retest system

Safety Requirements

CRITICAL SAFETY RULES

  • Only licensed professionals may install or modify gas piping
  • Never use unapproved materials (galvanized, brass, compression fittings on steel)
  • Always pressure test with air/nitrogen before introducing gas
  • Purge air from lines before lighting appliances
  • Install with proper clearances from electrical and other utilities
  • Bond CSST to electrical ground per NEC
  • If you smell gas: evacuate, call gas company, no sparks/flames

Installation Best Practices

  • Slope pipes to allow condensate drainage (1/4" per 10 feet)
  • Install drip legs at appliances and low points
  • Use pipe dope rated for gas (yellow Teflon tape or approved compound)
  • Support pipes every 6-8 feet horizontally
  • Label all shutoff valves clearly
  • Install shutoff valve at each appliance
  • Protect exposed piping from mechanical damage
  • Keep records of pressure test and inspection results

Professional Disclaimer

Gas piping installation is governed by IFGC (International Fuel Gas Code), NFPA 54 (National Fuel Gas Code), and local amendments. This calculator provides preliminary estimates only. Actual installation must be performed by licensed contractors, comply with all code requirements, use approved materials and methods, be pressure tested for leaks, and pass inspection by the authority having jurisdiction. Improper gas piping can result in fires, explosions, carbon monoxide poisoning, and death. When in doubt, consult a licensed professional.
