Case Study 1: Custom Home with Graywater System

Project Overview

Project: 4,500 sq ft custom residence

Location: Arizona (water-scarce region)

Requirement: Reduce outdoor water use by 50%

Challenge

Homeowner wanted to minimize potable water use for extensive xeriscaping and vegetable garden irrigation. Local water costs were high and increasing. Arizona allows graywater systems for subsurface irrigation.

Solution

System Design

  • Sources: All showers, bathroom sinks, and washing machine
  • Exclusions: Kitchen sink and dishwasher (food/grease)
  • Treatment: 3-chamber sediment tank with outlet filter
  • Distribution: Subsurface drip irrigation to mulched planting areas
  • Overflow: Automatic diversion to regular sewer when storage full
  • Capacity: 200-gallon storage tank

Results

  • Graywater Production: ~150 gallons/day average
  • Potable Water Savings: 55,000 gallons/year for irrigation
  • Cost Savings: $350/year in water and sewer costs
  • Payback: 6-year payback on $2,100 system cost
  • Permit: Required plumbing permit with graywater addendum

Lessons Learned

  • Homeowner education critical - must use biodegradable soaps
  • Regular maintenance required on filters
  • Proper slope essential for gravity distribution
  • Winterization needed in cold climates

Case Study 2: Office Building Low Pressure Problem

Project Overview

Project: 6-story office building, 75,000 sq ft

Issue: Low pressure on upper floors after tenant build-out

Complaint: Toilets not flushing properly on 5th and 6th floors

Investigation

Testing Results

  • Ground Floor: 75 psi static, 65 psi flowing
  • 3rd Floor: 58 psi static, 45 psi flowing
  • 6th Floor: 38 psi static, 18 psi flowing
  • Required: Minimum 25 psi for flush valve toilets

Root Cause Analysis

Findings:

  • Building designed for tank-type toilets (lower pressure requirement)
  • Tenant improvements specified flush valve toilets for faster recovery
  • Plumbing riser sized for original design (15 WSFU per floor)
  • Actual demand with flush valves: 30 WSFU per floor
  • Excessive friction loss in undersized 1-1/2" riser

Solution Options Evaluated

Option 1: Booster Pump System

Cost: $35,000

Pros: Solves problem, adds capacity for future

Cons: Ongoing energy cost, maintenance required

Option 2: Replace Toilets with Tank Type

Cost: $15,000 (30 toilets × $500)

Pros: Lower cost, no ongoing costs

Cons: Longer flush cycle, tenant objection

Option 3: Upsize Riser Piping (Selected)

Cost: $28,000

Pros: Permanent solution, improved flow throughout building

Cons: Disruptive construction, access through tenant spaces

Implementation

  • Replaced 1-1/2" riser with 2" copper
  • Work performed during weekend/nights to minimize disruption
  • Coordinated access with tenants
  • Post-installation testing confirmed 28-35 psi on 6th floor

Lessons Learned

  • Tenant improvement reviews must verify fixture types match design
  • Base building systems should have some design margin
  • Early investigation prevented more costly fixes
  • Proper documentation of original design intent important

Case Study 3: Hospital Medical Gas Expansion

Project Overview

Project: Add 4-bed ICU expansion to existing hospital

Requirement: Medical oxygen, medical air, vacuum, WAGD to each bed

Code: NFPA 99 Healthcare Facilities Code

Design Approach

Load Calculation

Per ICU Bed (NFPA 99 requirements):

  • Medical Oxygen: 2 outlets × 50 SCFH = 100 SCFH per bed
  • Medical Air: 2 outlets × 50 SCFH = 100 SCFH per bed
  • Vacuum: 2 outlets × 3 SCFM = 6 SCFM per bed
  • WAGD: Not required for ICU (non-surgical)

Total for 4 beds:

  • Oxygen: 400 SCFH (simultaneous use factor: 1.0 for ICU)
  • Medical Air: 400 SCFH
  • Vacuum: 24 SCFM

Source Capacity Verification

Existing Systems:

  • Oxygen: Bulk liquid O₂ system, 3000 SCFH capacity, currently 2100 SCFH demand
  • Result: 900 SCFH available - adequate for 400 SCFH expansion ✓
  • Medical Air: 3× compressors, 2700 SCFH total, currently 1900 SCFH demand
  • Result: 800 SCFH available - adequate for 400 SCFH expansion ✓
  • Vacuum: 2× pumps, 85 SCFM total, currently 70 SCFM demand
  • Result: Only 15 SCFM available - need to add 1 pump ✗

Solution Implemented

  • Extended existing O₂ and medical air distribution with 3/4" copper runs
  • Added third vacuum pump (45 SCFM) to source equipment room
  • Installed zone valves at ICU entry for emergency isolation
  • All piping brazed with BCuP-5, nitrogen-purged during brazing
  • System tested at 150 psig for 24 hours (zero leakage)
  • Gas purity testing verified before clinical use

Special Considerations

  • Infection Control: Construction barriers and negative pressure during work
  • Continuity: Work scheduled to maintain existing patient care
  • Alarms: Integrated new zones into existing master alarm panel
  • Training: Facilities staff trained on new zone valve locations
  • Documentation: As-built drawings and valve location tags updated

Case Study 4: Restaurant Kitchen Grease Interceptor

Project Overview

Project: New fast-casual restaurant, 3,000 sq ft

Kitchen: 3-compartment sink, pre-rinse, mop sink, floor drains

Requirement: Size grease interceptor per local ordinance

Sizing Methodology (Local Requirements)

Fixture Flow Rates

  • 3-Compartment Sink (3 bays): 3 × 25 GPM = 75 GPM
  • Pre-Rinse Spray: 1.6 GPM
  • Mop Sink: 5 GPM
  • Floor Drains (indirect waste): Not counted
  • Total: 81.6 GPM

Grease Factor

Local code requires 0.25 retention factor for fast-casual restaurant

Calculation

Required Capacity = GPM × Retention Time × Grease Factor

= 81.6 × 30 minutes × 0.25

= 612 gallons

Selected: 750-gallon in-ground grease interceptor

Installation Details

  • Location: Exterior, in parking area for truck access
  • Depth: Invert 8 feet below grade to match building drain elevation
  • Access: Two 24" diameter manholes for pumping
  • Venting: Separate vent extended to roof (prevents odors)
  • Outlet Baffle: Prevents grease carryover to sewer

Maintenance Requirements

  • Pumping Frequency: When 25% full of grease (typically monthly)
  • Service Contract: Licensed waste hauler required
  • Records: Manifest records retained for 3 years
  • Inspection: Annual health department inspection

Case Study 5: High-Rise Pressure Zoning

Project Overview

Project: 25-story residential tower

Height: 250 feet from street to roof

Municipal Pressure: 65 psi at building service

Pressure Analysis

Without Pressure Zoning:

  • Ground Floor (worst case): 65 psi + (250 ft × 0.433) = 173 psi
  • Problem: Exceeds 80 psi code maximum, fixture damage risk
  • 25th Floor (worst case): 65 psi - (250 ft × 0.433) = -43 psi
  • Problem: Negative pressure - no flow possible

Solution: Three-Zone System

Zone 1: Floors 1-8 (Low Zone)

  • Supply: Municipal pressure (65 psi)
  • PRV Settings: Reduced to 55 psi at each floor
  • Elevation Loss: ~35 psi from ground to 8th floor
  • Pressure Range: 45-65 psi (acceptable)

Zone 2: Floors 9-17 (Mid Zone)

  • Supply: Booster pump in basement
  • Pump Discharge: 130 psi
  • PRVs at Each Floor: Reduce to 55-65 psi
  • Separate Riser: From Zone 1

Zone 3: Floors 18-25 (High Zone)

  • Supply: Second booster pump in basement
  • Pump Discharge: 195 psi
  • PRVs at Each Floor: Reduce to 55-65 psi
  • Separate Riser: From Zones 1 and 2

Equipment Selected

  • Zone 2 Pumps: 2× duplex booster pumps, 50 GPM @ 130 psi each
  • Zone 3 Pumps: 2× duplex booster pumps, 35 GPM @ 195 psi each
  • Controls: VFD drives maintain constant pressure
  • Redundancy: Lead/lag alternation, both pumps available
