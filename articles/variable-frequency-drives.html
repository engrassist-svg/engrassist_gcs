Overview

Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs), also called inverters or adjustable speed drives, control the speed of AC motors by varying the frequency and voltage of the electrical power supplied to the motor. In HVAC applications, VFDs are the single most effective energy-saving technology, reducing fan and pump energy consumption by 30-70% while improving comfort and extending equipment life.

Modern VFDs are sophisticated electronic devices incorporating power electronics, microprocessors, and advanced control algorithms. They've evolved from expensive specialty items to standard equipment on virtually all commercial HVAC fans and pumps over 5 HP.

How It Works

VFDs convert fixed-frequency AC power to variable-frequency AC power through a three-stage process:

VFD Operation

  1. Rectifier: Converts incoming AC power (50/60 Hz) to DC power using diodes
  2. DC Bus: Smooths and filters the DC power using capacitors (and sometimes inductors)
  3. Inverter: Converts DC back to variable-frequency AC using Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs)
  4. Output: Variable voltage and frequency AC power controls motor speed

V/Hz Control (Scalar Control)

Most common method for HVAC applications:

  • Maintains constant ratio of voltage to frequency (V/Hz)
  • Ensures constant motor flux and torque capability
  • Simple, reliable, cost-effective
  • Adequate for fans and pumps (constant torque not required)

Vector Control

More sophisticated control for demanding applications:

  • Independently controls motor flux and torque
  • Better performance at low speeds
  • Higher cost, more complex setup
  • Rarely needed in HVAC (more common in industrial processes)

Energy Savings

Fan and Pump Laws (Affinity Laws)

The key to VFD energy savings - power varies with the cube of speed:

  • Flow: Q ∝ N (flow proportional to speed)
  • Pressure: P ∝ N² (pressure proportional to speed squared)
  • Power: kW ∝ N³ (power proportional to speed cubed)

Example: Energy Savings Calculation

Supply fan, 25 HP, reducing speed to 60%:

  • Airflow: 100% → 60%
  • Pressure: 100% → 36%
  • Power: 100% → 21.6% (0.6³ = 0.216)
  • Energy savings: 78.4%!

Annual savings for 25 HP motor, 6,000 hours, $0.12/kWh:

  • Without VFD: 25 HP × 0.746 kW/HP × 6,000 hrs × $0.12 = $13,428
  • With VFD @ 60%: $13,428 × 0.216 = $2,900
  • Annual savings: $10,528
  • VFD cost (~$4,000) = Payback < 5 months

Where VFDs Save Energy

  • VAV Systems: Supply fan speed reduced as VAV boxes close (30-50% savings)
  • Chilled Water Pumps: Flow reduced based on valve positions (40-60% savings)
  • Condenser Water Pumps: Flow optimized for chiller/tower efficiency (30-50% savings)
  • Cooling Tower Fans: Modulate to maintain setpoint (20-40% savings)

HVAC Applications

Supply Air Fans

  • VAV system fan control (most common VFD application)
  • Maintains duct static pressure setpoint
  • Standard on fans > 7.5 HP per most energy codes
  • Typical turndown: 40-100% speed

Pumps (Chilled Water, Hot Water, Condenser Water)

  • Maintains differential pressure setpoint
  • Often use differential pressure reset for additional savings
  • Critical: Maintain minimum flow through equipment
  • Typical turndown: 30-100% speed

Cooling Tower Fans

  • Modulates to maintain condenser water temperature setpoint
  • Significant savings compared to on/off control
  • Reduces start/stop cycles and mechanical stress
  • Typical turndown: 30-100% speed

Chiller Compressors

  • Standard on modern centrifugal chillers
  • Enables superior part-load efficiency
  • Complex drive with specialized motor bearings
  • Usually supplied as integrated unit with chiller

Sizing and Selection

Sizing Parameters

Parameter Requirements/Notes
Motor Horsepower VFD must be rated for motor HP or kW
Input Voltage 208V, 230V, 460V, 575V - match building power
Phase Single-phase or three-phase (3-phase standard for HVAC)
Motor Full Load Amps (FLA) VFD output current must exceed motor FLA
Load Type Variable torque (fans/pumps) or constant torque
Duty Cycle Continuous, intermittent, or heavy-duty
Environment NEMA 1 (indoor), NEMA 3R (outdoor), NEMA 4X (wash down)

Sizing Guidelines

  • Match Motor HP: VFD and motor must have compatible ratings
  • Check Output Current: VFD continuous current ≥ motor FLA
  • Overload Capability: VFD must handle 110-150% for 60 seconds (motor starting)
  • Service Factor: Generally don't need to oversize VFD for motor service factor
  • Multiple Motors: Can drive multiple motors if total FLA < VFD rating (not common in HVAC)

Common Sizing Mistakes

  • Using motor nameplate HP instead of actual load (can lead to undersizing)
  • Oversizing VFD (costs more, no benefit for fans/pumps)
  • Not accounting for altitude derating (>3,300 ft requires derating)
  • Ignoring ambient temperature (VFD capacity reduces at high temps)

Harmonics and Power Quality

What Are Harmonics?

VFDs draw non-sinusoidal current from the power system, creating harmonic distortion:

  • Total Harmonic Distortion (THD): Measure of waveform distortion
  • IEEE 519: Standard limiting harmonic distortion (typically THD < 5%)
  • Effects: Overheating of transformers, neutral conductors, capacitor banks; interference with sensitive electronics

Harmonic Mitigation Methods

1. Standard 6-Pulse VFD

  • Most economical
  • THD: 30-50% (unacceptable for larger drives)
  • Use only for small drives (< 10 HP) or where harmonics aren't a concern

2. AC Line Reactors or DC Chokes

  • Reduces THD to 25-35%
  • Low cost (~5-10% of VFD cost)
  • Standard practice for most HVAC VFDs

3. 12-Pulse or 18-Pulse VFDs

  • Requires special transformer with phase-shifting windings
  • THD: 8-15% (12-pulse), 3-5% (18-pulse)
  • Cost: 30-50% premium over standard VFD
  • Used for large drives (> 100 HP) or sensitive installations

4. Active Front End (AFE) VFDs

  • Uses IGBTs in rectifier instead of diodes
  • THD: < 5%, near-unity power factor
  • Can regenerate power back to grid
  • Cost: 50-80% premium
  • Rare in HVAC (more common in elevators, cranes)

When to Worry About Harmonics

  • Total VFD load > 30% of transformer capacity
  • Sensitive equipment (medical imaging, servers) on same transformer
  • Utility has strict harmonic limits
  • Solution: Use line reactors as minimum, consider 12-pulse for large drives

Installation Requirements

Electrical Installation

  • Disconnect: Lockable disconnect within sight of VFD (code requirement)
  • Input Wiring: Size per VFD input current rating and NEC
  • Output Wiring: Size per motor FLA, use THHN/THWN for VFD output
  • Grounding: Critical for VFD - use low-impedance ground path
  • Cable Length: Limit to 150-300 feet without reactors/filters

Motor Considerations

  • Inverter-Duty Motor: Required for VFD use (better insulation)
  • Voltage Spikes: VFD output can have 2-3× voltage spikes at motor terminals
  • Shaft Currents: VFDs can induce bearing currents - use insulated bearings or grounding brushes on larger motors
  • Motor Overload: Provide overload protection in VFD or separate relay

Control Wiring

  • Shielded Cable: Use for speed reference and feedback signals
  • Separation: Keep control wiring 12-18" from power wiring
  • Grounding: Ground shield at VFD end only (avoid ground loops)
  • Common Signals: 4-20mA (most noise-immune), 0-10VDC, or digital protocols (BACnet, Modbus)

Environmental

  • Temperature: 0-40°C (32-104°F) standard, derate above 40°C
  • Ventilation: Provide adequate airflow - VFD generates heat (2-4% of motor power)
  • Altitude: Derate 1% per 330 feet above 3,300 feet
  • Dust: Use NEMA 12 or higher in dusty environments

Programming and Setup

Essential Parameters

  • Motor Nameplate Data: Voltage, FLA, RPM, frequency - enter accurately
  • Minimum/Maximum Frequency: Typically 15-60 Hz (fans/pumps)
  • Acceleration/Deceleration Time: 15-30 seconds typical for HVAC
  • Control Mode: V/Hz for most HVAC applications
  • Current Limit: Set to 100-110% of motor FLA

Control Methods

Local Control (at VFD)

  • Keypad or potentiometer on VFD face
  • Used for testing and commissioning
  • Not typical for normal operation

Analog Input (4-20mA or 0-10V)

  • Most common for HVAC control
  • Signal from pressure sensor, temperature controller, or BMS
  • 4-20mA preferred for noise immunity

Digital Communication (BACnet, Modbus, etc.)

  • Increasingly common with modern BMS
  • Allows monitoring of motor amps, frequency, faults, etc.
  • More complex setup but better functionality

PID Control

Most VFDs include built-in PID controller:

  • Set process setpoint (e.g., 1.5" w.c. duct static pressure)
  • VFD automatically adjusts speed to maintain setpoint
  • Tune P, I, D gains for stable control without hunting

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Specify VFDs on all HVAC fans and pumps > 5 HP
  • Include 3-5% line reactors as standard
  • Consider 12-pulse drives for motors > 100 HP
  • Coordinate VFD enclosure with installation location (indoor/outdoor)
  • Don't spec bypass starters - defeats the purpose and wastes money

Installation

  • Provide adequate ventilation in electrical rooms
  • Use inverter-duty motors or verify existing motors are compatible
  • Install line reactors or harmonic filters as specified
  • Route control wiring separately from power wiring
  • Test VFD operation before connecting to BMS

Commissioning

  • Verify motor rotation direction (critical!)
  • Test emergency stop functionality
  • Verify minimum and maximum speed limits
  • Tune PID parameters if using internal PID
  • Measure and log motor current at various speeds
  • Document all parameter settings for future reference

Maintenance

  • Monthly: Check for fault codes, verify operation
  • Quarterly: Clean cooling fins/fans, check connections
  • Annually: Inspect capacitors (bulging = replacement needed), check fan operation
  • Every 5 Years: Consider capacitor replacement (typical lifespan)

Common Problems and Solutions

Overcurrent Fault

  • Possible Causes: Short circuit, ground fault, mechanical binding, acceleration too fast
  • Check: Motor windings, cable insulation, mechanical load
  • Solution: Repair short, increase acceleration time, reduce current limit

Overvoltage Fault

  • Possible Causes: Deceleration too fast, regenerative load, utility voltage spike
  • Check: Deceleration time, mechanical load
  • Solution: Increase deceleration time, add braking resistor

Motor Won't Start

  • Possible Causes: Wrong programming, control signal missing, fault condition
  • Check: Control signal, fault display, enable inputs
  • Solution: Verify programming, check control wiring, clear faults

Motor Overheating

  • Possible Causes: Continuous low-speed operation, poor ventilation, VFD carrier frequency too high
  • Check: Operating speed, motor ventilation, carrier frequency
  • Solution: Add external cooling, reduce carrier frequency, don't operate at very low speeds continuously

Often Overlooked Considerations

Heat Generation

  • VFDs generate heat = 2-4% of motor power as losses
  • 25 HP VFD generates ~450-900 BTU/hr
  • Must be accounted for in electrical room cooling
  • Provide adequate ventilation or air conditioning

Typical Pricing (2024)

  • 5 HP VFD: $800-1,200
  • 15 HP VFD: $1,500-2,500
  • 30 HP VFD: $2,500-4,000
  • 60 HP VFD: $4,500-7,000
  • 100 HP VFD: $7,000-11,000
  • Line Reactors (add 5-10%): $100-1,000

Installation labor typically adds 30-50% to equipment cost. Energy savings usually pay back VFD investment in 6 months to 3 years.

Service Life

  • Typical VFD lifespan: 10-15 years
  • Limiting factor: Electrolytic capacitors (5-10 year life)
  • Operating temperature significantly affects life (10°C increase = 50% life reduction)
  • Plan for eventual replacement or capacitor refurbishment

Bypass Starters

  • Sometimes specified to "bypass VFD for reliability"
  • Don't do this! Defeats energy savings, doubles cost, rarely used
  • If reliability is critical, provide N+1 redundancy with multiple smaller units
  • Modern VFDs are reliable (MTBF > 50,000 hours typical)
