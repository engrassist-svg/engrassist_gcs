Overview

Rooftop Units (RTUs) are self-contained, packaged HVAC systems installed on the roof of a building. They contain all major components - compressor, condenser, evaporator, air handler, controls, and often heating elements - in a single weatherproof enclosure. RTUs are the most common HVAC solution for commercial buildings in North America, accounting for approximately 50% of commercial cooling capacity.

Their popularity stems from several advantages: lower installation cost compared to central plant systems, ease of zoning (one RTU per zone), simpler maintenance, and the ability to continue partial building operation if one unit fails.

How It Works

RTUs operate on the vapor-compression refrigeration cycle while simultaneously providing ventilation and heating:

The Cooling Cycle

  1. Air Intake: Return air from the space (and outdoor air for ventilation) enters the unit through the bottom
  2. Filtration: Air passes through filters to remove particulates
  3. Cooling: Air flows over the DX evaporator coil where refrigerant absorbs heat, cooling and dehumidifying the air
  4. Supply: Supply fan pushes cooled air through ductwork to the space
  5. Heat Rejection: Compressor pumps refrigerant to the condenser coil where outdoor air removes heat

Heating Mode

Most RTUs include heating capability through one or more methods:

  • Gas Furnace: Natural gas or propane burners heat air directly (most common)
  • Electric Heat: Resistance heating elements (expensive to operate but simple)
  • Heat Pump: Reverses refrigeration cycle to extract heat from outdoor air
  • Hot Water/Steam Coils: Uses building's central heating system

Economizer Operation

When outdoor conditions are favorable (cool and dry), the economizer brings in 100% outdoor air for "free cooling" instead of running the compressor. This can reduce cooling energy by 20-40% in appropriate climates.

Types and Configurations

1. Constant Air Volume (CAV)

Supply fan runs at constant speed. Simple, reliable, and least expensive.

  • Advantages: Lower first cost, simple controls, proven reliability
  • Disadvantages: Poor part-load efficiency, less comfort control, wastes fan energy
  • Applications: Simple applications, budget-constrained projects, small single-zone spaces

2. Variable Air Volume (VAV)

Supply fan speed varies with load via VFD. More efficient but more complex.

  • Advantages: Energy savings of 20-40%, better humidity control, quieter at low load
  • Disadvantages: Higher first cost, requires proper commissioning
  • Applications: Larger spaces, variable occupancy, energy-conscious projects

3. Dedicated Outdoor Air System (DOAS)

Handles 100% outdoor air for ventilation while separate units handle space cooling.

  • Advantages: Superior humidity control, energy recovery potential, simplified zoning
  • Disadvantages: Requires coordination with other systems, higher first cost
  • Applications: High ventilation requirements, humid climates, buildings with varying internal loads

4. Heat Pump RTUs

Provides both heating and cooling via refrigeration cycle reversal.

  • Advantages: No gas connection required, efficient in mild climates, single system for both modes
  • Disadvantages: Reduced heating capacity at low temps, requires auxiliary heat in cold climates
  • Applications: Mild climates, electric-only buildings, buildings without gas service

Applications

  • Retail: Big box stores, shopping centers, restaurants (50-80% of installations use RTUs)
  • Office Buildings: Small to mid-size office buildings, especially single-story
  • Schools: Classrooms, gymnasiums, administrative areas
  • Warehouses: Distribution centers, light industrial (with make-up air requirements)
  • Healthcare: Outpatient clinics, medical offices (not critical care areas)
  • Hospitality: Hotels, motels (guest room areas)

Where RTUs Are NOT Ideal

  • Buildings without suitable roof structure or access
  • Multi-story buildings (>3-4 floors) - duct shaft space and static pressure become issues
  • Very large single zones (>30,000 CFM) - consider central plant or multiple smaller units
  • Critical environments requiring high reliability (hospitals, data centers) - prefer central plant with redundancy

Key Components

Compressor

  • Scroll: Most common, 3-25 tons, quiet, reliable, good efficiency
  • Reciprocating: Older technology, less common in modern RTUs
  • Screw: Used in larger units (25+ tons), good part-load efficiency
  • Staging: Multiple compressors provide capacity control (33%/66%/100% with 2 compressors)

Heat Exchangers

  • Evaporator Coil: Copper tubes with aluminum fins, cools and dehumidifies air
  • Condenser Coil: Rejects heat to outdoor air, copper or aluminum construction
  • Coil Coatings: E-coat or enhanced coatings for corrosive environments (coastal, industrial)

Supply Fan

  • Belt-Drive: Adjustable speed via pulley change, common on older units
  • Direct-Drive Plenum (DDP): Modern standard, more efficient, less maintenance
  • Forward-Curved vs. Backward-Curved: BC fans more efficient but require more space
  • Variable Frequency Drive (VFD): Critical for VAV operation and energy savings

Economizer

  • Dry-Bulb Control: Based on outdoor air temperature only (simpler)
  • Enthalpy Control: Based on outdoor air heat content (better in humid climates)
  • Differential Enthalpy: Compares outdoor vs. return air (most sophisticated)
  • Dampers: Modulating outdoor air, return air, and exhaust dampers with actuators

Outdoor Air Requirement CFM per ASHRAE 62.1 or local code
External Static Pressure inches w.c. (supply + return duct losses)
Design Outdoor Temperature Summer cooling and winter heating design temps
Indoor Design Conditions Typically 75°F / 50% RH cooling, 70°F heating

Roof Structure Weight capacity, curb size, access

Sizing Rules and Guidelines

  • Cooling: Do NOT use "400 SF/ton" rule - perform proper load calculation per ACCA Manual J or ASHRAE
  • Airflow: 400 CFM/ton is typical for standard applications (350 for humid climates, 450 for dry)
  • Heating: Size for heating load OR 125% of cooling capacity, whichever is greater
  • Outdoor Air: Verify economizer capacity can handle 100% outdoor air without excessive static pressure
  • Safety Factor: 5-10% maximum - RTUs are available in standard sizes, pick closest without excessive oversizing

Example Sizing Calculation

Small retail space, 5,000 SF:

  • Calculated cooling load: 100,000 BTU/hr (8.3 tons)
  • Select: 10-ton RTU (next standard size up)
  • Airflow required: 10 tons × 400 CFM/ton = 4,000 CFM
  • Outdoor air: 5,000 SF × 0.06 CFM/SF = 300 CFM minimum
  • Heating: 10 tons × 12,000 BTU/ton × 1.25 = 150 MBH input

Available Options

Efficiency Upgrades

  • High-Efficiency Compressors: 14-16 SEER / 11-13 EER vs. standard 13 SEER / 10 EER
  • Variable-Speed Supply Fan: 20-30% fan energy savings
  • Variable-Speed Condenser Fans: Improved part-load efficiency, quieter operation
  • Advanced Economizers: Differential enthalpy control for maximum free cooling
  • Energy Recovery Ventilator (ERV): Reduces ventilation air load by 50-70%

Comfort Enhancements

  • Multi-Stage or Modulating Heat: Better comfort than single-stage
  • Hot Gas Reheat: Improves humidity control by allowing lower coil temps
  • Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV): CO2 sensors modulate outdoor air based on occupancy
  • Variable Refrigerant Flow (VRF): Precise capacity control, excellent part-load performance

Controls and Integration

  • Integrated Economizer Control: Coordinated with DX cooling for maximum efficiency
  • BMS Integration: BACnet, Modbus, or LonWorks communication
  • Advanced Diagnostics: Predictive maintenance, fault detection
  • Cloud Connectivity: Remote monitoring and control

Construction Options

  • Curb Adapter: Allows replacement without new curb (match existing dimensions)
  • Seismic Certification: Required in high-seismic zones (OSHPD in California)
  • Hail Guards: Protect condenser coils in hail-prone areas
  • Enhanced Corrosion Protection: E-coat coils, stainless fasteners for coastal/industrial
  • Low-Ambient Kit: Allows cooling operation to 0°F or lower (data centers, food service)

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Perform room-by-room load calculations - never rely on SF/ton rules
  • Coordinate curb location with structural engineer - verify roof capacity
  • Plan duct routing during design - don't leave it to installers to figure out
  • Size outdoor air for ASHRAE 62.1 or local code, whichever is stricter
  • Specify economizers in climates with >1,000 hours below 55°F dry-bulb
  • Include VFDs on supply fans for units >7.5 tons in most energy codes
  • Don't forget knockout panels for future RTU replacement access

Installation

  • Install RTU on properly constructed curb - level and sealed to prevent leaks
  • Use spring isolators under curb to minimize vibration transmission
  • Install flexible duct connectors at supply and return to prevent vibration transmission
  • Commission economizer operation - verify dampers open/close fully and sequence correctly
  • Verify refrigerant charge - factory charge is often for standard conditions only
  • Balance airflow and verify against design - critical for performance
  • Test all safety controls before turning over to owner

Operation & Maintenance

  • Filters: Change every 1-3 months depending on environment (most important task)
  • Coils: Clean condenser coil annually, evaporator coil every 2-3 years
  • Economizer: Verify operation quarterly - economizers fail frequently and waste energy
  • Belts: Inspect quarterly, adjust tension, replace when worn (belt-drive units)
  • Drain Pan: Clean annually to prevent biological growth and odors
  • Refrigerant: Check superheat/subcooling annually, repair leaks promptly
  • Gas Heat Exchanger: Inspect annually for cracks (safety critical)

Often Overlooked Considerations

Physical Dimensions and Weight

RTUs are larger and heavier than many people expect:

  • 10-ton unit: ~10' L × 5' W × 4' H, 1,500-2,000 lbs
  • 25-ton unit: ~15' L × 6' W × 5' H, 3,000-4,000 lbs
  • Verify roof structural capacity including curb and supports
  • Plan rigging access - how will you get it on the roof?
  • Consider future replacement - will a crane have access?

Static Pressure Capacity

RTUs have limited fan capacity - high duct losses are problematic:

  • Standard units: 0.5-1.0" w.c. external static
  • High-static units: 1.5-2.5" w.c. external static
  • Calculate duct pressure drop carefully during design
  • Long duct runs or restrictive fittings require high-static units
  • High-static units cost 10-20% more and use more fan energy

Noise Levels

RTUs generate significant noise from compressor and fans:

  • Standard unit: 70-78 dBA at 10 feet
  • Low-noise option: 65-72 dBA at 10 feet (10-20% premium)
  • Consider location relative to noise-sensitive areas
  • Sound attenuation curb can reduce transmission to occupied space below
  • Check local noise ordinances - many limit rooftop equipment noise

Typical Pricing (2024)

  • 3-5 tons (13 SEER): $4,000-7,000
  • 7.5-10 tons (13 SEER): $7,000-12,000
  • 12.5-15 tons (13 SEER): $12,000-18,000
  • 20-25 tons (13 SEER): $20,000-30,000
  • High-Efficiency Premium: Add 15-25% for 14-16 SEER
  • Heat Pump: Add 20-30% vs. standard gas heat

Installation typically adds $3,000-8,000 depending on complexity. Budget 3-5% of equipment cost annually for maintenance.

Economizer Maintenance

Studies show 40-60% of economizers don't work properly:

  • Dampers stuck, linkages broken, or actuators failed
  • Sensors out of calibration or failed
  • Controls improperly programmed
  • Result: Either no free cooling (wasting energy) or excessive outdoor air (wasting energy)
  • Solution: Test economizer operation quarterly as part of routine maintenance

Service Access

  • Technicians need to service units year-round - even in winter
  • Provide safe roof access with ladder, stairs, or ship's ladder
  • Consider walkway pads to roof drains and all RTUs
  • Ensure adequate working space around units per code (36" on service side)
  • Provide 110V receptacle on roof for tools and test equipment
