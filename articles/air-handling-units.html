Overview

Air Handling Units (AHUs) are large assemblies used to condition and circulate air as part of a building's HVAC system. Unlike packaged rooftop units that contain refrigeration equipment, AHUs typically consist of a blower/fan, heating and cooling coils, filters, dampers, and controls housed in a modular metal enclosure. They receive chilled water and hot water from a central plant to provide heating and cooling to building zones.

AHUs range from small units serving a single floor (~2,000 CFM) to massive custom-built units serving entire buildings (100,000+ CFM). They offer design flexibility, energy efficiency, and precise control that make them ideal for large commercial buildings, hospitals, laboratories, and other applications requiring sophisticated air handling.

How It Works

Air handling units condition and distribute air through the following process:

The Air Handling Process

  1. Air Intake: Return air from the building and outdoor air for ventilation enter through dampered intakes
  2. Mixing: Return and outdoor air mix in proportions controlled by economizer dampers
  3. Filtration: Mixed air passes through filters (typically MERV 8-16) to remove particulates
  4. Heating/Cooling: Air flows over chilled water coils (summer) or hot water/steam coils (winter)
  5. Humidification: Steam or spray humidifiers add moisture if required (winter operation)
  6. Supply Fan: Pressurizes and distributes conditioned air through ductwork to VAV boxes or diffusers
  7. Return: Return air comes back to the AHU or exhausts to atmosphere via return fan

Control Sequences

Modern AHUs employ sophisticated control strategies:

  • Supply Air Temperature Reset: Adjusts supply temp based on zone demand to minimize energy
  • Static Pressure Reset: Reduces fan speed when VAV boxes aren't calling for maximum airflow
  • Economizer Control: Maximizes free cooling when outdoor conditions permit
  • Demand-Controlled Ventilation: Modulates outdoor air based on CO2 levels or occupancy

Types of Air Handling Units

1. Draw-Through AHUs

Fan is located downstream of coils, "pulling" air through the unit.

  • Advantages: More uniform airflow over coils, better coil performance, easier to add humidification
  • Disadvantages: Fan operates in conditioned air stream (heat gain), slightly larger footprint
  • Applications: Most common configuration, preferred for critical applications

2. Blow-Through AHUs

Fan is located upstream of coils, "pushing" air through the unit.

  • Advantages: More compact, better for high static pressure applications
  • Disadvantages: Less uniform airflow over coils, potential for turbulence
  • Applications: Space-constrained applications, makeup air units

3. Packaged vs. Built-Up

Packaged (Modular): Factory-assembled sections that bolt together on site. Standard sizes, faster delivery.

  • Capacity: 1,000 - 50,000 CFM typical
  • Lower cost, faster installation
  • Limited customization

Built-Up (Custom): Custom-designed and fabricated for specific project requirements.

  • Capacity: Any size, commonly 20,000 - 200,000+ CFM
  • Fully customizable
  • Higher cost, longer lead time
  • Ideal for unique applications (labs, hospitals, industrial)

4. Dedicated Outdoor Air Systems (DOAS)

Specialized AHUs designed to handle 100% outdoor air for ventilation.

  • Often includes energy recovery (ERV) to reduce loads
  • Can provide neutral, warm, or cool supply air
  • Works with separate sensible cooling systems
  • Superior humidity control compared to traditional systems

5. Makeup Air Units (MAUs)

Handle 100% outdoor air for spaces with high exhaust requirements (kitchens, labs, manufacturing).

  • Temper outdoor air to acceptable temperature
  • Often gas-fired for direct heating
  • May include energy recovery from exhaust
  • Typically blow-through configuration

Key Components

Supply Fan

  • Centrifugal (Forward-Curved): Compact, quiet, lower efficiency, common in smaller units
  • Centrifugal (Backward-Curved/Airfoil): More efficient, better for larger units, steeper performance curve
  • Plenum Fan: Direct-drive, no belts, very reliable, common in modern AHUs
  • Variable Frequency Drive (VFD): Standard on units >5,000 CFM, provides energy savings of 30-60%

Heating and Cooling Coils

Chilled Water Coil:

  • Copper tubes with aluminum fins most common
  • Typically 6-12 rows deep for comfort cooling, 8-16 rows for dehumidification
  • Face velocity: 400-600 FPM for standard applications
  • Fin spacing: 8-12 fins per inch (FPI) standard, 6-8 FPI for outdoor air coils

Heating Coil:

  • Hot water coils: 2-6 rows, 180°F typical
  • Steam coils: 1-4 rows, non-freeze design critical
  • Electric heat: Resistance elements, expensive to operate

Filters

  • Pre-Filters (MERV 8-11): Protect coils, extend final filter life
  • Final Filters (MERV 13-16): IAQ improvement, required for many healthcare applications
  • HEPA Filters (MERV 17-20): Cleanrooms, surgical suites, pharmaceutical manufacturing
  • Budget for higher pressure drop with higher MERV ratings

Dampers

  • Outdoor Air Damper: Modulates outdoor air intake
  • Return Air Damper: Modulates return air (works opposite of OA damper)
  • Relief/Exhaust Damper: Relieves excess pressure when economizing
  • Opposed-Blade vs. Parallel-Blade: Opposed blade for modulating, parallel blade for shutoff

Humidifiers (Optional)

  • Steam Grid: Most common, very responsive, requires steam source
  • Electrode Steam Generators: Generate steam electrically
  • Ultrasonic: Efficient, potential for white dust with untreated water
  • Locate after heating coil to prevent condensation in duct

Applications

  • Office Buildings: Constant volume or VAV systems serving multiple floors
  • Hospitals: Critical air handling with HEPA filtration, 100% outdoor air in some areas
  • Laboratories: High outdoor air requirements (8-10 ACH minimum), energy recovery
  • Schools/Universities: Large air volumes, economizer savings, DCV for variable occupancy
  • Manufacturing: Makeup air for process exhaust, specialized filtration
  • Data Centers: Precise temperature/humidity control, N+1 redundancy
  • Clean Rooms: HEPA filtration, pressure control, very high air change rates

Common Configurations

Single-Zone Constant Volume

  • Simplest configuration
  • AHU controls space temperature directly
  • Best for spaces with uniform loads
  • Less energy-efficient than VAV

Multi-Zone VAV

  • Most common in modern commercial buildings
  • AHU supplies constant temperature air
  • VAV boxes modulate airflow to each zone
  • Excellent energy efficiency with proper controls

Dual-Duct System

  • AHU supplies both hot and cold air streams
  • Terminal boxes mix to achieve zone setpoint
  • Excellent comfort but energy-intensive
  • Rare in new construction, seen in renovations of older buildings

DOAS + Radiant

  • DOAS handles ventilation and humidity
  • Radiant panels or chilled beams handle sensible loads
  • Superior comfort and energy efficiency
  • Growing in popularity for commercial offices

Sizing Guidelines

  • Cooling Coil Face Velocity: 400-500 FPM for good dehumidification, up to 600 FPM for sensible-only
  • Filter Face Velocity: 300-500 FPM depending on filter type and MERV rating
  • Coil Rows: 6-8 rows typical for comfort cooling, 10-12 for high latent loads
  • Fan Speed: 1,200-1,800 RPM optimal for efficiency and noise
  • Casing Velocity: Keep under 800-1,000 FPM to minimize turbulence

Example AHU Selection

Office building floor, 20,000 SF:

  • Calculated airflow: 16,000 CFM (400 CFM per 1,000 SF typical)
  • Cooling load: 200,000 BTU/hr (16.7 tons)
  • Outdoor air: 3,200 CFM (20% of total)
  • External static: 2.5" w.c. (ductwork + VAV boxes)
  • Select: 16,000 CFM AHU with 6-row CHW coil, 4-row HW coil, MERV 13 filters, 40 HP supply fan with VFD

Available Options

Energy Efficiency Features

  • Energy Recovery Ventilator (ERV): Recovers 60-80% of energy from exhaust air
  • VFD on Supply and Return Fans: 30-60% fan energy savings
  • Demand-Controlled Ventilation: CO2 sensors reduce OA during low occupancy
  • Premium Efficiency Motors: NEMA Premium or IE3/IE4 standard in modern units
  • Low-Leakage Construction: AMCA Class 1 or better to prevent air leakage

Filtration Upgrades

  • MERV 13-16 Filters: Improved IAQ, captures smaller particles including many viruses
  • UV-C Lights: Germicidal treatment of coils and airstream
  • Photocatalytic Oxidation (PCO): Advanced air treatment for VOCs
  • Carbon Filters: Odor and VOC removal

Sound Attenuation

  • Acoustic Panels: Installed in mixing and discharge sections
  • Vibration Isolation: Spring or neoprene isolators to prevent structure-borne noise
  • Flexible Connections: Canvas or neoprene duct connections
  • Low-Noise Fans: Oversized fans running at lower speeds

Controls and Monitoring

  • DDC Controls: Direct digital controls for precise operation
  • VFD Integration: Coordinated fan speed with damper position
  • Differential Pressure Sensors: Monitor filter loading
  • Airflow Measuring Stations: Accurate outdoor air measurement

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Locate AHUs in dedicated mechanical rooms with adequate service access
  • Size mechanical room for unit removal/replacement (length + 5-10 feet)
  • Plan for filter replacement access - 36" clearance minimum
  • Coordinate coil connections with piping routing early
  • Don't skimp on filtration - higher MERV is worth the cost
  • Include VFDs from day one - simple payback typically 2-4 years
  • Consider energy recovery for systems with >40% outdoor air

Installation

  • Level unit properly - critical for drain pan drainage
  • Install vibration isolation under entire unit
  • Use flexible duct connections at supply and return
  • Pipe coils for counter-flow operation (coolest water to coldest air)
  • Install balancing valves on all coil circuits
  • Verify condensate drain trap is properly sized and primed
  • Commission controls thoroughly - economizers often don't work as designed

Operation & Maintenance

  • Filter Replacement: Monitor differential pressure, replace at design pressure drop
  • Coil Cleaning: Clean annually or when airflow drops significantly
  • Belt Inspection: Check tension quarterly (belt-drive fans)
  • Bearing Lubrication: Per manufacturer schedule, typically semi-annually
  • Damper Operation: Verify full open and closed positions quarterly
  • Economizer Testing: Test integrated economizer operation seasonally
  • Condensate Drain: Clean and flush annually to prevent clogs

Design Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO's - Critical for Success

  1. DO design for face velocity limits on coils and filters - Exceeding face velocity limits on coils (typically 450-550 FPM for chilled water coils, 600-700 FPM for hot water coils) causes water carryover, noise, and reduced heat transfer effectiveness. Filters must stay below 300-500 FPM depending on type or efficiency drops and pressure increases dramatically. Calculate actual coil and filter face areas based on CFM and maximum velocity. Overspeeding coils by 20% to save first cost is false economy - performance suffers, water droplets enter ductwork causing damage and IAQ issues.
  2. DO properly select and sequence heating and cooling coils - In mixed air AHUs serving variable loads, coil selection and control sequence are critical. Cooling coils should be sized for entering air at mixed air temperature (not return air temperature) - this accounts for hot outdoor air on design day. Use face-and-bypass dampers or control valves with wide authority (20:1+ turndown). Implement proper warm-up and cool-down sequences to prevent simultaneous heating and cooling. Include morning warm-up with 100% return air before introducing outdoor air. Poor sequencing wastes 10-30% of HVAC energy fighting itself.
  3. DO design AHU mechanical rooms with full service access from day one - AHUs require substantial service clearance: minimum 4-6 feet in front of coil sections for tube removal, walk-in filter sections need 5-8 feet of access, fan sections need bearing and belt access. Many installations wedge AHUs into undersized mechanical rooms, then discover coils cannot be removed for cleaning or replacement without cutting piping. During design, model the AHU with dimensions from manufacturer's submittal (not just block dimensions), verify coil pullout space, ensure doorways are wide enough (coils are 6-8' wide), and provide permanent lighting and floor drains.

✗ DON'Ts - Avoid These Common Mistakes

  1. DON'T underestimate duct connection static pressure losses - The transition from AHU discharge (often 5-6 feet square) to supply duct system causes substantial pressure losses if poorly designed. Abrupt transitions, sharp turns immediately after discharge, and undersized ductwork create turbulence and pressure loss that reduces airflow. Design gradual transitions (no more than 15° expansion angle), provide at least 3-5 duct diameters of straight duct before first elbow, and use turning vanes in all elbows near AHU. Budget 0.15-0.30" w.c. for properly designed duct connections; poorly designed connections can consume 0.5-1.0" w.c., reducing available system static.
  2. DON'T forget about condensate disposal and drain pan geometry - AHU drain pans must be properly sloped (minimum 1/8" per foot, preferably 1/4" per foot) to a single drain point. Standing water in drain pans breeds bacteria, creates odors, corrodes the pan, and can overflow into ductwork. Trap the condensate line properly (minimum P-trap depth = negative pressure in AHU + 2") or the AHU will draw air through the drain, reducing airflow and causing whistling. Provide cleanouts and ensure drain piping is sized adequately (minimum 1.5" for units up to 20,000 CFM). Include drain pan overflow switches to shut down unit before water overflows into occupied space.
  3. DON'T mix indoor and outdoor air without proper controls - Mixing cold outdoor air with warm return air can cause stratification (layers of different temperatures) if done poorly, resulting in cold coils with warm air bypassing above, or frozen coil detection sensors that shut down the system unnecessarily. Use mixing boxes or airfoil mixing dampers (not opposed-blade dampers) to thoroughly mix airstreams. Locate mixing zone at least 3-5 feet upstream of coils. Install mixed air temperature sensor downstream of mixing point, not in the outdoor or return airstreams. In cold climates, consider preheat coils in outdoor air stream before mixing to prevent freeze-ups.

Filter Selection Trade-offs

Filter Efficiency vs. Pressure Drop & Cost

✓ Standard Filters (MERV 8-11):

  • Lower pressure drop (0.3-0.6" w.c. clean, 1.0" final)
  • Lower first cost ($10-30 per filter)
  • Adequate for general office, retail applications
  • Longer filter life (3-6 months typical)
  • Less fan energy required

✓ High-Efficiency Filters (MERV 13-14):

  • Higher pressure drop (0.6-1.2" w.c. clean, 2.0" final)
  • Higher cost ($30-80 per filter)
  • Required for hospitals, labs, high-IAQ applications
  • Shorter filter life (1-3 months typical)
  • Requires higher fan power (20-30% more energy)
  • Better indoor air quality (captures PM2.5, virus particles)

Recommendation: Match filter efficiency to actual IAQ requirements, not higher. Select AHU fan for final filter pressure drop (not clean), and include differential pressure gauges to indicate when to change filters. Over-filtering wastes energy; under-filtering compromises IAQ.

Fan Selection Recommendation

Modern best practice: Use direct-drive plenum fans with VFDs for most commercial applications. The premium for direct-drive (~10-15%) and VFD (~15-20%) pays back in 3-5 years through reduced energy and maintenance. The combination provides:

  • Maximum efficiency (no belt losses + fan speed optimization)
  • Minimal maintenance (no belts to inspect or replace)
  • Precise control for VAV or demand ventilation systems
  • Quiet operation (lower speeds during part-load)

Exception: Use belt-drive for very large systems (>50,000 CFM), high-static applications, or where field adjustment flexibility is essential.

Often Overlooked Considerations

Physical Dimensions and Access

AHUs are large and require substantial space:

  • 15,000 CFM unit: ~20' L × 7' W × 8' H
  • 50,000 CFM unit: ~40' L × 10' W × 12' H
  • Plan mechanical room for unit + 5-10 feet for coil removal
  • Ensure doorways and corridors allow rigging path
  • Provide permanent access for filter changes (many units have walk-in sections)

Static Pressure Budget

Account for all pressure drops in system design:

  • Filters: 0.3-1.0" w.c. (increases as filters load)
  • Coils: 0.2-0.8" w.c. each
  • Dampers: 0.1-0.3" w.c.
  • Internal AHU losses: 0.3-0.5" w.c.
  • Ductwork + terminals: Varies, commonly 1.5-3.0" w.c.
  • Total: 3-6" w.c. typical for VAV systems

Condensate Disposal

  • Cooling coils produce condensate - plan for drainage
  • Trap must be deep enough to overcome unit pressure (1" trap per 1" w.c. negative pressure)
  • Provide condensate pump if gravity drainage isn't possible
  • Include overflow switch to prevent damage from clogged drains
  • Route to sanitary drain, not storm (many jurisdictions)

Energy Costs

Fan energy is often underestimated:

  • 15,000 CFM at 3" static = ~22 HP = 16 kW continuous
  • 6,000 hours/year operation × $0.12/kWh = $11,500/year
  • VFD reduces average to 40-50% speed = 10-15% of full power = $1,000-1,500/year savings
  • Simple payback on VFD typically 2-3 years
