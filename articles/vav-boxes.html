Overview

Variable Air Volume (VAV) boxes, also called VAV terminal units or VAV dampers, are zone-level air distribution devices that regulate airflow to maintain comfortable conditions in individual spaces. They are the workhorses of modern commercial HVAC systems, providing independent temperature control for each zone while allowing the central air handling unit to operate more efficiently by reducing airflow during part-load conditions.

A typical VAV system might have dozens or even hundreds of VAV boxes, each serving a single zone (conference room, private office, open office area, etc.). Together, they create a flexible, energy-efficient HVAC system that adapts to changing loads throughout the building.

How It Works

VAV boxes receive constant-temperature supply air (typically 55°F) from the central AHU and modulate the airflow to each zone based on cooling demand:

Basic VAV Operation

  1. Cooling Mode: Zone thermostat calls for cooling → VAV box damper opens → More cold air flows into space → Space cools down
  2. Satisfied: Zone reaches setpoint → Damper modulates to minimum position → Minimum airflow maintains ventilation
  3. Heating Mode (with Reheat): Zone calls for heat → Damper goes to minimum → Reheat coil activates → Warm air delivered to space

System Interaction

VAV boxes work in concert with the AHU:

  • As VAV boxes close, duct static pressure increases
  • Static pressure sensor signals AHU to slow supply fan (via VFD)
  • Fan energy reduces proportionally to cube of speed reduction
  • Result: Significant energy savings compared to constant volume systems (30-50% fan energy reduction typical)

Types of VAV Boxes

1. Cooling-Only (Single-Duct)

Most basic type - provides cooling only, no heating capability.

  • Use: Interior zones with consistent cooling loads, mild climates
  • Operation: Modulates from minimum (ventilation) to maximum (cooling) airflow
  • Cost: Lowest first cost
  • Typical Min/Max: 30% minimum, 100% maximum

2. VAV Reheat (Hot Water)

Includes hot water heating coil downstream of damper for heating mode.

  • Use: Perimeter zones, spaces requiring year-round temperature control
  • Operation: Damper to minimum, then reheat coil activates for heating
  • Cost: Mid-range, requires hot water piping to each box
  • Typical Coil: 1-2 rows, 180°F hot water

3. VAV Reheat (Electric)

Uses electric resistance heating elements instead of hot water coil.

  • Use: When hot water isn't available, small heating loads
  • Advantages: No piping required, simple installation, precise control
  • Disadvantages: Expensive to operate, high electrical demand
  • Typical Capacity: 1-20 kW

4. Series Fan-Powered VAV

Includes small fan that runs continuously, mixes return plenum air with primary air.

  • Use: Perimeter zones, long duct runs, better air distribution
  • Operation: Fan runs continuously; as primary damper closes, fan draws more return air
  • Advantages: Better air circulation, reduced primary air requirements
  • Disadvantages: Fan energy, noise, more maintenance

5. Parallel Fan-Powered VAV

Fan operates only during heating mode or when primary airflow is insufficient.

  • Use: When continuous fan operation isn't needed
  • Operation: Primary air during cooling; fan + reheat during heating
  • Advantages: Lower fan energy than series, maintains air circulation during heating
  • Disadvantages: More complex controls

6. Dual-Duct VAV

Two dampers mix hot and cold air streams to achieve setpoint.

  • Use: Existing dual-duct systems, spaces requiring immediate mode changes
  • Operation: Modulates hot and cold dampers to mix air to desired temperature
  • Disadvantages: Energy-intensive, rare in new construction

Control Types

Pressure Dependent (Mechanical)

Damper position directly controlled by thermostat - airflow varies with duct pressure.

  • Advantages: Simple, low cost, reliable
  • Disadvantages: Airflow not precisely controlled, affected by system pressure changes
  • Use: Small systems, low-budget projects, simple applications

Pressure Independent (DDC)

Measures actual airflow and modulates damper to maintain setpoint regardless of pressure.

  • Advantages: Precise airflow control, better comfort, easier balancing
  • Disadvantages: Higher cost, requires airflow sensor and controller
  • Use: Most modern commercial buildings, critical applications
  • Note: Now standard on most projects

BMS Integration

Modern VAV boxes communicate with Building Management Systems:

  • Protocols: BACnet, Modbus, LonWorks
  • Points Monitored: Airflow, temperature, damper position, reheat output
  • Benefits: Remote monitoring, trend logging, fault detection, optimization

Applications

  • Office Buildings: Individual offices, conference rooms, open areas - ideal application
  • Schools: Classrooms, libraries, administrative areas
  • Hospitals: Patient rooms, offices, administrative (not critical care areas)
  • Retail: Individual shops in mall, different departments in department store
  • Hotels: Meeting rooms, back-of-house areas (guest rooms typically use FCUs or PTACs)
  • Laboratories: Office areas, non-critical lab spaces

Sizing Requirements

Information Needed for Proper Sizing

Parameter Typical Values/Notes
Zone Cooling Load BTU/hr or CFM from load calculation
Zone Heating Load BTU/hr (if reheat equipped)
Minimum Airflow CFM for ventilation per ASHRAE 62.1
Maximum Airflow CFM for peak cooling (typically 1 CFM per SF for offices)
Supply Air Temperature Typically 55°F from AHU
Inlet Static Pressure Available pressure at box inlet (0.5-2.0" w.c. typical)
Sound Requirements NC rating for space (NC 35-40 typical offices)
Duct Size Round or rectangular dimensions

Sizing Guidelines

  • Maximum CFM: Size for calculated cooling load, don't just use area × 1 CFM/SF
  • Minimum CFM: Greater of:
    • Ventilation requirement per ASHRAE 62.1
    • 30% of maximum for air circulation
    • Minimum needed to maintain duct velocity (prevent stratification)
  • Turndown Ratio: Typical 3:1 to 5:1 (300-500 CFM max = 100 CFM min)
  • Sizing Tip: Don't oversize! Oversized boxes have poor control at low loads

Example VAV Box Selection

Private office, 150 SF:

  • Cooling load: 1,500 BTU/hr
  • Max CFM: 1,500 BTU/hr ÷ (1.08 × 20°F ΔT) = 69 CFM
  • Ventilation requirement: 150 SF × 0.06 CFM/SF = 9 CFM (very low for office)
  • Min CFM: 69 × 0.30 = 21 CFM (use 30% rule)
  • Select: 70 CFM max VAV with HW reheat, 21 CFM minimum
  • Reheat coil: 150 SF × 35 BTU/hr/SF = 5,250 BTU/hr = select 1-row HW coil @ 180°F

Available Options

Damper Options

  • Single-Blade: Standard, economical
  • Opposed-Blade: Better control at partial loads, more expensive
  • Round vs. Rectangular: Round more common, rectangular for tight ceiling spaces

Control Options

  • Airflow Sensor Type: Hot wire, differential pressure, averaging pitot
  • Actuator: Electric (24V, 120V) or pneumatic (increasingly rare)
  • Controller Features: Occupancy override, CO2-based DCV, scheduling

Reheat Options

  • Hot Water Coil: 1-2 rows, various capacities
  • Electric Heat: Staged (multiple elements) or SCR modulating
  • No Reheat: Cooling-only applications

Sound Attenuation

  • Acoustic Liner: Reduces generated noise by 5-10 NC
  • Flex Duct: 3-6 feet of flex from box to diffuser helps reduce noise transmission
  • Discharge Silencer: For very noise-sensitive spaces

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Size VAV boxes based on actual load calculations, not rules of thumb
  • Specify pressure-independent controls for all but the simplest systems
  • Locate boxes in accessible ceiling spaces - NOT above inaccessible areas
  • Avoid locating boxes directly above occupied workstations (noise)
  • Specify acoustic liner for boxes serving noise-sensitive spaces (conference rooms, private offices)
  • Don't forget to size reheat for actual heating load, not just "throw in a coil"
  • Plan for ceiling access panels at each VAV box for service

Installation

  • Install box level and support from structure (not ductwork)
  • Follow manufacturer's straight duct requirements (typically 3-5 diameters upstream)
  • Use flex duct connections to prevent vibration transmission
  • Wire controls before installing ceiling - easier access
  • Label each box clearly with zone number/name
  • Commission each box individually - verify min/max flows

Commissioning

  • Calibrate airflow sensors per manufacturer instructions
  • Verify minimum and maximum airflow setpoints
  • Test full heating and cooling sequences
  • Verify static pressure control with VAV boxes modulating
  • Check sound levels in occupied spaces
  • Train operators on system operation and troubleshooting

Maintenance

  • Quarterly: Verify operation, check for stuck dampers
  • Annually: Clean airflow sensors, calibrate if needed
  • Every 2-3 Years: Replace filters on fan-powered boxes
  • As Needed: Lubricate damper bearings (older units)

Common Problems and Solutions

Zone Too Cold

  • Possible Causes: Minimum airflow set too high, damper stuck open, supply air too cold, reheat not working
  • Check: Actual airflow vs. setpoint, damper position, reheat output
  • Solution: Adjust minimum flow, repair damper, check reheat valve/element

Zone Too Warm

  • Possible Causes: Damper stuck closed, maximum flow too low, airflow sensor miscalibrated, supply air not cold enough
  • Check: Damper position at full demand, actual vs. displayed airflow
  • Solution: Free stuck damper, recalibrate sensor, increase max flow if undersized

Poor Air Circulation

  • Possible Causes: Minimum flow too low, diffusers poorly located, return air short-circuiting
  • Check: Airflow at minimum, air pattern in space
  • Solution: Increase minimum flow (within code limits), relocate diffusers, adjust return grilles

Noisy Operation

  • Possible Causes: Excessive inlet pressure, damper flutter, fan noise (fan-powered boxes)
  • Check: Inlet static pressure, damper position cycling
  • Solution: Add acoustic liner, increase minimum position to prevent flutter, balance system static

Often Overlooked Considerations

Access for Service

  • VAV boxes require periodic service (filter changes on FP boxes, sensor calibration)
  • Provide ceiling access panels within 10 feet of each box
  • Don't install above hard ceilings or in inaccessible soffits
  • Consider catwalks or walkable ceiling grid for large installations

Minimum Flow and Ventilation

  • Energy codes and ASHRAE 62.1 have strict requirements
  • Can't just set minimum to 0% - need adequate ventilation air
  • Some boxes may run at minimum most of the time (interior zones)
  • Consider DOAS for high-ventilation applications to reduce reheat energy

Acoustics

  • VAV boxes generate noise from damper and fan (FP boxes)
  • Noise increases at high inlet pressures and partially-closed dampers
  • Specify acoustic liner for noise-sensitive areas (private offices, conference rooms)
  • Use flex duct from box to diffuser to attenuate breakout noise

Typical Pricing (2024)

  • Cooling-Only (pressure-independent): $400-800
  • HW Reheat: $600-1,200
  • Electric Reheat: $700-1,400
  • Series Fan-Powered with HW Reheat: $1,200-2,000
  • Parallel Fan-Powered with HW Reheat: $1,100-1,800
  • Dual-Duct: $900-1,600

Prices vary significantly with size and options. Installation labor typically equals equipment cost.

Energy Impact

VAV systems save significant energy compared to constant volume:

  • Fan energy reduction: 30-50% (due to reduced airflow and static pressure)
  • Cooling energy reduction: 10-20% (less overcooling of zones)
  • However: Simultaneous heating and cooling (reheat) wastes energy
  • Solution: Reset supply air temperature up as loads decrease to minimize reheat
