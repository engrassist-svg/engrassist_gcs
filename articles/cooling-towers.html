Overview

Cooling towers are heat rejection devices that transfer waste heat from water-cooled systems to the atmosphere through evaporative cooling. They are essential components of central plant systems, working in conjunction with water-cooled chillers to provide efficient cooling for commercial and industrial buildings.

By using evaporation as the primary heat transfer mechanism, cooling towers can reject heat at temperatures below ambient dry-bulb temperature, down to the wet-bulb temperature. This makes them significantly more efficient than air-cooled systems, especially in hot, dry climates.

How It Works

Cooling towers operate on the principle of evaporative cooling. Hot condenser water from the chiller (typically 95°F) is distributed over the fill media inside the tower. As the water cascades down through the fill, air is drawn or blown through the tower, causing a small portion (approximately 1-2%) of the water to evaporate.

The Evaporative Cooling Process

Evaporation requires energy (approximately 1,000 BTU per pound of water evaporated). This energy comes from the remaining water, which cools down. The cooled water (typically 85°F) is then returned to the chiller condenser to continue the refrigeration cycle.

Key Principle: The lowest water temperature achievable is the ambient wet-bulb temperature. The difference between the leaving water temperature and wet-bulb temperature is called the "approach."

Water Balance

Understanding water consumption is critical:

  • Evaporation: ~1-2% of circulation rate (3 GPM per 100 tons)
  • Blowdown: Water discharged to maintain water quality (varies with cycles of concentration)
  • Drift: Tiny water droplets carried out with exhaust air (~0.001-0.02% with modern drift eliminators)
  • Makeup Water: Fresh water added to replace evaporation, blowdown, and drift

Types of Cooling Towers

1. Induced Draft Counterflow

Fan located at the top of the tower draws air upward through the fill while water flows downward. This is the most common type for commercial HVAC applications.

  • Advantages: High efficiency, low recirculation potential, smaller footprint
  • Disadvantages: Higher static pressure (more fan power), difficult to service fan at roof height
  • Typical Efficiency: 7-9°F approach to wet-bulb

2. Induced Draft Crossflow

Fan at top draws air horizontally through the fill while water flows downward via gravity distribution basins.

  • Advantages: Easy access for maintenance, lower static pressure (less fan power), quieter operation
  • Disadvantages: Larger footprint, slightly lower efficiency, more complex distribution system
  • Typical Efficiency: 8-10°F approach to wet-bulb

3. Forced Draft

Fan located at the bottom or side forces air into the tower. Less common in modern applications.

  • Advantages: Easy fan service, high exit velocity reduces recirculation
  • Disadvantages: Higher potential for recirculation in winds, increased pump head (elevated water inlet)

4. Closed Circuit (Fluid Coolers)

Water to be cooled flows through coils while spray water evaporates on the outside of the coils. The process water never contacts the evaporative spray.

  • Advantages: No contamination of process water, can handle glycol solutions, lower maintenance
  • Disadvantages: Higher initial cost, larger footprint, slightly less efficient
  • Applications: Data centers, medical facilities, facilities with high water quality requirements

Applications

  • Central Chilled Water Plants: Heat rejection for water-cooled chillers (most common)
  • Industrial Process Cooling: Manufacturing, power generation, chemical processing
  • HVAC Condensing Water: Large commercial buildings, hospitals, campuses
  • Data Centers: High-density heat rejection with closed-circuit towers
  • Refrigeration Plants: Food processing, cold storage facilities

Typical Configurations

Single Tower vs. Multiple Cells

Single Cell: Used for smaller loads (100-400 tons). Single fan, simple controls, but no redundancy.

Multiple Cells: Typical for larger systems. Benefits include:

  • Redundancy - operation continues if one cell fails
  • Capacity control through staging
  • Phased installation capability
  • More efficient part-load operation

Series vs. Parallel Piping

Parallel (Most Common): Condenser water split between cells. Equal flow through each operating cell. Simpler piping and control.

Series: Water flows through multiple cells sequentially. Can achieve colder leaving water but requires larger pumps and more complex controls.

Basin Location

  • Factory-Assembled: Complete unit shipped to site (up to ~500 tons)
  • Field-Erected: Components assembled on-site for large capacities (500+ tons)
  • Concrete Basin: Field-poured basin for very large installations, better freeze protection

Sizing Calculations

Basic Sizing Formula:

Tower Capacity (BTU/hr) = GPM × 500 × Range

Where: Range = EWT - LWT (typically 10°F)

Example Sizing Calculation

For a 500-ton chiller system:

  • Chiller rejects: 500 tons × 15,000 BTU/ton = 7,500,000 BTU/hr
  • Tower heat rejection: 7,500,000 × 1.25 = 9,375,000 BTU/hr (625 tons)
  • Flow rate: 625 tons × 3 GPM/ton = 1,875 GPM
  • Check: 1,875 GPM × 500 × 10°F = 9,375,000 BTU/hr ✓

Approach vs. Tower Cost

Smaller approach (colder water) requires larger, more expensive tower:

  • 10°F approach: Baseline cost
  • 7°F approach: ~1.4× larger tower
  • 5°F approach: ~1.8× larger tower

However, colder condenser water improves chiller efficiency, creating an economic optimization opportunity.

Available Options

Fan Options

  • Variable Speed Drives (VFDs): Most important efficiency upgrade. Can reduce fan energy by 50-80% at part load
  • Low-Noise Fans: Larger, slower fans for noise-sensitive locations
  • Belt Drive vs. Direct Drive: Belt allows speed adjustment but requires maintenance; direct drive is more efficient

Fill Media

  • Film Fill: High efficiency, but susceptible to fouling in dirty water applications
  • Splash Fill: More forgiving of poor water quality, easier to clean, slightly lower efficiency

Materials and Coatings

  • Galvanized Steel: Standard for most applications
  • Stainless Steel: Coastal or corrosive environments
  • Fiberglass: Best corrosion resistance, higher initial cost

Controls and Monitoring

  • Water Level Controls: Automatic makeup water valve and overflow
  • Vibration Sensors: Early warning of fan imbalance or bearing failure
  • Water Quality Monitors: Conductivity controllers for automated blowdown
  • BMS Integration: Remote monitoring and optimization

Best Practices

Design Phase

  • Use ASHRAE design wet-bulb (1% or 2%), not extreme conditions
  • Consider noise - cooling towers can be loud. Review local ordinances
  • Plan for makeup water connection and drain to accommodate blowdown
  • Ensure structural support for operating weight (water + structure)
  • Provide adequate clearance for airflow - minimum 1.5× tower height horizontally
  • Consider wind direction - place towers to minimize recirculation
  • Include VFDs from day one - ROI is typically 2-3 years

Installation

  • Level the tower carefully - improper leveling causes uneven water distribution
  • Install flexible pipe connections to prevent vibration transmission
  • Provide screen or strainer upstream of tower to protect nozzles
  • Install blowdown valve sized for proper water treatment
  • Winterize piping if tower operates year-round in freezing climates

Operation & Maintenance

  • Water Treatment: Most critical aspect. Test water weekly minimum
  • Keep It Clean: Remove debris from basin monthly during cooling season
  • Fill Inspection: Check for scaling, biological growth, or damage annually
  • Fan Bearings: Grease or oil per manufacturer schedule (typically quarterly)
  • Drift Eliminators: Inspect and clean to maintain efficiency and minimize water loss
  • Nozzles: Clean or replace clogged spray nozzles
  • Winterization: Drain completely if shut down in freezing weather, or maintain flow and heat trace

Often Overlooked Considerations

Physical Dimensions and Weight

Cooling towers are large and heavy when filled with water:

  • 500-ton tower: ~25' L × 12' W × 15' H, 30,000-50,000 lbs operating weight
  • Verify structural capacity - especially for rooftop installations
  • Plan rigging path early - many towers must be assembled on-site
  • Consider future replacement - can a crane access the roof?

Noise Levels

Cooling towers generate significant noise from fans and falling water:

  • Typical Sound Level: 65-80 dBA at 50 feet
  • Check local noise ordinances - many limit noise at property line
  • Options: Low-noise fans, sound attenuators, acoustic barriers
  • Consider neighbors and outdoor spaces when locating towers

Water Consumption

Evaporative cooling uses substantial amounts of water:

  • Evaporation: ~15-20 gallons per hour per 100 tons
  • Blowdown: Additional 5-15 gallons per hour per 100 tons (depends on cycles of concentration)
  • Annual consumption (500-ton system, 2000 hrs): 200,000-350,000 gallons
  • Cost: At $5/1000 gallons, this is $1,000-1,750 per year
  • May require separate water meter for sewer charge reduction

Water Treatment Costs

Often underbudgeted but critical for equipment life:

  • Initial Treatment System: $3,000-10,000
  • Annual Chemical Costs: $2,000-5,000 for a 500-ton system
  • Water Testing: $500-1,500 per year
  • Consequence of Poor Treatment: Reduced efficiency, corrosion, scale buildup, Legionella risk, equipment failure

Legionella Concerns

Cooling towers are a potential source of Legionella bacteria:

  • Required to have Legionella management plan in most jurisdictions
  • Regular water testing and treatment program is critical
  • Locate away from building air intakes (minimum 25 feet recommended)
  • Consider drift eliminators rated for 0.001% or better

Typical Pricing (2024)

  • Small Factory-Assembled (100-200 tons): $20,000-40,000
  • Medium Factory-Assembled (300-500 tons): $40,000-80,000
  • Large Field-Erected (1000+ tons): $150,000-300,000+
  • Closed-Circuit Towers: 1.5-2× cost of open towers

Installation typically adds 30-50% to equipment cost. Annual maintenance costs run 2-5% of capital cost.
